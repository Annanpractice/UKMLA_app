Blood Disorders

17 conditions
Abnormal blood film
  • Ix: repeat FBC + blood film; reticulocytes, haematinics, LFT, U&E, LDH, bilirubin, haptoglobin, DAT if haemolysis suspected.
  • Tx: depends on film; treat emergency patterns first: blasts, schistocytes, malaria parasites, sickled cells, severe pancytopenia.
  • Escalate: same-day haematology if blasts, Auer rods, schistocytes/TTP picture, severe cytopenias, hyperviscosity, suspected acute leukaemia.
  • Mimics: lab artefact, EDTA clumping, reactive lymphocytosis, infection/inflammation, drugs, alcohol/liver disease.
  • Red flags: blasts, schistocytes + thrombocytopenia/AKI/neuro signs, DIC pattern, Hb crash, platelet <50, neutrophils <0.5.
Anaemia
  • Ix: FBC indices, film, reticulocytes, ferritin/iron studies, B12/folate, CRP, U&E, LFT, TFT; FIT/endoscopy if IDA cancer concern.
  • Tx: cause-specific; IDA ferrous sulfate 200 mg OD, folic acid 5 mg OD, hydroxocobalamin 1 mg IM; transfuse if unstable/severe symptomatic.
  • Escalate: admission if haemodynamic compromise, active bleeding, chest pain, syncope, Hb very low/symptomatic, haemolysis, pancytopenia.
  • Mimics: dilutional anaemia, thalassaemia trait, anaemia of chronic disease, CKD, malignancy, haemolysis, marrow failure.
  • Red flags: IDA in adult men/postmenopausal women, weight loss, melaena, dysphagia, jaundice, dark urine, neuro signs with B12 deficiency.
Coagulation disorders
  • Ix: FBC, PT/INR, APTT, fibrinogen, LFT, U&E; mixing studies, factor assays, VWF antigen/activity, lupus anticoagulant/thrombophilia screen if indicated.
  • Tx: major bleed: ABC, stop anticoagulants, local haemostasis, TXA 1 g IV, factor replacement/PCC/vit K as cause-specific; local policy applies.
  • Escalate: same-day haematology for unexplained bleeding, prolonged APTT/PT, suspected haemophilia/VWD major bleed, recurrent VTE, thrombosis age <40– 50.
  • Mimics: liver disease, DIC, sepsis, DOAC/warfarin/heparin effect, thrombocytopenia, platelet dysfunction, vitamin K deficiency.
  • Red flags: intracranial/GI bleeding, compartment bleed, haemarthrosis, postpartum/surgical bleeding, thrombosis + bleeding, purpura fulminans.
Disseminated intravascular coagulation
  • Ix: FBC, PT/APTT, fibrinogen, D-dimer, film for schistocytes, U&E/LFT, lactate; find trigger: sepsis, trauma, malignancy, obstetric cause.
  • Tx: treat trigger urgently; if bleeding/procedure: platelets, FFP, cryoprecipitate/fibrinogen; VTE prophylaxis if non-bleeding unless contraindicated.
  • Escalate: admit/ICU if bleeding, shock, sepsis, organ failure, rapidly falling platelets/fibrinogen, purpura fulminans.
  • Mimics: TTP/HUS, severe liver disease, massive transfusion, vitamin K deficiency, anticoagulant excess, primary fibrinolysis.
  • Red flags: bleeding + thrombosis, hypotension, AKI, neuro signs, skin necrosis, fibrinogen <1 g/L, platelets falling fast.
Haemoglobinopathies
  • Ix: FBC/film, retics, haematinics, haemoglobin electrophoresis/HPLC; genetic testing/counselling if carrier state, family planning or unclear phenotype.
  • Tx: trait usually reassurance; disease-specific specialist care; avoid empiric iron unless deficient, because naturally humanity tried that.
  • Escalate: haematology for symptomatic disease, severe anaemia, haemolysis, pregnancy/planning pregnancy, abnormal electrophoresis, suspected sickle/thalassaemia major.
  • Mimics: IDA, anaemia of chronic disease, sideroblastic anaemia, lead toxicity, hereditary spherocytosis, G6PD deficiency.
  • Red flags: severe anaemia, jaundice, splenomegaly, growth delay, crises, acute chest symptoms, stroke symptoms, family/partner carrier risk.
Hyposplenism/splenectomy
  • Ix: confirm risk: history/surgery, Howell–Jolly bodies; document vaccine status, infection plan, antibiotic allergy, travel/malaria risk.
  • Tx: pneumococcal, MenACWY/MenB, Hib, influenza vaccines; antibiotic prophylaxis often penicillin V 250 mg BD; local policy varies.
  • Escalate: same-day assessment/admission for fever or systemic illness; give prompt broad-spectrum antibiotics per local sepsis policy.
  • Mimics: functional hyposplenism in coeliac disease, sickle cell, IBD, SLE, lymphoma, severe liver disease.
  • Red flags: fever, rigors, vomiting, confusion, hypotension, animal bites, travel infection, meningism; overwhelming sepsis can progress obscenely fast.
Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura
  • Ix: isolated thrombocytopenia on FBC/film; exclude pseudothrombocytopenia, HIV/HCV, drugs, alcohol, liver disease, pregnancy, marrow disease.
  • Tx: no bleed/mild often observe; if treatment needed: corticosteroid first-line; major bleed: IVIg 1 g/kg + IV steroid ± platelet transfusion.
  • Escalate: same-day haematology/admit if active bleeding, platelets <20–30, new diagnosis with severe thrombocytopenia, pregnancy, neuro symptoms.
  • Mimics: TTP/HUS, DIC, leukaemia, aplastic anaemia, drug-induced thrombocytopenia, hypersplenism, HIT.
  • Red flags: mucosal bleeding, haematuria/GI bleed, headache/neuro signs, schistocytes, anaemia, renal impairment, fever.
Leukaemia
  • Ix: urgent FBC + film, coagulation, U&E, LFT, urate, LDH; diagnosis by marrow aspirate/trephine, flow cytometry, cytogenetics/molecular tests.
  • Tx: emergency supportive care: treat sepsis/bleeding, transfusion support, allopurinol/rasburicase if TLS risk; definitive chemo via haematology.
  • Escalate: same-day haematology/admission if blasts, suspected acute leukaemia, Auer rods/APL, hyperleucocytosis, DIC, neutropenic sepsis.
  • Mimics: viral infection, reactive lymphocytosis, lymphoma spillover, MDS, aplastic anaemia, severe B12/folate deficiency.
  • Red flags: bruising/bleeding, infections, fever, bone pain, lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, pancytopenia, WCC very high, DIC in APL.
Lymphoma
  • Ix: FBC, film, ESR/CRP, LDH, U&E/LFT, HIV; CXR/CT; diagnosis by excision lymph node biopsy, not casual needle-stabbing if avoidable.
  • Tx: specialist chemo/immunotherapy/radiotherapy; manage emergencies: steroids only after biopsy unless airway/SVCO/cord compression emergency.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway for unexplained lymphadenopathy; sameday if SVCO, airway compromise, cord compression, TLS, sepsis.
  • Mimics: EBV/CMV/HIV/TB, reactive nodes, metastatic cancer, sarcoid, autoimmune disease, dental/ENT infection.
  • Red flags: B symptoms, hard fixed nodes, supraclavicular nodes, splenomegaly, alcohol-induced node pain, pruritus, breathlessness/SVCO.
Multiple myeloma
  • Ix: FBC, U&E/eGFR, corrected Ca, ESR/PV, LFT, immunoglobulins, serum electrophoresis + free light chains, urine Bence-Jones; whole-body MRI/CT, marrow biopsy.
  • Tx: urgent fluids for hypercalcaemia/AKI, analgesia, infection treatment, bisphosphonate after renal review; definitive therapy via haematology.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway/urgent haematology; same-day if spinal cord compression, AKI, hypercalcaemia, sepsis, severe pain/fracture.
  • Mimics: MGUS, metastatic bone disease, lymphoma, CKD anaemia, osteoporosis, primary hyperparathyroidism.
  • Red flags: CRAB: hyperCalcaemia, Renal failure, Anaemia, Bone pain/lesions; recurrent infections, weight loss, raised total protein, cord compression.
Myeloproliferative disorders
  • Ix: FBC trend, film, U&E/LFT, urate, LDH, ferritin, EPO if erythrocytosis; JAK2/CALR/MPL, BCR-ABL; marrow biopsy specialist-led.
  • Tx: haematology-led; aspirin/venesection in PV, cytoreduction e.g. hydroxycarbamide if high risk; manage thrombosis/bleeding urgently.
  • Escalate: urgent haematology if thrombosis/bleeding, platelets >1000, Hct very high, WCC very high, splenomegaly, constitutional symptoms.
  • Mimics: reactive thrombocytosis, secondary polycythaemia, CML, chronic inflammation, iron deficiency, smoking/OSA, dehydration.
  • Red flags: erythromelalgia, aquagenic pruritus, thrombosis, bleeding, splenomegaly, hyperviscosity, weight loss/night sweats, transformation to AML.
Neutropenia/neutropenic sepsis
  • Ix: FBC/diff, cultures before antibiotics if no delay, U&E/LFT/CRP/lactate, CXR if respiratory symptoms; neutropenic sepsis = fever/sepsis with neutrophils ≤0.5.
  • Tx: medical emergency: IV antibiotics within 1 hour; piperacillin-tazobactam commonly 4.5 g IV, but follow local antimicrobial policy.
  • Escalate: immediate admission; ICU if shock, hypoxia, lactate high, organ dysfunction; discuss haematology/oncology/microbiology.
  • Mimics: viral illness, drug-induced neutropenia, autoimmune neutropenia, B12/folate deficiency, marrow failure, hypersplenism.
  • Red flags: fever may be absent; hypotension, rigors, mucositis, line infection, abdominal/perianal pain; avoid PR exam because germs need no invitations.
Pancytopenia
  • Ix: repeat FBC/diff + film, retics, haematinics, LFT/U&E, LDH, bilirubin, viral screen, autoimmune screen if indicated; marrow biopsy if unexplained.
  • Tx: treat cause; transfusion support, infection precautions, stop marrow-toxic drugs, urgent antibiotics if febrile neutropenia.
  • Escalate: same-day haematology/admit if blasts, severe neutropenia, bleeding, Hb symptomatic/very low, platelets <20–30, suspected aplastic anaemia/leukaemia.
  • Mimics: B12/folate deficiency, alcohol/liver disease, hypersplenism, sepsis, drugs, viral infection, MDS, marrow infiltration.
  • Red flags: fever, mucosal bleeding, bruising, recurrent infections, weight loss, bone pain, splenomegaly, very low retics.
Platelet disorders
  • Ix: FBC repeat, citrate sample if clumping, film, coagulation, LFT/U&E/CRP/ferritin; platelet function testing/vWF screen if bleeding with normal count.
  • Tx: thrombocytopenia: treat cause, avoid NSAIDs/IM injections; thrombocytosis: treat reactive cause, correct iron deficiency if safe; antiplatelets specialist-led.
  • Escalate: same-day if platelets <50 with bleeding/procedure, <20, >1000, thrombosis/bleeding, schistocytes, suspected HIT/TTP/DIC.
  • Mimics: EDTA clumping, ITP, DIC, TTP/HUS, liver disease, drugs, infection/inflammation, iron deficiency, MPN.
  • Red flags: petechiae + fever, mucosal/GI/ICH bleed, neuro signs, AKI, thrombosis, rapidly falling platelets, recent heparin exposure.
Polycythaemia
  • Ix: repeat FBC/Hct, oxygen sats, smoking/CO exposure, ferritin, U&E/LFT, EPO, JAK2 V617F; consider OSA/COPD/renal tumour.
  • Tx: apparent/secondary: hydration, smoking cessation, treat hypoxia/OSA; PV specialist: venesection target Hct <0.45 + low-dose aspirin if suitable.
  • Escalate: haematology if persistent Hct >0.52 men or >0.48 women, JAK2 positive, low EPO, thrombosis, hyperviscosity.
  • Mimics: dehydration, diuretics, smoking, COPD/OSA, testosterone, renal disease/EPO tumours, high altitude.
  • Red flags: headache, visual symptoms, erythromelalgia, aquagenic pruritus, thrombosis, bleeding, splenomegaly, very high Hct.
Sickle cell disease
  • Ix: acute crisis: pain score, sats, FBC/retics, U&E/LFT, CRP, group & save, cultures if febrile; CXR if chest symptoms/hypoxia.
  • Tx: analgesia within 30 min; morphine titrated IV/SC commonly used, paracetamol ± NSAID if safe, fluids if dehydrated, oxygen only if hypoxic.
  • Escalate: admit if severe/uncontrolled pain, fever, hypoxia, chest pain, neuro signs, priapism, pregnancy, aplastic crisis, sequestration, AKI.
  • Mimics: sepsis, PE, pneumonia, ACS, osteomyelitis, surgical abdomen, renal colic, DVT.
  • Red flags: acute chest syndrome, stroke/TIA, priapism >2 h, splenic/hepatic sequestration, severe anaemia, saturation drop, fever.
Transfusion reactions
  • Ix: stop transfusion, check patient/unit ID, observations, clerical check; send FBC, U&E, LFT, coag, DAT, repeat group screen, cultures if fever/sepsis.
  • Tx: maintain IV access with saline; anaphylaxis: IM adrenaline 500 micrograms; TACO: sit up, oxygen, furosemide; haemolysis/sepsis: urgent senior help.
  • Escalate: call transfusion lab/haematology; ICU if anaphylaxis, shock, hypoxia, severe haemolysis, TRALI/TACO, sepsis.
  • Mimics: underlying sepsis, fluid overload, anxiety/vasovagal, febrile illness, drug allergy, pulmonary embolism.
  • Red flags: fever/rigors, back/flank pain, hypotension, dyspnoea, wheeze, urticaria/angioedema, haemoglobinuria, bleeding/DIC, symptoms within 24 h.

Dermatology

24 conditions
Acne vulgaris
  • Ix: clinical; grade severity; pregnancy/PCOS drug history; swab only if atypical, gram-negative folliculitis or treatment failure.
  • Tx: mild: adapalene/BPO OD; comedonal: topical retinoid; moderate–severe: topical combo + lymecycline 408 mg OD or doxycycline 100 mg OD.
  • Escalate: dermatology if nodulocystic/scarring, severe psychosocial distress, failed 2 courses, suspected acne fulminans; urgent if systemic illness.
  • Mimics: rosacea, perioral dermatitis, folliculitis, hidradenitis suppurativa, druginduced acne, PCOS.
  • Red flags: rapid severe painful nodules, scarring, fever/arthralgia, virilisation, sudden adult-onset acne.
Atopic dermatitis / eczema
  • Ix: clinical; assess severity, infection, triggers; swab only if recurrent/severe infected eczema; consider allergy referral if clear immediate trigger.
  • Tx: emollient 250–500 g/week; topical steroid to severity: hydrocortisone 1% mild, betamethasone valerate 0.1% potent, usually OD 7–14 days.
  • Escalate: same-day if eczema herpeticum; dermatology if severe/refractory, recurrent infection, diagnostic doubt, systemic therapy needed.
  • Mimics: contact dermatitis, psoriasis, scabies, tinea, seborrhoeic dermatitis, cutaneous lymphoma.
  • Red flags: painful punched-out vesicles, fever, eye involvement, rapidly spreading infection, erythroderma.
Benign skin lesions
  • Ix: clinical ± dermoscopy; cyst/lipoma usually clinical; biopsy/excision if diagnostic doubt or atypical features.
  • Tx: reassure; excise if symptomatic/infected/cosmetic; actinic keratosis: sun protection ± 5-FU/imiquimod/cryotherapy depending burden.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway if changing pigmented lesion, ulceration, bleeding, rapid growth, irregular colour/shape.
  • Mimics: melanoma, BCC, SCC, keratoacanthoma, dermatofibrosarcoma protuberans, sebaceous cyst abscess.
  • Red flags: ABCDE change, new lesion age >50, non-healing crusted/ulcerated lesion, immunosuppression, recurrent “cyst” after drainage.
Burns
  • Ix: ABCDE first; stop burning, cool with running water 20 min, assess TBSA/depth/site; check inhalation, tetanus, CO exposure.
  • Tx: analgesia, remove jewellery/clothing not stuck, cling film dressing, fluids if major burn; avoid ice/butter/folk-medicine nonsense.
  • Escalate: burns service for deep, circumferential, chemical/electrical, inhalation, face/hands/genitals/joints, >3% full thickness or >10% partial thickness.
  • Mimics: SJS/TEN, bullous pemphigoid, cellulitis, contact dermatitis, safeguarding/non-accidental injury.
  • Red flags: airway soot/hoarseness, circumferential limb/chest burn, reduced perfusion, chemical/electrical burn, sepsis, safeguarding concern.
Cellulitis
  • Ix: clinical; mark edge; FBC/CRP/U&E if systemic; blood cultures if septic; USS if abscess/DVT doubt; consider portal of entry.
  • Tx: flucloxacillin 500 mg QDS 5–7 days; penicillin allergy: clarithromycin 500 mg BD or doxycycline 100 mg BD then OD; local policy applies.
  • Escalate: admit if sepsis, rapidly spreading, immunocompromised, face/orbit/hand, lymphoedema severe, failed oral antibiotics.
  • Mimics: DVT, venous eczema, lipodermatosclerosis, gout, nec fasc, contact dermatitis, erythema nodosum.
  • Red flags: pain out of proportion, crepitus, bullae, skin necrosis, hypotension, confusion, severe neutropenia, orbital signs.
Contact dermatitis
  • Ix: clinical distribution; identify irritant/allergen; patch testing if persistent/recurrent allergic pattern.
  • Tx: avoid trigger; emollients; topical steroid matched to site/severity, usually OD 7–14 days; treat secondary infection only if clearly infected.
  • Escalate: dermatology/occupational health if occupational, severe hand dermatitis, refractory, facial/genital severe disease, diagnostic doubt.
  • Mimics: atopic eczema, tinea, scabies, psoriasis, rosacea, cellulitis.
  • Red flags: widespread blistering, facial/eye swelling, infection, occupational disability, systemic allergic symptoms.
Cutaneous fungal infection
  • Ix: clinical; skin scrapings if uncertain, recurrent, extensive, scalp/nail involvement, treatment failure.
  • Tx: tinea corporis/cruris: terbinafine 1% cream OD–BD 1–2 weeks or clotrimazole 1% BD 4 weeks; oral therapy if extensive/refractory.
  • Escalate: urgent/specialist if immunocompromised, scalp kerion, extensive infection, diagnostic doubt, failed oral therapy.
  • Mimics: eczema, psoriasis, pityriasis rosea, intertrigo, candidiasis, erythrasma.
  • Red flags: steroid-modified tinea, painful boggy scalp swelling, systemic symptoms, diabetes/immunosuppression, recurrent widespread disease.
Cutaneous warts
  • Ix: clinical; dermoscopy/biopsy if atypical, pigmented, ulcerated, immunosuppressed or diagnostic doubt.
  • Tx: often no treatment; salicylic acid daily up to 12 weeks; cryotherapy every 2–3 weeks if suitable.
  • Escalate: dermatology/podiatry if immunosuppressed, facial/genital lesions, extensive/recalcitrant, painful plantar lesions affecting function.
  • Mimics: corns/callus, SCC, molluscum, seborrhoeic keratosis, verrucous carcinoma.
  • Red flags: rapid growth, bleeding, ulceration, pigmentation, pain out of keeping, transplant/immunosuppression.
Erythema nodosum
  • Ix: clinical tender shin nodules; FBC/CRP/ESR, throat swab/ASO, CXR if sarcoid/TB, pregnancy test, stool/TB tests if indicated.
  • Tx: treat cause; rest, leg elevation, NSAID if safe e.g. naproxen 250–500 mg BD; compression if tolerated.
  • Escalate: urgent review if systemic illness, TB/sarcoid/IBD symptoms, pregnancy, persistent >6–8 weeks, ulceration or atypical distribution.
  • Mimics: cellulitis, vasculitis, superficial thrombophlebitis, panniculitis, nodular vasculitis, bruising.
  • Red flags: fever, weight loss, cough, haemoptysis, diarrhoea/bloody stool, arthropathy, unilateral necrotic/ulcerating lesions.
Folliculitis / hidradenitis suppurativa
  • Ix: folliculitis clinical ± swab if recurrent; HS clinical: recurrent painful nodules/abscesses in axilla/groin/inframammary areas with scarring/sinuses.
  • Tx: folliculitis: hygiene, antiseptic wash, topical fusidic acid if localised; HS: weight/smoking advice, topical clindamycin 1% BD 12 weeks.
  • Escalate: HS dermatology/surgery if scarring, sinus tracts, recurrent abscesses, failed primary care, severe pain; urgent if sepsis.
  • Mimics: acne, furuncles/carbuncles, pilonidal sinus, Crohn perianal disease, lymphadenitis, Bartholin abscess.
  • Red flags: fever, spreading cellulitis, immunosuppression, severe perianal disease, squamous change in chronic HS scar.
Haemangioma
  • Ix: clinical; USS/MRI if deep, rapidly enlarging, functional compromise or diagnostic doubt.
  • Tx: most observe; ulcerated/painful lesions need wound care; infantile lesions may need propranolol specialist-led, not casual GP jazz.
  • Escalate: urgent specialist if airway/eye/oral/genital, ulcerated, bleeding, large segmental, multiple lesions, high-output cardiac failure concern.
  • Mimics: vascular malformation, pyogenic granuloma, Kaposi sarcoma, melanoma, angiosarcoma.
  • Red flags: rapid growth in adult, bleeding/ulceration, visual obstruction, airway symptoms, multiple visceral haemangioma concern.
Impetigo
  • Ix: clinical; swab if recurrent, widespread, MRSA suspected, treatment failure, outbreak.
  • Tx: localised non-bullous: hydrogen peroxide 1% cream BD/TDS 5 days; fusidic acid 2% TDS 5 days if unsuitable; oral flucloxacillin if widespread.
  • Escalate: same-day if systemic illness, bullous extensive disease, cellulitis, immunocompromised, infants, outbreaks in care settings.
  • Mimics: HSV, eczema herpeticum, infected eczema, scabies, contact dermatitis, bullous pemphigoid.
  • Red flags: fever, rapidly spreading erythema, pain, bullae, periorbital involvement, renal symptoms after infection.
Lichen planus
  • Ix: clinical violaceous itchy polygonal papules; check mouth/genitals/scalp/nails; biopsy if atypical or erosive mucosal disease.
  • Tx: potent topical steroid e.g. betamethasone valerate 0.1% OD short course; oral disease: topical steroid preparations specialist/dental-led.
  • Escalate: dermatology/oral medicine if mucosal, erosive, genital, scalp/nail disease, severe symptoms, diagnostic doubt.
  • Mimics: psoriasis, eczema, drug eruption, lupus, lichen sclerosus, oral leukoplakia/candidiasis.
  • Red flags: painful erosions, dysphagia, genital scarring, alopecia, non-healing oral ulcer, SCC concern in chronic oral disease.
Lipoma
  • Ix: clinical soft mobile subcutaneous mass; USS if uncertain; MRI/urgent referral if deep, fixed, painful, enlarging or >5 cm.
  • Tx: reassurance; excision if painful, growing, functionally troublesome or diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Escalate: suspected sarcoma pathway if >5 cm, increasing size, deep to fascia, painful, recurrent after excision.
  • Mimics: epidermoid cyst, abscess, lymph node, hernia, neurofibroma, liposarcoma.
  • Red flags: rapid growth, firmness/fixation, deep location, neurovascular symptoms, systemic symptoms.
Malignant / pre-malignant skin lesions
  • Ix: full skin exam + dermoscopy; melanoma diagnosis by excision biopsy; AK/Bowen often clinical ± biopsy if uncertain.
  • Tx: AK: sun protection ± cryotherapy/5-FU/imiquimod; Bowen: cryotherapy/5- FU/excision; melanoma/BCC/SCC specialist excision or Mohs where indicated.
  • Escalate: 2WW for suspected melanoma/SCC; urgent BCC if size/site means delay harmful; routine referral often acceptable for low-risk BCC.
  • Mimics: seborrhoeic keratosis, dermatofibroma, pyogenic granuloma, wart, eczema, non-healing traumatic ulcer.
  • Red flags: ABCDE, 7-point score ≥3, nodular melanoma, ulcerated/crusted keratin lesion, bleeding, non-healing, immunosuppression.
Necrotising fasciitis
  • Ix: do not delay surgery for imaging; FBC, U&E, CRP, CK, lactate, blood cultures, VBG/ABG, coag; CT only if diagnosis uncertain and stable.
  • Tx: surgical emergency: immediate senior surgical review + broad-spectrum IV antibiotics per local policy + fluids/analgesia.
  • Escalate: theatre urgently for debridement; ICU for shock, organ failure, lactate high; urology for Fournier’s gangrene.
  • Mimics: cellulitis, abscess, DVT, compartment syndrome, pyomyositis, gas gangrene.
  • Red flags: pain out of proportion, rapid progression, skin necrosis, bullae, crepitus, anaesthesia, shock, confusion.
Pressure sores
  • Ix: assess grade, size/depth, exudate, infection, pain, nutrition, mobility, continence; Waterlow/Braden risk; probe/scan if osteomyelitis concern.
  • Tx: pressure offloading, repositioning, support surface, dressings, nutrition/hydration, analgesia; antibiotics only if cellulitis/sepsis/osteomyelitis.
  • Escalate: tissue viability/surgical review if grade 3–4, necrosis, spreading infection, exposed bone, suspected osteomyelitis, safeguarding concern.
  • Mimics: moisture lesion, venous ulcer, arterial ulcer, diabetic foot ulcer, vasculitis, malignancy.
  • Red flags: sepsis, rapidly spreading cellulitis, black necrosis, foul discharge, exposed bone, severe pain, new pressure damage in neglect context.
Psoriasis
  • Ix: clinical; assess severity/site, nails/joints, triggers, CV risk; skin biopsy only if uncertain.
  • Tx: trunk/limbs: potent topical steroid + vitamin D analogue OD for up to 4 weeks, then vitamin D analogue maintenance; emollients.
  • Escalate: dermatology if severe/extensive, face/genitals/hands, failed topical therapy, erythroderma/pustular psoriasis; rheumatology if psoriatic arthritis.
  • Mimics: eczema, tinea, seborrhoeic dermatitis, pityriasis rosea, lichen planus, cutaneous T-cell lymphoma.
  • Red flags: erythroderma, generalised pustules, fever, severe pain, new hot swollen joints, eye symptoms.
Rosacea
  • Ix: clinical; eye symptoms screen; consider lupus/photosensitivity if atypical; no comedones, because acne likes to confuse everyone.
  • Tx: trigger avoidance, SPF; papulopustular: metronidazole 0.75% BD or ivermectin 1% OD; moderate–severe: doxycycline 40 mg MR OD or 100 mg OD.
  • Escalate: ophthalmology same-day if painful red eye/visual change; dermatology if severe phymatous disease, refractory, diagnostic doubt.
  • Mimics: acne, SLE, seborrhoeic dermatitis, perioral dermatitis, contact dermatitis, steroid rosacea.
  • Red flags: ocular pain, photophobia, reduced vision, unilateral facial swelling, flushing with systemic symptoms, steroid-induced worsening.
Scabies
  • Ix: clinical itch worse at night, burrows, contacts affected; dermoscopy/scraping if uncertain; examine finger webs, wrists, genitals.
  • Tx: permethrin 5% cream whole body, repeat after 7 days; treat all household/sexual contacts same time; wash bedding/clothes ≥50°C.
  • Escalate: dermatology/infection control if crusted scabies, outbreaks, immunosuppression, treatment failure, care home/hospital setting.
  • Mimics: eczema, urticaria, insect bites, dermatitis herpetiformis, folliculitis, delusional parasitosis.
  • Red flags: crusting/hyperkeratosis, widespread secondary infection, outbreaks, immunosuppression, infants/elderly institutional setting.
Skin manifestations of systemic disease
  • Ix: acanthosis: HbA1c/lipids ± malignancy screen if abrupt; livedo: FBC/coag/autoimmune/APLS; purpura: FBC, coag, U&E, LFT, urine.
  • Tx: treat cause; acanthosis: weight/insulin resistance; livedo: vascular/autoimmune management; purpura: manage platelet/coag/vasculitic cause.
  • Escalate: same-day/admit for non-blanching rash with fever, thrombocytopenia, renal signs, meningism, DIC/TTP suspicion.
  • Mimics: bruising, vasculitis, cellulitis, livedo from cold, drug eruption, meningococcal sepsis.
  • Red flags: palpable purpura, necrosis, mucosal bleeding, fever, hypotension, neuro signs, haematuria/proteinuria, sudden widespread acanthosis.
Skin ulcers
  • Ix: ABPI before compression, pulses, neuropathy screen, HbA1c, FBC/CRP if infected; venous duplex/vascular imaging if non-healing or arterial concern.
  • Tx: venous: compression if ABPI safe; arterial: vascular risk reduction + revascularisation assessment; neuropathic: offload, podiatry, glycaemic control.
  • Escalate: vascular urgent if arterial ulcer/rest pain/gangrene; diabetic foot sameday/within 24 h if infected/ischaemic; 2WW if non-healing suspicious ulcer.
  • Mimics: pyoderma gangrenosum, vasculitis, malignancy, pressure sore, traumatic ulcer, atypical infection.
  • Red flags: ABPI <0.8, rest pain, gangrene, spreading infection, exposed tendon/bone, sepsis, rapidly enlarging painful ulcer.
Stevens–Johnson syndrome / TEN
  • Ix: emergency clinical diagnosis; drug timeline, mucosal sites, BSA detachment; FBC/U&E/LFT/CRP/coag, cultures if septic, biopsy if uncertain.
  • Tx: stop culprit drug immediately; ABCDE, fluids, analgesia, temperature control, non-adherent dressings, eye/genital care; avoid unnecessary antibiotics.
  • Escalate: immediate admission; dermatology, ophthalmology, ICU/burns centre if extensive detachment, TEN >30%, deterioration or sepsis.
  • Mimics: erythema multiforme major, staphylococcal scalded skin, bullous pemphigoid, pemphigus, severe drug eruption, burns.
  • Red flags: skin pain, dusky targets, mucosal erosions, positive Nikolsky, eye pain, fever, hypotension, rapidly increasing BSA.
Urticaria
  • Ix: clinical transient wheals <24 h; check anaphylaxis/angioedema; routine tests rarely needed unless chronic/systemic features.
  • Tx: non-sedating antihistamine: cetirizine 10 mg OD, loratadine 10 mg OD or fexofenadine 120–180 mg OD; avoid triggers/NSAIDs if worsen.
  • Escalate: 999/ED if airway, wheeze, hypotension, anaphylaxis; allergy/dermatology if chronic >6 weeks, angioedema, treatment failure.
  • Mimics: urticarial vasculitis, mastocytosis, angioedema, erythema multiforme, drug eruption, scabies.
  • Red flags: tongue/throat swelling, breathing difficulty, syncope, hypotension, wheals lasting >24 h with bruising/pain, fever/arthralgia.

Eye Problems

18 conditions
Acute glaucoma
  • Ix: emergency clinical diagnosis; reduced VA, fixed mid-dilated pupil, cloudy cornea; check IOP if available but don’t delay referral.
  • Tx: analgesia/antiemetic; acetazolamide 500 mg PO/IV if no contraindication; topical IOP-lowering drops if locally agreed.
  • Escalate: same-day emergency ophthalmology/eye casualty; bilateral prophylactic laser iridotomy often needed.
  • Mimics: uveitis, keratitis, migraine, conjunctivitis, cluster headache, iritis.
  • Red flags: severe eye pain, headache, halos, vomiting, reduced vision, hard red eye, irregular/fixed pupil.
Benign eyelid disorders
  • Ix: clinical; evert lid if foreign body; check VA/cornea if entropion, trichiasis, severe pain or photophobia.
  • Tx: blepharitis: lid hygiene/warm compresses; hordeolum/chalazion: warm compresses; ectropion/entropion: lubricants pending referral.
  • Escalate: ophthalmology if corneal damage, recurrent chalazion same site, persistent >3 months, entropion with lashes rubbing, suspicious lid lesion.
  • Mimics: preseptal cellulitis, BCC/SCC, sebaceous carcinoma, dacryocystitis, herpes zoster ophthalmicus.
  • Red flags: ulcerated lid lesion, lash loss, distorted lid margin, reduced VA, corneal staining, fever, orbital signs.
Cataracts
  • Ix: VA, pinhole, red reflex, fundoscopy if possible; slit lamp confirms lens opacity; assess impact on driving/work/falls.
  • Tx: optimise glasses/lighting short-term; definitive treatment is phacoemulsification with intraocular lens implant.
  • Escalate: routine ophthalmology if symptomatic; urgent if acute painful red eye, lens-induced glaucoma or sudden vision change.
  • Mimics: refractive error, AMD, glaucoma, diabetic retinopathy, posterior capsular opacification post-surgery.
  • Red flags: pain, redness, rapid visual loss, unilateral marked change, absent red reflex, trauma history.
Central retinal arterial occlusion
  • Ix: sudden painless monocular visual loss; fundoscopy pale retina/cherry-red spot; check ESR/CRP if GCA possible; stroke work-up.
  • Tx: treat as ocular stroke: immediate ED/stroke + ophthalmology; manage vascular risks; GCA symptoms need immediate high-dose steroid.
  • Escalate: same-day emergency stroke/ophthalmology; urgent carotid/cardiac assessment; thrombolysis decision is specialist/time-window dependent.
  • Mimics: retinal detachment, vitreous haemorrhage, optic neuritis, GCA, migraine aura, CRVO.
  • Red flags: curtain/field loss, jaw claudication, scalp tenderness, headache, AF, TIA/stroke symptoms, profound VA loss.
Central retinal vein occlusion
  • Ix: sudden/subacute painless monocular loss; fundoscopy “blood and thunder”; check BP, HbA1c/glucose, lipids, FBC, ESR/CRP if GCA concern.
  • Tx: ophthalmology-led anti-VEGF for macular oedema; PRP if neovascularisation; optimise HTN, diabetes, lipids, smoking.
  • Escalate: urgent ophthalmology, especially if poor VA, suspected ischaemic CRVO, neovascular glaucoma, pain or raised IOP.
  • Mimics: diabetic retinopathy, hypertensive retinopathy, ocular ischaemic syndrome, retinal detachment, vitreous haemorrhage.
  • Red flags: painful red eye, very poor VA, rubeosis iridis, high IOP, bilateral disease, young patient/thrombophilia features.
Chronic glaucoma
  • Ix: optometry/ophthalmology: IOP, optic disc/OCT, visual fields, gonioscopy; assess family history, steroid use, myopia.
  • Tx: prostaglandin analogue first-line often latanoprost 0.005% nocte; laser trabeculoplasty/surgery if drops fail or unsuitable.
  • Escalate: ophthalmology for all suspected glaucoma; urgent if rapidly progressive field loss, very high IOP or acute angle-closure symptoms.
  • Mimics: ocular hypertension, optic neuritis, compressive optic neuropathy, retinal disease, normal-tension glaucoma.
  • Red flags: tunnel vision, optic disc cupping, asymmetrical discs, high IOP, family history, steroid exposure, Afro-Caribbean ancestry.
Conjunctivitis
  • Ix: clinical; check VA, pupils, cornea, contact lens use; swab if neonatal, severe purulent, recurrent, STI risk or treatment failure.
  • Tx: viral/allergic usually supportive; bacterial if severe: chloramphenicol 0.5% drops q2h for 2 days then QDS, or 1% ointment nocte.
  • Escalate: same-day eye casualty if contact lens wearer, corneal opacity, reduced VA, severe pain, photophobia, herpes suspicion.
  • Mimics: keratitis, uveitis, acute glaucoma, episcleritis/scleritis, dry eye, foreign body.
  • Red flags: photophobia, reduced VA, severe pain, unilateral marked redness, ciliary flush, irregular pupil, corneal staining.
Corneal ulcer / abrasion
  • Ix: VA first; fluorescein staining; evert lid; ask contact lens/trauma/foreign body; slit lamp if available.
  • Tx: abrasion: remove foreign body if superficial, lubricants, simple analgesia; chloramphenicol if infection risk; avoid contact lenses.
  • Escalate: same-day ophthalmology if ulcer, contact lens abrasion, organic trauma, rust ring, large/central defect, reduced VA.
  • Mimics: HSV keratitis, infective keratitis, recurrent erosion, uveitis, conjunctivitis, dry eye.
  • Red flags: contact lens wearer, white corneal opacity/infiltrate, hypopyon, severe pain, photophobia, reduced VA, penetrating injury.
Hypertensive retinopathy
  • Ix: BP confirmation, fundoscopy; check U&E/eGFR, urine ACR, ECG, HbA1c/lipids; grade retinal haemorrhages, cotton wool spots, papilloedema.
  • Tx: treat hypertension per severity; malignant/accelerated HTN needs controlled BP reduction under specialist care.
  • Escalate: same-day medical admission if papilloedema, retinal haemorrhages with severe BP, AKI, chest pain, neuro symptoms.
  • Mimics: diabetic retinopathy, CRVO, anaemia, leukaemia, raised ICP, ocular ischaemic syndrome.
  • Red flags: papilloedema, BP ≥180/120 with end-organ damage, headache/confusion, chest pain, AKI, visual loss.
Infective keratitis
  • Ix: VA, fluorescein, slit lamp; corneal scrape/culture if severe/central/contact lens; ask trauma, HSV, immunosuppression.
  • Tx: stop contact lenses; urgent ophthalmology; topical antibiotics/antivirals specialist-led, often intensive hourly drops initially.
  • Escalate: same-day eye casualty for all suspected keratitis, especially contact lens, central lesion, reduced VA or hypopyon.
  • Mimics: abrasion, sterile contact lens infiltrate, uveitis, HSV keratitis, episcleritis, dry eye.
  • Red flags: corneal opacity/infiltrate, hypopyon, photophobia, severe pain, reduced VA, contact lens use, dendritic ulcer.
Inflammatory eye disease
  • Ix: VA, pupils, fluorescein, pattern of redness; uveitis has photophobia/ciliary flush; scleritis has deep boring pain; episcleritis milder.
  • Tx: episcleritis: lubricants ± oral NSAID; uveitis/scleritis need ophthalmology-led steroid/cycloplegic treatment, not DIY steroid chaos.
  • Escalate: same-day ophthalmology for suspected uveitis/scleritis; urgent systemic work-up if recurrent, bilateral, severe or systemic features.
  • Mimics: conjunctivitis, keratitis, acute glaucoma, migraine, cluster headache, foreign body.
  • Red flags: reduced VA, photophobia, severe pain, irregular pupil, corneal haze, hypopyon, autoimmune symptoms, headache/vomiting.
Macular degeneration
  • Ix: VA, Amsler grid, fundoscopy/OCT; distinguish dry AMD from wet AMD with distortion, haemorrhage, subretinal fluid.
  • Tx: dry: smoking cessation, visual aids; wet: intravitreal anti-VEGF; manage CV risk and driving/visual impairment support.
  • Escalate: urgent macular service if new distortion or suspected wet AMD; routine ophthalmology/optometry for dry AMD monitoring.
  • Mimics: diabetic maculopathy, cataract, epiretinal membrane, central serous retinopathy, retinal vein occlusion.
  • Red flags: new metamorphopsia, central scotoma, rapid VA loss, macular haemorrhage, sudden unilateral central distortion.
Optic neuritis
  • Ix: painful visual loss esp. with eye movement; check colour vision/RAPD; MRI brain/orbits; screen for MS/NMOSD/MOGAD if indicated.
  • Tx: often spontaneous recovery; IV methylprednisolone specialist-led if severe/bilateral/atypical or rapid recovery needed.
  • Escalate: same-day ophthalmology/neurology if atypical, bilateral, severe, no pain, older age, systemic symptoms, poor recovery.
  • Mimics: CRAO, GCA, compressive optic neuropathy, migraine, papilloedema, functional visual loss.
  • Red flags: age >50, jaw claudication/headache, profound bilateral loss, disc swelling with haemorrhages, neuro deficits, immunosuppression.
Papilloedema
  • Ix: bilateral disc swelling until proven raised ICP; check BP; urgent neuro exam; MRI/CT brain + MRV/CTV before LP if safe.
  • Tx: treat cause; malignant HTN, mass lesion, CVST, meningitis and IIH need urgent specialist management.
  • Escalate: same-day ED/neurology/ophthalmology; emergency if reduced consciousness, focal neurology, severe headache or vomiting.
  • Mimics: pseudopapilloedema, optic disc drusen, optic neuritis, hypertensive retinopathy, diabetic papillopathy.
  • Red flags: headache worse lying/coughing, vomiting, diplopia/VI palsy, transient visual obscurations, reduced consciousness, very high BP.
Periorbital / orbital cellulitis
  • Ix: assess VA, pupils, colour vision, eye movements, proptosis; FBC/CRP/cultures if septic; CT orbit/sinuses if orbital signs.
  • Tx: periorbital mild may use oral co-amoxiclav; orbital cellulitis needs IV antibiotics per local policy and ENT/ophthalmology.
  • Escalate: same-day admission for orbital cellulitis; urgent imaging if proptosis, painful/restricted eye movement, reduced VA, RAPD.
  • Mimics: allergic swelling, conjunctivitis, dacryocystitis, trauma, cavernous sinus thrombosis, insect bite.
  • Red flags: proptosis, ophthalmoplegia, pain on eye movement, reduced VA, RAPD, fever, severe headache, CNS signs.
Retinal detachment
  • Ix: flashes/floaters/curtain shadow; VA and visual fields; dilated fundoscopy/OCT/ocular USS if view obscured.
  • Tx: no drops fix this, tragically; retinal surgery/laser/cryotherapy depending tear/detachment type.
  • Escalate: same-day ophthalmology if field loss or suspected detachment; within 24 h for new flashes/floaters without field loss.
  • Mimics: posterior vitreous detachment, vitreous haemorrhage, migraine aura, CRAO/CRVO, optic neuritis.
  • Red flags: curtain/shadow, sudden shower of floaters, trauma, high myopia, previous detachment/surgery, reduced VA.
Subconjunctival haemorrhage
  • Ix: clinical bright red patch; check VA, pain, trauma, BP; consider FBC/coag if recurrent, bleeding history or anticoagulated.
  • Tx: reassure; lubricants if gritty; manage BP/anticoagulant issues but don’t stop anticoagulants casually like a gremlin.
  • Escalate: urgent eye review if traumatic, painful, reduced VA, suspected globe injury, recurrent unexplained bleeding.
  • Mimics: conjunctivitis, episcleritis, scleritis, trauma, conjunctival lesion.
  • Red flags: pain, photophobia, reduced VA, 360° haemorrhage after trauma, hyphaema, proptosis, bleeding elsewhere.
Visual field defects
  • Ix: define monocular vs binocular; confrontation fields, VA, pupils/RAPD; formal perimetry; neuroimaging for homonymous/bitemporal defects.
  • Tx: cause-specific: stroke pathway, pituitary/compressive lesion management, glaucoma therapy, retinal emergency referral.
  • Escalate: same-day ED/stroke/ophthalmology if sudden onset, neuro signs, papilloedema, retinal detachment symptoms or GCA features.
  • Mimics: migraine aura, retinal detachment, glaucoma, optic neuritis, pituitary lesion, stroke/TIA, functional visual loss.
  • Red flags: homonymous hemianopia, bitemporal hemianopia, sudden painless loss, headache/papilloedema, jaw claudication, focal neurology.

Endocrine Problems

26 conditions
Acromegaly
  • Ix: serum IGF-1; confirm with OGTT showing failure of GH suppression; pituitary MRI + visual fields.
  • Tx: trans-sphenoidal surgery first-line if pituitary adenoma; somatostatin analogue e.g. octreotide/lanreotide if unresectable/persistent.
  • Escalate: endocrinology for all; urgent neurosurgery/ophthalmology if visual field defect, optic compression or pituitary apoplexy.
  • Mimics: familial coarse features, severe hypothyroidism, Paget disease, anabolic steroid use, pseudoacromegaly.
  • Red flags: bitemporal hemianopia, severe headache, cranial nerve palsy, diabetes, OSA, cardiomyopathy, colon cancer risk.
Adrenal insufficiency
  • Ix: 9 am cortisol ± ACTH; short Synacthen test; U&E/glucose; renin/aldosterone if primary; adrenal antibodies if Addison’s.
  • Tx: adrenal crisis: hydrocortisone 100 mg IV/IM stat + 0.9% saline; maintenance hydrocortisone 15–25 mg/day split ± fludrocortisone 50–300 micrograms OD.
  • Escalate: admit adrenal crisis, hypotension, vomiting, severe hyponatraemia/hyperkalaemia; endocrinology for diagnosis and sick-day education.
  • Mimics: sepsis, gastroenteritis, SIADH, hypopituitarism, eating disorder, chronic fatigue, hypothyroidism.
  • Red flags: shock, collapse, severe weakness, vomiting, confusion, hypoglycaemia, Na low/K high, recent steroid withdrawal.
Arginine vasopressin deficiency/resistance
  • Ix: paired serum/urine osmolality, U&E, glucose, calcium; specialist water deprivation/desmopressin test; pituitary MRI if AVP deficiency.
  • Tx: AVP deficiency: desmopressin oral/intranasal; AVP resistance: treat cause, stop lithium if possible, low-solute diet ± thiazide specialist-led.
  • Escalate: admit if hypernatraemia, dehydration, reduced consciousness, pregnancy, post-neurosurgery polyuria or diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Mimics: primary polydipsia, uncontrolled diabetes mellitus, hypercalcaemia, hypokalaemia, CKD, diuretics.
  • Red flags: Na >150, severe thirst/polyuria, dehydration, confusion, seizures, rapid onset after head injury/neurosurgery.
Cushing syndrome
  • Ix: screen with 1 mg overnight dexamethasone suppression, late-night salivary cortisol or 24 h urinary free cortisol; ACTH then imaging.
  • Tx: treat cause: pituitary/adrenal surgery, radiotherapy or steroidogenesis inhibitors specialist-led; taper exogenous steroids safely.
  • Escalate: endocrinology for all suspected endogenous Cushing; urgent if sepsis, psychosis, severe hypokalaemia, uncontrolled diabetes/HTN.
  • Mimics: obesity, alcohol excess, depression, PCOS, metabolic syndrome, exogenous steroid exposure, pseudo-Cushing states.
  • Red flags: proximal myopathy, easy bruising, purple striae, recurrent infection, osteoporosis/fracture, severe HTN, hypokalaemia.
Diabetes mellitus
  • Ix: HbA1c ≥48 mmol/mol confirms if asymptomatic repeat; glucose/ketones if symptomatic; U&E, ACR, lipids, BP, foot + retinal screening.
  • Tx: T1 insulin; T2 lifestyle + metformin 500 mg OD/BD titrate; add SGLT2 early if HF/CKD/ASCVD/high CV risk where appropriate.
  • Escalate: same-day if ketones, weight loss, vomiting, dehydration, suspected T1, pregnancy, foot infection/ischaemia, severe hyperglycaemia.
  • Mimics: steroid hyperglycaemia, MODY, LADA, pancreatic diabetes, Cushing, acromegaly, stress hyperglycaemia.
  • Red flags: ketosis, rapid weight loss, osmotic symptoms, recurrent infection, visual change, chest pain, foot ulcer, pregnancy.
Diabetic ketoacidosis
  • Ix: ABCDE; capillary ketones ≥3 or urine ketones ++, glucose usually >11, VBG pH <7.3 or bicarbonate <15; U&E, osmolality, ECG, infection screen.
  • Tx: 0.9% saline + fixed-rate IV insulin 0.1 units/kg/h + potassium per protocol; continue long-acting basal insulin; local DKA chart applies.
  • Escalate: immediate admission/HDU if severe acidosis, shock, K abnormality, pregnancy, reduced GCS, AKI, sepsis or young/frail patient.
  • Mimics: HHS, starvation ketosis, alcoholic ketoacidosis, sepsis, lactic acidosis, salicylate toxicity.
  • Red flags: pH <7.1, K <3.5 or >5.5, reduced consciousness, vomiting, hypotension, chest pain, pregnancy, SGLT2 inhibitor use.
Diabetic nephropathy
  • Ix: annual urine ACR + eGFR; repeat abnormal ACR; BP, HbA1c, lipids; exclude haematuria/rapid decline/non-diabetic renal disease.
  • Tx: ACEi/ARB if albuminuria/HTN; optimise BP/glycaemia/lipids; SGLT2 inhibitor if CKD criteria met; avoid NSAIDs and nephrotoxins.
  • Escalate: nephrology if eGFR <30, rapid fall, ACR very high, haematuria, resistant HTN, suspected non-diabetic kidney disease.
  • Mimics: hypertensive nephropathy, glomerulonephritis, renal artery stenosis, myeloma, obstructive uropathy, drug nephrotoxicity.
  • Red flags: nephrotic syndrome, haematuria, AKI, hyperkalaemia, pulmonary oedema, rapidly rising creatinine.
Diabetic neuropathy
  • Ix: foot pulses, monofilament, vibration/proprioception, reflexes; check B12, TFT, alcohol, renal function; assess ulcer risk.
  • Tx: painful neuropathy: duloxetine first-line often 60 mg OD; alternatives amitriptyline, pregabalin, gabapentin; foot protection/podiatry.
  • Escalate: urgent diabetic foot service if ulcer, infection, ischaemia, Charcot foot; neurology if asymmetrical, motor, rapid or atypical.
  • Mimics: B12 deficiency, alcohol neuropathy, radiculopathy, CIDP, hypothyroidism, uraemia, vasculitis.
  • Red flags: hot swollen foot, ulcer, spreading infection, rest pain, foot drop, autonomic symptoms, rapid progression.
Diabetic retinopathy
  • Ix: annual retinal screening; urgent ophthalmology if sudden visual symptoms; assess HbA1c, BP, lipids, renal disease.
  • Tx: optimise glucose/BP/lipids; ophthalmology: laser, intravitreal anti-VEGF or vitrectomy depending maculopathy/proliferation.
  • Escalate: same-day/urgent if sudden visual loss, vitreous haemorrhage, retinal detachment symptoms; pregnant diabetics need enhanced screening.
  • Mimics: hypertensive retinopathy, CRVO, AMD, retinal detachment, ocular ischaemic syndrome.
  • Red flags: new floaters, curtain vision, macular oedema, proliferative changes, pregnancy, rapid HbA1c improvement with severe baseline disease.
Graves disease
  • Ix: TSH low, FT4/FT3 high; TRAb positive; ECG if palpitations; assess thyroid eye disease, goitre, bruit, smoking.
  • Tx: carbimazole commonly 20–40 mg OD initially + propranolol 40 mg TDS/QDS if symptomatic; radioiodine/surgery specialist-led.
  • Escalate: endocrinology for all; urgent if thyroid storm, pregnancy, severe eye disease, arrhythmia, agranulocytosis symptoms on carbimazole.
  • Mimics: thyroiditis, toxic nodular goitre, exogenous thyroxine, anxiety, phaeochromocytoma, medication-induced thyrotoxicosis.
  • Red flags: fever/sore throat on carbimazole, diplopia, reduced colour vision, corneal exposure, AF, heart failure, fever/confusion.
Haemochromatosis
  • Ix: ferritin + transferrin saturation; LFT, glucose/HbA1c, FBC; HFE genetic testing if iron overload pattern.
  • Tx: venesection until ferritin target, then maintenance; avoid iron/vitamin C supplements, manage alcohol and liver risk.
  • Escalate: hepatology/haematology if ferritin >1000, abnormal LFTs, cirrhosis concern, diabetes, cardiomyopathy, family screening.
  • Mimics: alcohol-related liver disease, NAFLD, inflammation, metabolic syndrome, iron-loading anaemia, repeated transfusion.
  • Red flags: cirrhosis, hepatocellular carcinoma risk, heart failure/arrhythmia, diabetes, hypogonadism, bronze pigmentation.
Hyperlipidaemia
  • Ix: non-fasting lipid profile, QRISK3, HbA1c, TFT, LFT, renal function; suspect FH if TC >7.5 or LDL >4.9 plus family history.
  • Tx: lifestyle; atorvastatin 20 mg OD primary prevention if QRISK ≥10%; atorvastatin 80 mg OD secondary prevention if tolerated.
  • Escalate: lipid clinic if suspected familial hypercholesterolaemia, triglycerides >10, statin intolerance needing complex therapy.
  • Mimics: hypothyroidism, nephrotic syndrome, cholestasis, alcohol excess, diabetes, steroids, pregnancy.
  • Red flags: pancreatitis risk with very high TG, tendon xanthomata, premature CVD, strong family history, eruptive xanthomas.
Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state
  • Ix: glucose usually >30, osmolality >320, marked dehydration, pH >7.3, bicarbonate >15, ketones usually <3; U&E hourly initially.
  • Tx: medical emergency: 0.9% saline first; potassium per protocol; insulin usually delayed unless ketonaemia or glucose stops falling with fluids.
  • Escalate: admit/HDU if osmolality high, shock, AKI, reduced GCS, sepsis, thrombosis, frailty; senior diabetes/acute medicine review.
  • Mimics: DKA, sepsis delirium, stroke, steroid hyperglycaemia, lactic acidosis, renal failure.
  • Red flags: osmolality >350, Na abnormality, coma, seizures, hypovolaemic shock, thrombosis, lactate high, elderly/frail patient.
Hyperparathyroidism / hypoparathyroidism
  • Ix: adjusted calcium, phosphate, magnesium, PTH, vitamin D, U&E/eGFR; urinary calcium if primary hyperparathyroidism/FHH question.
  • Tx: hypercalcaemia severe: IV 0.9% saline ± IV bisphosphonate; hypocalcaemia symptomatic: calcium gluconate 10% 10–20 mL IV.
  • Escalate: admit severe Ca >3.0, symptoms, AKI, ECG changes; endocrine/surgery for primary hyperparathyroidism meeting criteria.
  • Mimics: malignancy hypercalcaemia, vitamin D excess/deficiency, CKD-MBD, FHH, hypomagnesaemia, pancreatitis.
  • Red flags: confusion, dehydration, arrhythmia, seizures, tetany, laryngospasm, pancreatitis, renal stones, shortened/long QT.
Hyperprolactinaemia
  • Ix: repeat fasting prolactin; pregnancy test, TFT, U&E, medication review, macroprolactin; pituitary MRI if persistent/unexplained.
  • Tx: treat cause; prolactinoma: cabergoline first-line specialist-led; stop/switch causative drugs only with appropriate prescriber input.
  • Escalate: endocrinology if prolactin persistently raised, galactorrhoea/amenorrhoea/infertility/hypogonadism, pituitary mass symptoms.
  • Mimics: pregnancy, hypothyroidism, antipsychotics/metoclopramide, CKD, PCOS, macroprolactin, chest wall stimulation.
  • Red flags: headache, bitemporal hemianopia, ophthalmoplegia, very high prolactin, pituitary apoplexy symptoms.
Hyperthyroidism / thyrotoxicosis
  • Ix: TSH, FT4/FT3; TRAb if Graves suspected; ESR/CRP if thyroiditis; ECG if tachycardia/AF.
  • Tx: beta-blocker for symptoms e.g. propranolol 40 mg TDS; carbimazole specialist/locally guided; thyroiditis usually NSAID ± beta-blocker.
  • Escalate: same-day/admit thyroid storm, AF with instability, heart failure, pregnancy, severe symptoms, suspected amiodarone thyrotoxicosis.
  • Mimics: anxiety, sepsis, phaeochromocytoma, menopause, stimulant use, thyroiditis, exogenous thyroid hormone.
  • Red flags: fever, agitation/confusion, vomiting/diarrhoea, jaundice, AF, heart failure, collapse, sore throat on carbimazole.
Hypoglycaemia
  • Ix: capillary glucose <4 mmol/L; if unexplained take lab glucose, insulin/Cpeptide, sulfonylurea screen, cortisol; review diabetes meds/alcohol.
  • Tx: conscious: 15–20 g fast carbohydrate, recheck 10–15 min; unconscious: glucagon 1 mg IM or IV glucose per local protocol.
  • Escalate: 999/admit if reduced consciousness, seizure, recurrent hypo, longacting insulin/sulfonylurea, frailty, liver disease, adrenal crisis.
  • Mimics: seizure, syncope, stroke/TIA, intoxication, panic attack, sepsis, adrenal insufficiency.
  • Red flags: no recovery, recurrent within hours, hypothermia, seizure, pregnancy, insulin overdose, renal failure, beta-blocker masking symptoms.
Hypopituitarism
  • Ix: 9 am cortisol/ACTH, TFT, prolactin, LH/FSH/testosterone or oestradiol, IGF-1, U&E; pituitary MRI + visual fields.
  • Tx: replace cortisol first with hydrocortisone before levothyroxine; sex hormone/GH replacement specialist-led.
  • Escalate: urgent endocrine/neurosurgery if pituitary apoplexy, visual field loss, severe hyponatraemia, adrenal crisis.
  • Mimics: primary hypothyroidism, Addison’s, depression, anorexia, chronic systemic disease, menopause, opioid/steroid suppression.
  • Red flags: severe headache, visual loss, ophthalmoplegia, hypotension, hypoglycaemia, hyponatraemia, postpartum collapse/Sheehan syndrome.
Hypothyroidism
  • Ix: TSH high + FT4 low confirms primary; TPO antibodies; check lipids, FBC; central hypothyroidism: low/normal TSH with low FT4.
  • Tx: levothyroxine first-line; usually 50–100 micrograms OD, or 25 micrograms OD in elderly/CVD; titrate by TSH every 6–8 weeks.
  • Escalate: endocrinology if pregnancy, central disease, persistent symptoms despite normal TSH, age <16, suspected myxoedema coma.
  • Mimics: depression, anaemia, menopause, chronic fatigue, CKD, dementia, obesity, sleep apnoea.
  • Red flags: hypothermia, bradycardia, reduced consciousness, hyponatraemia, pericardial effusion, pregnancy, pituitary symptoms.
Metabolic syndrome
  • Ix: waist/BMI, BP, HbA1c/glucose, lipids, LFT, U&E/eGFR, urine ACR; screen OSA/NAFLD/CVD risk.
  • Tx: weight loss, Mediterranean-style diet, exercise, smoking cessation; treat BP, lipids and dysglycaemia individually.
  • Escalate: diabetes prevention/weight management service; hepatology if advanced liver fibrosis markers; cardiology if symptomatic CVD.
  • Mimics: Cushing syndrome, hypothyroidism, PCOS, familial dyslipidaemia, medication-related weight gain.
  • Red flags: HbA1c diabetic range, severe HTN, TG >10, abnormal fibrosis score, chest pain, OSA with daytime somnolence.
Phaeochromocytoma
  • Ix: plasma free or urinary fractionated metanephrines; CT/MRI adrenal; genetic testing if confirmed/young/bilateral/family history.
  • Tx: alpha-blockade before surgery, commonly doxazosin/phenoxybenzamine specialist-led; never start beta-blocker before alpha-blockade.
  • Escalate: endocrinology/surgery for all; emergency admission if hypertensive crisis, arrhythmia, stroke symptoms, cardiomyopathy.
  • Mimics: panic attacks, hyperthyroidism, drug withdrawal, carcinoid, mastocytosis, migraine, primary aldosteronism.
  • Red flags: episodic headache, sweating, palpitations, labile severe HTN, adrenal incidentaloma, crisis after anaesthesia/metoclopramide.
Pituitary tumours
  • Ix: pituitary profile: prolactin, IGF-1, 9 am cortisol, TFT, LH/FSH/sex hormones; MRI pituitary + formal visual fields.
  • Tx: prolactinoma: dopamine agonist; non-functioning/compressive tumours: trans-sphenoidal surgery; replace deficient hormones safely.
  • Escalate: urgent neurosurgery/endocrine if apoplexy, visual field loss, ophthalmoplegia, reduced consciousness, adrenal insufficiency.
  • Mimics: craniopharyngioma, meningioma, aneurysm, metastasis, hypophysitis, Rathke cleft cyst.
  • Red flags: sudden severe headache, bitemporal hemianopia, diplopia, vomiting, hypotension, hyponatraemia, low cortisol.
Prediabetes
  • Ix: HbA1c 42–47 mmol/mol or fasting glucose 5.5–6.9; repeat if borderline; assess BMI, BP, lipids, QRISK, NAFLD risk.
  • Tx: diabetes prevention programme, 5–10% weight loss target, diet/exercise; consider metformin in high-risk patients if lifestyle insufficient.
  • Escalate: diabetes team if symptomatic hyperglycaemia, HbA1c reaches ≥48, pregnancy/planning pregnancy, atypical diabetes features.
  • Mimics: stress hyperglycaemia, steroid effect, iron deficiency/haemoglobinopathy affecting HbA1c, early T1/LADA.
  • Red flags: osmotic symptoms, weight loss, ketones, recurrent infections, HbA1c rapidly rising, high-risk ethnicity/family history.
Primary aldosteronism
  • Ix: suspect resistant HTN/hypokalaemia; aldosterone-renin ratio after correcting K and reviewing interfering meds; adrenal CT ± adrenal vein sampling.
  • Tx: unilateral adenoma: adrenalectomy; bilateral hyperplasia/non-surgical: spironolactone or eplerenone specialist-led.
  • Escalate: endocrine/HTN clinic for positive screen; urgent admission if severe hypokalaemia, arrhythmia, malignant HTN.
  • Mimics: diuretic use, Cushing, liquorice, renal artery stenosis, Liddle syndrome, vomiting/laxatives.
  • Red flags: K <3.0, resistant HTN, young-onset HTN, adrenal incidentaloma, AF/arrhythmia, stroke at young age.
Refeeding syndrome
  • Ix: identify high risk: low BMI, weight loss, little intake, alcohol, insulin/chemo/diuretics; check phosphate, Mg, K, glucose, U&E.
  • Tx: thiamine 200–300 mg/day before feeding; start 10 kcal/kg/day, or 5 kcal/kg/day if extreme risk; replace K/Mg/phosphate.
  • Escalate: admit/medical dietetics if high risk, severe electrolyte derangement, eating disorder instability, arrhythmia, heart failure.
  • Mimics: malnutrition alone, alcohol withdrawal, sepsis, DKA treatment shifts, diuretic-induced electrolyte loss.
  • Red flags: phosphate very low, arrhythmia, oedema, heart failure, seizures, confusion, respiratory weakness, BMI <14 or negligible intake >15 days.
SIADH
  • Ix: hypotonic hyponatraemia: low serum osmolality, urine osmolality >100, urine Na >30, euvolaemia; exclude adrenal, thyroid, renal failure.
  • Tx: fluid restriction first-line if mild/moderate chronic; stop culprit drugs; severe symptomatic hyponatraemia needs hypertonic saline specialist-led.
  • Escalate: admit if Na <125, symptoms, rapid fall, seizures, confusion, malignancy/CNS/pulmonary cause suspected.
  • Mimics: adrenal insufficiency, hypothyroidism, heart failure, cirrhosis, renal failure, diuretics, primary polydipsia.
  • Red flags: seizure, coma, vomiting, severe headache, Na <120, rapid correction risk, malnutrition/alcoholism, postoperative state.

Cardiovascular Problems

30 conditions
Acute coronary syndromes
  • Ix: ABCDE, 12-lead ECG within 10 min, repeat ECGs, troponin 0/1–3 h, FBC/U&E/LFT/coag/lipids/HbA1c, CXR if needed.
  • Tx: aspirin 300 mg stat unless allergic; GTN if BP ok; morphine + antiemetic if severe pain; STEMI: primary PCI; NSTEMI: risk-stratify + antithrombotics.
  • Escalate: 999/CCU/cath lab for STEMI, ongoing ischaemia, shock, arrhythmia, acute HF, dynamic ECG changes, very high troponin.
  • Mimics: PE, aortic dissection, pericarditis, pneumothorax, oesophageal spasm, pancreatitis, MSK pain.
  • Red flags: crushing chest pain, diaphoresis, syncope, hypotension, new murmur, pulmonary oedema, VT/VF, posterior/right-sided MI.
Aneurysms, ischaemic limb and occlusions
  • Ix: vascular exam, pulses/Doppler, ABPI, lactate, FBC/U&E/coag/group & save; CT angiography if acute limb ischaemia or aneurysm complication.
  • Tx: acute limb ischaemia: IV unfractionated heparin unless contraindicated, analgesia, keep limb dependent, nil by mouth.
  • Escalate: immediate vascular surgery for 6 Ps, threatened limb, rupture, embolus, aneurysm pain, neurological deficit.
  • Mimics: DVT, cellulitis, neuropathy, compartment syndrome, sciatica, critical limb ischaemia, vasculitis.
  • Red flags: pain, pallor, pulselessness, paraesthesia, paralysis, perishingly cold limb; paralysis means the limb is already losing the argument.
Aortic aneurysm
  • Ix: USS screening/monitoring for AAA; CT angiography if symptomatic, expanding, pre-op or rupture suspected; check BP/CV risks.
  • Tx: smoking cessation, BP/lipid control, antiplatelet/statin if vascular disease; elective repair generally considered AAA ≥5.5 cm or rapid growth.
  • Escalate: 999/vascular same-day if abdominal/back pain, hypotension, collapse, pulsatile mass; urgent vascular if symptomatic aneurysm.
  • Mimics: renal colic, diverticulitis, pancreatitis, MSK back pain, aortic dissection, mesenteric ischaemia.
  • Red flags: syncope, shock, severe back/abdominal pain, tender pulsatile mass, known AAA, rapid expansion.
Aortic dissection
  • Ix: ABCDE, bilateral BP/pulses, ECG/troponin, CXR; CT angiography aorta if stable; TOE/MRI if CT unsuitable.
  • Tx: analgesia + BP/HR control, usually IV beta-blocker e.g. labetalol/esmolol specialist-led; avoid anticoagulation unless clearly indicated.
  • Escalate: immediate cardiothoracic/vascular; type A = emergency surgery, type B complicated = vascular intervention.
  • Mimics: ACS, PE, stroke, pericarditis, pneumothorax, ruptured AAA, renal colic.
  • Red flags: tearing chest/back pain, pulse/BP differential, neuro deficit, syncope, AR murmur, tamponade, shock.
Aortic valve disease
  • Ix: murmur assessment, ECG, CXR, BNP if HF, transthoracic echo confirms severity; exercise testing specialist-led if uncertain symptoms.
  • Tx: manage HF/AF/HTN cautiously; severe symptomatic AS needs valve replacement/TAVI; AR surgery if symptomatic or LV dilation/dysfunction.
  • Escalate: cardiology for all moderate–severe disease; urgent if syncope, angina, HF, severe AS, suspected endocarditis.
  • Mimics: HCM, flow murmur, mitral disease, anaemia, thyrotoxicosis, pulmonary stenosis.
  • Red flags: exertional syncope, angina, dyspnoea, pulmonary oedema, soft/absent S2 in AS, collapsing pulse in AR.
Arterial thrombosis / embolism
  • Ix: limb/organ ischaemia assessment, ECG for AF, FBC/U&E/coag, lactate, thrombophilia only if selected; CT angiography if acute occlusion.
  • Tx: IV unfractionated heparin unless contraindicated; analgesia; treat embolic source; revascularisation by thrombolysis, thrombectomy or bypass.
  • Escalate: same-day vascular/interventional radiology for acute occlusion; stroke/mesenteric/coronary pathways if organ-specific.
  • Mimics: DVT, vasospasm, compartment syndrome, neuropathy, cellulitis, gout.
  • Red flags: sudden severe pain, cold pulseless limb, neuro deficit, mottling, renal/mesenteric ischaemia, AF.
Bradyarrhythmias
  • Ix: ABCDE, ECG rhythm strip, U&E including K/Mg/Ca, glucose, TFT, drug review, troponin if ACS, check haemodynamic compromise.
  • Tx: adverse features: atropine 500 micrograms IV, repeat to 3 mg; prepare pacing; correct causes e.g. hyperkalaemia, drugs.
  • Escalate: immediate senior/ALS if shock, syncope, ischaemia, HF; cardiology for Mobitz II, complete heart block, pauses, pacemaker need.
  • Mimics: vasovagal syncope, hypothermia, hyperkalaemia, beta-blocker/digoxin toxicity, inferior MI, athletic bradycardia.
  • Red flags: HR <40, systolic BP <90, syncope, chest pain, HF, broad QRS escape, complete AV block.
Cardiac arrest
  • Ix: confirm unresponsive/not breathing normally; rhythm check during ALS; identify 4 Hs/4 Ts: hypoxia, hypovolaemia, hypo/hyperkalaemia/metabolic, hypothermia; thrombosis, tamponade, toxins, tension pneumothorax.
  • Tx: high-quality CPR 30:2, defibrillate shockable rhythm; adrenaline 1 mg IV/IO every 3–5 min; amiodarone 300 mg after 3 shocks.
  • Escalate: ALS team immediately; reversible cause teams: cath lab, thrombolysis/PE, thoracostomy, pericardiocentesis, ECMO where available.
  • Mimics: seizure, syncope, opioid toxicity, severe hypoglycaemia, pseudo-PEA, profound shock.
  • Red flags: no pulse, agonal breathing, shockable rhythm, peri-arrest arrhythmia, post-ROSC coma/shock/STEMI.
Cardiomyopathy
  • Ix: ECG, BNP, CXR, echo, troponin if acute, TFT/ferritin/HbA1c; cardiac MRI/genetics if indicated.
  • Tx: phenotype-specific; HFrEF therapy if dilated: ACEi/ARB/ARNI, beta-blocker, MRA, SGLT2; avoid dehydration/vasodilators in obstructive HCM.
  • Escalate: cardiology for all suspected; urgent if syncope, VT, severe LV dysfunction, family sudden death, myocarditis, decompensated HF.
  • Mimics: IHD, valvular disease, hypertensive heart disease, athlete’s heart, infiltrative disease, alcohol/toxin cardiomyopathy.
  • Red flags: exertional syncope, sudden death family history, palpitations with collapse, HF, abnormal ECG, LV wall thickness marked.
Carotid dissection
  • Ix: neuro exam, Horner syndrome check; CT/MR angiography head/neck; brain imaging for stroke/TIA.
  • Tx: stroke-unit care; antiplatelet or anticoagulation specialist-led; analgesia; avoid neck manipulation because apparently arteries object.
  • Escalate: same-day stroke/neurology for suspected dissection, TIA/stroke, severe headache/neck pain with neuro signs.
  • Mimics: migraine, SAH, cluster headache, TIA, cervical radiculopathy, vertebral artery dissection.
  • Red flags: unilateral headache/neck pain, partial Horner syndrome, TIA/stroke symptoms, pulsatile tinnitus, recent trauma/manipulation.
Congenital heart disease
  • Ix: ECG, CXR, sats, echo; cardiac MRI/CT and exercise testing specialist-led; screen pulmonary HTN/arrhythmia/endocarditis risk.
  • Tx: lesion-specific; manage HF/arrhythmias; endocarditis prevention advice; pregnancy contraception counselling in complex disease.
  • Escalate: adult congenital heart disease cardiology; urgent if cyanosis, syncope, HF, arrhythmia, pregnancy, infective endocarditis concern.
  • Mimics: valvular disease, cardiomyopathy, asthma, anxiety, anaemia, pulmonary HTN.
  • Red flags: central cyanosis, clubbing, exertional syncope, haemoptysis, new murmur, reduced exercise tolerance, Eisenmenger physiology.
Deep vein thrombosis
  • Ix: Wells score; D-dimer if unlikely; proximal leg vein USS; FBC/U&E/LFT/coag before anticoagulation.
  • Tx: anticoagulate while awaiting imaging if delay and likely DVT; apixaban 10 mg BD 7 days then 5 mg BD or rivaroxaban 15 mg BD 21 days then 20 mg OD.
  • Escalate: same-day if suspected PE, limb-threatening iliofemoral DVT, pregnancy, high bleeding risk, renal failure, cancer complexity.
  • Mimics: cellulitis, ruptured Baker cyst, superficial thrombophlebitis, lymphoedema, MSK injury, chronic venous disease.
  • Red flags: dyspnoea, chest pain, haemoptysis, syncope, whole-leg swelling, phlegmasia, severe pain, cancer.
Gangrene
  • Ix: vascular assessment, pulses/Doppler, ABPI/toe pressures, FBC/CRP/U&E/lactate, glucose, cultures if infected; CT/MR angiography if revascularisation possible.
  • Tx: analgesia, wound care, offloading, broad-spectrum antibiotics if wet/infected, optimise diabetes/CV risk; dry gangrene kept clean/dry pending vascular plan.
  • Escalate: same-day vascular/surgical; emergency if wet gangrene, sepsis, gas, spreading infection, critical limb ischaemia.
  • Mimics: nec fasc, pressure necrosis, vasculitis, embolic blue toe, frostbite, calciphylaxis.
  • Red flags: wet malodorous tissue, crepitus, fever, severe pain, spreading cellulitis, absent pulses, systemic toxicity.
Heart failure
  • Ix: BNP/NT-proBNP, ECG, CXR, FBC/U&E/LFT/TFT/HbA1c/lipids, echo confirms type/cause.
  • Tx: acute pulmonary oedema: sit up, oxygen if hypoxic, IV furosemide 20–40 mg; chronic HFrEF: ACEi/ARB/ARNI + beta-blocker + MRA + SGLT2.
  • Escalate: admit if new severe HF, hypoxia, pulmonary oedema, hypotension, ACS, arrhythmia, AKI, poor response to diuretics.
  • Mimics: COPD/asthma, PE, pneumonia, anaemia, renal failure, obesity/deconditioning.
  • Red flags: pink frothy sputum, sats low, shock, chest pain, syncope, new murmur, rapid weight gain, severe oedema.
Hypertension
  • Ix: confirm with ABPM/HBPM; U&E/eGFR, urine ACR, HbA1c, lipids, ECG; secondary screen if young/resistant/hypokalaemia.
  • Tx: lifestyle; step 1 usually ACEi/ARB if <55/non-Black African/Caribbean, or amlodipine 5 mg OD if ≥55/Black African/Caribbean.
  • Escalate: same-day if BP ≥180/120 with retinal haemorrhage/papilloedema or life-threatening end-organ damage.
  • Mimics: white-coat HTN, pain/anxiety, drugs, OSA, renal disease, primary aldosteronism, phaeochromocytoma.
  • Red flags: chest pain, neuro deficit, confusion, AKI, pulmonary oedema, pregnancy, papilloedema, severe headache.
Infective endocarditis
  • Ix: 3 sets blood cultures before antibiotics if stable, FBC/CRP/U&E, ECG, urine, echo; TOE if prosthetic valve or high suspicion.
  • Tx: IV antibiotics after cultures, or immediately if septic; regimen per local microbiology; surgery for valve failure/abscess/ongoing emboli.
  • Escalate: same-day admission/cardiology/microbiology; urgent if sepsis, HF, stroke, prosthetic valve, conduction block.
  • Mimics: sepsis source unknown, vasculitis, malignancy, rheumatic fever, myxoma, viral illness.
  • Red flags: fever + murmur, emboli, splinter haemorrhages, Janeway lesions, stroke, back pain, prosthetic valve/IVDU.
Ischaemic heart disease / stable angina
  • Ix: ECG, FBC/U&E/lipids/HbA1c/TFT, CT coronary angiography first-line for stable chest pain; functional imaging/angiography if needed.
  • Tx: GTN spray PRN; beta-blocker or calcium-channel blocker first-line; aspirin 75 mg OD + atorvastatin 80 mg OD if confirmed CAD.
  • Escalate: 999 if pain at rest, prolonged >15 min, not relieved by GTN, ACS features; cardiology if uncontrolled symptoms.
  • Mimics: GORD, costochondritis, anxiety, PE, aortic stenosis, HCM, biliary disease.
  • Red flags: crescendo angina, rest pain, syncope, dyspnoea, new HF, dynamic ECG change, diabetes with atypical symptoms.
Mitral valve disease
  • Ix: ECG, CXR, BNP if HF, transthoracic echo; TOE if endocarditis, repair planning or unclear severity.
  • Tx: manage AF/HF; anticoagulate AF in mitral stenosis with warfarin where indicated; repair/replacement for severe symptomatic disease.
  • Escalate: cardiology for moderate–severe disease; urgent if pulmonary oedema, new AF, embolic event, endocarditis, chordal rupture.
  • Mimics: aortic disease, HCM, flow murmur, pulmonary HTN, anaemia, thyrotoxicosis.
  • Red flags: dyspnoea, orthopnoea, haemoptysis, AF, embolic stroke, loud P2, acute severe MR with shock.
Myocarditis
  • Ix: ECG, troponin, CRP, BNP, viral history, echo; cardiac MRI confirms; coronary imaging if ACS cannot be excluded.
  • Tx: admit if suspected significant myocarditis; rest from exercise, treat HF/arrhythmias; avoid NSAIDs in HF; cause-specific therapy specialist-led.
  • Escalate: cardiology/CCU if chest pain + troponin, arrhythmia, syncope, LV dysfunction, shock, fulminant presentation.
  • Mimics: ACS, pericarditis, PE, Takotsubo, sepsis cardiomyopathy, costochondritis.
  • Red flags: syncope, VT, heart block, hypotension, rising troponin, HF, recent viral illness with chest pain, sudden death risk.
Orthostatic / postural hypotension
  • Ix: lying/standing BP and HR at 1 and 3 min; medication review; FBC/U&E/glucose/B12; ECG if syncope/palpitations.
  • Tx: fluids/salt if suitable, slow standing, compression stockings, stop culprit drugs; midodrine/fludrocortisone specialist or selected use.
  • Escalate: urgent if syncope injury, arrhythmia, GI bleed/dehydration, neuro signs, adrenal crisis, recurrent unexplained falls.
  • Mimics: vasovagal syncope, arrhythmia, hypoglycaemia, seizure, PE, autonomic neuropathy.
  • Red flags: exertional syncope, chest pain, palpitations, melaena, focal neurology, severe dehydration, new Parkinsonism/autonomic failure.
Pericardial disease
  • Ix: ECG, troponin, CRP, FBC/U&E, CXR, echo if effusion/tamponade suspected; pulsus paradoxus/JVP assessment.
  • Tx: pericarditis: ibuprofen 600 mg TDS + colchicine 500 micrograms BD if suitable; tamponade: urgent pericardiocentesis.
  • Escalate: admit if fever, immunosuppression, trauma, anticoagulation, large effusion, tamponade, high troponin, failed NSAIDs.
  • Mimics: ACS, PE, aortic dissection, pleurisy, myocarditis, GORD.
  • Red flags: hypotension, raised JVP, muffled heart sounds, pulsus paradoxus, syncope, electrical alternans, severe dyspnoea.
Peripheral vascular disease
  • Ix: pulses, ABPI, vascular risk profile, HbA1c/lipids, foot inspection; duplex/CTA/MRA if revascularisation considered.
  • Tx: smoking cessation, supervised exercise, clopidogrel 75 mg OD, statin, BP/diabetes control; cilostazol sometimes for claudication if suitable.
  • Escalate: vascular if lifestyle-limiting claudication despite therapy, rest pain, ulcers, gangrene, rapidly worsening symptoms.
  • Mimics: spinal stenosis, neuropathy, hip/knee OA, venous claudication, chronic exertional compartment syndrome.
  • Red flags: rest pain, tissue loss, non-healing ulcer, gangrene, acute limb ischaemia, ABPI very low.
Pulmonary embolism
  • Ix: ABCDE, ECG/CXR, ABG/VBG if hypoxic, Wells, D-dimer if unlikely, CTPA; V/Q if CTPA unsuitable; troponin/BNP/echo if severe.
  • Tx: anticoagulate if likely while awaiting imaging; DOAC as for DVT if stable; massive PE with shock: thrombolysis/embolectomy specialist-led.
  • Escalate: immediate admission if hypoxia, shock, syncope, RV strain, pregnancy, high bleeding risk, severe comorbidity.
  • Mimics: ACS, pneumonia, pneumothorax, asthma/COPD, pericarditis, anxiety.
  • Red flags: hypotension, syncope, haemoptysis, pleuritic chest pain, sats low, tachycardia, cancer, recent surgery/immobility.
Pulmonary hypertension
  • Ix: ECG, CXR, BNP, echo; confirm with right heart catheterisation; investigate cause: CTPA/VQ, lung function, CT chest, autoimmune/HIV/LFT.
  • Tx: treat cause, oxygen if hypoxic, diuretics for congestion; PAH-specific drugs specialist-led.
  • Escalate: pulmonary hypertension centre; urgent if syncope, RV failure, severe hypoxia, pregnancy, rapid deterioration.
  • Mimics: asthma/COPD, HFpEF, PE/CTEPH, deconditioning, anaemia, anxiety.
  • Red flags: exertional syncope, loud P2, raised JVP, peripheral oedema, cyanosis, haemoptysis, RV strain.
Right heart valve disease
  • Ix: ECG, CXR, BNP, echo; assess pulmonary pressures, RV size/function, IVDU/endocarditis risk.
  • Tx: treat cause: pulmonary HTN, left-sided disease, endocarditis; diuretics for congestion; valve intervention specialist-led.
  • Escalate: cardiology if moderate–severe TR/PS/PR, RV dysfunction, endocarditis, cyanosis, HF symptoms.
  • Mimics: constrictive pericarditis, pulmonary hypertension, left HF, liver disease, nephrotic syndrome.
  • Red flags: ascites, severe oedema, pulsatile liver, raised JVP, cyanosis, syncope, fever/IVDU with TR murmur.
Shock
  • Ix: ABCDE, BP/MAP, lactate, VBG/ABG, ECG, glucose, FBC/U&E/LFT/coag, cultures if sepsis, bedside echo/USS if available.
  • Tx: oxygen if hypoxic, IV access, 250–500 mL crystalloid bolus then reassess; treat cause: sepsis, bleeding, ACS, PE, tamponade, anaphylaxis.
  • Escalate: immediate senior/ICU if persistent hypotension, lactate high, vasopressors, airway compromise, major haemorrhage.
  • Mimics: vasovagal episode, severe dehydration without shock, panic, medication hypotension.
  • Red flags: cold clammy skin, confusion, oliguria, lactate ≥2, systolic BP <90, mottling, chest pain, bleeding, sepsis.
Superior vena cava obstruction
  • Ix: clinical facial/neck/arm swelling; CXR, urgent contrast CT chest; tissue diagnosis if malignancy suspected; check airway/neuro signs.
  • Tx: head elevation, oxygen if hypoxic; steroids if lymphoma suspected or airway compromise; stent/radiotherapy/chemo specialist-led.
  • Escalate: same-day oncology/respiratory; emergency if stridor, cerebral oedema, syncope, severe dyspnoea.
  • Mimics: anaphylaxis, angioedema, heart failure, nephrotic syndrome, bilateral upper limb DVT, goitre.
  • Red flags: stridor, confusion, headache, visual symptoms, cyanosis, severe dyspnoea, rapidly progressive swelling.
Syncope
  • Ix: ECG for all, lying/standing BP, glucose, pregnancy test if relevant; targeted FBC/U&E/troponin if bleeding/ACS/electrolyte concern.
  • Tx: vasovagal: reassurance, fluids, counter-pressure manoeuvres; treat cause; driving advice if recurrent/unexplained or cardiac.
  • Escalate: same-day/admit if abnormal ECG, exertional/supine syncope, chest pain, palpitations, HF, family sudden death, severe injury.
  • Mimics: seizure, hypoglycaemia, TIA, falls, intoxication, panic attack.
  • Red flags: no prodrome, during exertion, while lying down, new murmur, long QT, AV block, WPW, VT, structural heart disease.
Tachyarrhythmias
  • Ix: ABCDE, 12-lead ECG, rhythm strip, U&E/Mg/Ca, TFT, troponin if ACS; identify broad/narrow, regular/irregular, adverse features.
  • Tx: unstable: synchronised DC cardioversion; SVT stable: vagal then adenosine 6 mg, 12 mg, 12 mg; torsades: magnesium sulfate 2 g IV.
  • Escalate: ALS/cardiology if VT, pre-excited AF, AF with shock/HF/ischaemia, recurrent SVT, drug toxicity, prolonged QT.
  • Mimics: sinus tachycardia from sepsis/PE/bleed, anxiety, hyperthyroidism, stimulant use, artefact.
  • Red flags: shock, syncope, chest pain, HF, HR >150 with instability, broadcomplex tachycardia, irregular broad-complex rhythm.
Venous insufficiency / varicose veins
  • Ix: clinical standing exam; ABPI before compression if arterial disease risk; duplex USS if symptomatic/recurrent/ulceration or intervention planned.
  • Tx: exercise, weight loss, leg elevation; compression if no arterial disease; endothermal ablation first-line for suitable varicose veins.
  • Escalate: vascular referral if bleeding varicosity, ulcer/healed ulcer, skin changes, superficial thrombophlebitis, severe symptoms.
  • Mimics: DVT, lymphoedema, cellulitis, lipodermatosclerosis, heart failure, arterial ulcer.
  • Red flags: active bleeding, venous ulcer, sudden unilateral swelling, severe pain, skin necrosis, suspected DVT.

Respiratory Problems

19 conditions
Acute bronchitis
  • Ix: clinical; exclude pneumonia/asthma/COPD; check sats/temp/HR/RR; CXR only if pneumonia, cancer or serious illness suspected.
  • Tx: self-care, fluids, paracetamol/ibuprofen; no routine antibiotics; consider delayed/immediate antibiotic only if high-risk/systemically very unwell.
  • Escalate: same-day/admit if hypoxia, sepsis, haemoptysis, frailty with deterioration, suspected pneumonia/PE.
  • Mimics: pneumonia, asthma, COPD exacerbation, COVID/flu, PE, lung cancer, heart failure.
  • Red flags: sats <94%, RR >24, confusion, pleuritic pain, haemoptysis, fever >4 days, immunosuppression.
Acute respiratory distress syndrome
  • Ix: ABCDE, ABG, CXR/CT bilateral infiltrates, PaO2/FiO2 severity, echo to exclude cardiogenic oedema; find trigger: sepsis, pneumonia, aspiration, pancreatitis.
  • Tx: ICU; lung-protective ventilation 6 mL/kg predicted body weight, appropriate PEEP, conservative fluids after resuscitation, treat cause.
  • Escalate: ICU immediately; prone ventilation if severe hypoxaemia, consider paralysis/ECMO referral if refractory.
  • Mimics: cardiogenic pulmonary oedema, pneumonia, pulmonary haemorrhage, PE, ILD flare, fluid overload.
  • Red flags: refractory hypoxaemia, rising lactate, shock, multi-organ failure, high ventilator pressures, severe acidosis.
Asthma
  • Ix: FeNO/eosinophils, spirometry with bronchodilator reversibility, PEF variability; acute attack: PEF %, sats, ABG if life-threatening.
  • Tx: acute: oxygen 94–98%, salbutamol 5 mg neb, ipratropium 500 micrograms neb if severe, prednisolone 40–50 mg OD 5 days.
  • Escalate: admit if PEF <75% after treatment, sats <92%, silent chest, exhaustion, cyanosis, poor response; ICU if rising CO2.
  • Mimics: COPD, inducible laryngeal obstruction, heart failure, PE, bronchiectasis, anxiety, anaphylaxis.
  • Red flags: silent chest, PEF <33%, SpO2 <92%, PaCO2 normal/high, confusion, hypotension, previous ICU asthma.
Asthma-COPD overlap
  • Ix: smoking/exposure history, variability symptoms, eosinophils/FeNO, spirometry obstruction; assess exacerbations and inhaler technique.
  • Tx: smoking cessation, vaccines, pulmonary rehab; use ICS-containing inhaler if asthma features/eosinophilia; add LABA/LAMA as needed.
  • Escalate: respiratory referral if diagnostic doubt, frequent exacerbations, hypoxia, eosinophilia high, severe obstruction, poor response.
  • Mimics: bronchiectasis, heart failure, obesity/deconditioning, PE, ILD, vocal cord dysfunction.
  • Red flags: recurrent admissions, oxygen sats low, hypercapnia, weight loss, haemoptysis, recurrent pneumonia.
Bronchiectasis
  • Ix: CXR then HRCT confirms; sputum culture incl. Pseudomonas/NTM, spirometry, immunoglobulins, ABPA screen, consider CF/PCD if suggestive.
  • Tx: airway clearance physiotherapy; exacerbation antibiotics 14 days guided by sputum; amoxicillin/doxycycline often used, local policy applies.
  • Escalate: respiratory referral for all confirmed; admit if hypoxia, sepsis, haemoptysis, Pseudomonas, failed oral antibiotics.
  • Mimics: COPD, asthma, TB, lung cancer, recurrent aspiration, cystic fibrosis.
  • Red flags: massive haemoptysis, recurrent pneumonia, weight loss, clubbing, chronic Pseudomonas, respiratory failure.
Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease
  • Ix: post-bronchodilator spirometry FEV1/FVC <0.7; CXR, FBC, BMI, sats, eosinophils; ABG if sats low or hypercapnia suspected.
  • Tx: smoking cessation, vaccines, pulmonary rehab; SABA/SAMA PRN, then LABA/LAMA; exacerbation: prednisolone 30 mg OD 5 days ± antibiotics if purulent.
  • Escalate: admit if hypoxia, cyanosis, confusion, acute hypercapnia/acidosis, pneumonia, poor response, severe comorbidity.
  • Mimics: asthma, bronchiectasis, heart failure, lung cancer, ILD, obesity hypoventilation.
  • Red flags: SpO2 <88–92%, pH <7.35 with raised CO2, haemoptysis, weight loss, new chest pain, cor pulmonale.
Empyema
  • Ix: CXR/USS/CT; diagnostic pleural aspiration: pus, pH <7.2, low glucose, high LDH, Gram stain/culture; blood cultures if septic.
  • Tx: IV antibiotics per local policy + image-guided chest drain; consider intrapleural tPA/DNase or surgery if poor drainage.
  • Escalate: same-day admission; respiratory/thoracic surgery if loculated, persistent sepsis, trapped lung, failed drain.
  • Mimics: parapneumonic effusion, malignant effusion, lung abscess, TB pleuritis, PE.
  • Red flags: sepsis, persistent fever, pleuritic pain, hypoxia, low pleural pH, loculations, failure to improve.
Interstitial lung disease
  • Ix: CXR, HRCT, spirometry with DLCO, sats/6MWT, autoimmune screen, exposure/drug history; MDT diagnosis.
  • Tx: remove exposure/offending drug; pulmonary rehab, oxygen if hypoxic; antifibrotics for IPF/progressive fibrosis specialist-led.
  • Escalate: respiratory ILD clinic; urgent/admit if acute worsening, hypoxia, suspected acute exacerbation, pulmonary hypertension.
  • Mimics: heart failure, COPD, bronchiectasis, sarcoidosis, hypersensitivity pneumonitis, malignancy.
  • Red flags: rapidly progressive dyspnoea, resting hypoxia, clubbing, crackles, weight loss, haemoptysis, new pulmonary hypertension.
Lower respiratory tract infection
  • Ix: clinical severity: sats, RR, HR, BP, temp; CRB65/CURB65 if pneumonia possible; CXR if focal signs, hypoxia or serious illness.
  • Tx: supportive if likely viral/bronchitis; antibiotics only if pneumonia, COPD exacerbation with purulence or high-risk bacterial illness.
  • Escalate: same-day/admit if CRB65 ≥1 with concern, hypoxia, sepsis, confusion, dehydration, frailty, immunosuppression.
  • Mimics: pneumonia, acute bronchitis, asthma/COPD exacerbation, PE, heart failure, lung cancer.
  • Red flags: SpO2 <94%, RR ≥30, hypotension, confusion, cyanosis, haemoptysis, pleuritic pain, rigors.
Lung abscess
  • Ix: CXR then CT chest; sputum/blood cultures, inflammatory markers; bronchoscopy if obstruction, cancer, foreign body or poor response suspected.
  • Tx: prolonged antibiotics with anaerobic cover, often co-amoxiclav or IV regimen per local policy; physiotherapy, nutrition, dental/aspiration risk management.
  • Escalate: admit if sepsis, hypoxia, large abscess, haemoptysis, immunocompromised, failed oral therapy; drainage/surgery if refractory.
  • Mimics: cavitating lung cancer, TB, fungal infection, septic emboli, GPA, infected bulla.
  • Red flags: haemoptysis, weight loss, night sweats, persistent fever, foul sputum, aspiration risk, cavitating lesion in smoker.
Lung cancer
  • Ix: urgent CXR; CT chest/abdomen with contrast; tissue via bronchoscopy/EBUS/CT biopsy; staging PET-CT/MRI brain if indicated.
  • Tx: MDT: surgery/SABR/radiotherapy/systemic therapy; stop smoking, manage symptoms, palliative care early when needed.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway for haemoptysis age ≥40 or CXR suspicious; urgent if SVCO, stridor, spinal cord compression, massive haemoptysis.
  • Mimics: pneumonia, TB, lung abscess, COPD, PE, ILD, benign nodules.
  • Red flags: haemoptysis, weight loss, persistent cough, hoarseness, clubbing, recurrent pneumonia same lobe, bone pain, hypercalcaemia.
Obstructive sleep apnoea
  • Ix: Epworth/STOP-Bang, BMI/neck circumference, BP; home sleep study or polysomnography with AHI/ODI.
  • Tx: weight loss, reduce alcohol/sedatives, sleep position; CPAP first-line if symptomatic moderate–severe; mandibular device if mild/CPAP intolerant.
  • Escalate: sleep clinic; urgent if sleepy driver, occupational driving risk, severe hypoxaemia, suspected obesity hypoventilation.
  • Mimics: insomnia, depression, hypothyroidism, narcolepsy, restless legs, alcohol/sedative use.
  • Red flags: daytime sleepiness while driving, witnessed apnoeas, pulmonary hypertension, resistant HTN, hypercapnia, morning headaches.
Occupational lung disease
  • Ix: full job/exposure history; spirometry, CXR/HRCT, serial PEF for occupational asthma, DLCO; consider IgE/precipitins depending exposure.
  • Tx: remove/reduce exposure, smoking cessation, respiratory PPE only as backup; treat phenotype: asthma/COPD/ILD/silicosis/asbestosis.
  • Escalate: occupational lung specialist/occupational health; suspected mesothelioma/lung cancer via cancer pathway.
  • Mimics: non-occupational asthma/COPD, sarcoidosis, IPF, TB, malignancy, hypersensitivity pneumonitis.
  • Red flags: symptoms improve on days off, asbestos/silica exposure, progressive dyspnoea, pleural plaques/effusion, weight loss, haemoptysis.
Pleural effusion
  • Ix: CXR + thoracic USS; diagnostic aspiration if unilateral/unknown; protein/LDH, pH, glucose, cytology, Gram stain/culture.
  • Tx: treat cause; therapeutic aspiration for dyspnoea; chest drain if empyema/complicated parapneumonic effusion; malignant effusion: IPC/pleurodesis.
  • Escalate: respiratory referral for unexplained unilateral effusion; admit if hypoxia, sepsis, large symptomatic effusion, suspected empyema.
  • Mimics: consolidation, raised hemidiaphragm, pleural thickening, atelectasis, ascites, heart failure.
  • Red flags: unilateral large effusion, bloodstained fluid, weight loss, fever/sepsis, pleural pH <7.2, mediastinal shift.
Pneumonia
  • Ix: sats, CRB65/CURB65, CXR, FBC/U&E/CRP; blood/sputum cultures if admitted; ABG if hypoxic or severe.
  • Tx: low-severity CAP: amoxicillin 500 mg TDS 5 days; doxycycline or clarithromycin if penicillin allergy/atypical; local policy applies.
  • Escalate: admit if CURB65 ≥2, hypoxia, sepsis, confusion, inability to maintain intake, frailty or significant comorbidity.
  • Mimics: PE, heart failure, COPD/asthma exacerbation, lung cancer, TB, pulmonary oedema.
  • Red flags: RR ≥30, SBP <90/DBP ≤60, confusion, SpO2 <94%, multilobar disease, pleural effusion, immunosuppression.
Pneumothorax
  • Ix: ABCDE; tension is clinical; CXR if stable, CT if uncertain/complex; classify primary vs secondary and symptom burden.
  • Tx: tension: immediate needle decompression then chest drain; stable cases: conservative/aspiration/chest drain depending size, symptoms and reserve.
  • Escalate: same-day respiratory/ED; admit secondary pneumothorax, bilateral, breathless, hypoxia, large, persistent air leak.
  • Mimics: asthma, PE, ACS, pneumonia, rib fracture, panic attack.
  • Red flags: hypotension, tracheal deviation, distended neck veins, severe dyspnoea, cyanosis, bilateral pneumothorax, underlying COPD/ILD.
Respiratory arrest
  • Ix: shout for help; check responsiveness, airway, breathing, pulse; ABG/VBG after ventilation started; identify reversible cause.
  • Tx: high-flow oxygen, airway manoeuvres, suction, BVM ventilation, airway adjuncts; if pulseless start ALS, because oxygen is not decorative.
  • Escalate: crash team/anaesthetics/ICU immediately; intubation/ventilation if not rapidly reversible.
  • Mimics: cardiac arrest, seizure, opioid overdose, severe hypoglycaemia, syncope with agonal breathing.
  • Red flags: absent/ineffective breathing, cyanosis, silent chest, exhaustion, reduced GCS, bradycardia, peri-arrest rhythm.
Respiratory failure
  • Ix: ABG defines type 1 hypoxaemia vs type 2 hypercapnia; CXR, ECG, U&E/FBC/CRP, cultures if infection, consider CTPA/echo.
  • Tx: oxygen target 94–98%; if CO2 retention risk target 88–92%; NIV for acute hypercapnic acidosis after initial COPD treatment.
  • Escalate: ICU/HDU if worsening acidosis, hypoxia despite oxygen, exhaustion, reduced GCS, shock, NIV failure.
  • Mimics: anxiety hyperventilation, metabolic acidosis compensation, PE, heart failure, pneumonia, COPD/asthma, neuromuscular disease.
  • Red flags: pH <7.35 with raised CO2, SpO2 low despite oxygen, drowsiness, silent chest, cyanosis, rising oxygen requirement.
Sarcoidosis
  • Ix: CXR bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy, CT if needed, spirometry/DLCO, calcium, LFT, ECG; biopsy if atypical or treatment needed.
  • Tx: observe if mild/asymptomatic; prednisolone specialist-led for significant lung, eye, cardiac, neuro, renal or hypercalcaemic disease.
  • Escalate: respiratory referral; urgent if eye pain/visual symptoms, arrhythmia/syncope, neuro signs, severe hypercalcaemia, progressive breathlessness.
  • Mimics: TB, lymphoma, lung cancer, hypersensitivity pneumonitis, fungal infection, pneumoconiosis.
  • Red flags: cardiac symptoms, uveitis, facial palsy, hypercalcaemia, progressive fibrosis, haemoptysis, weight loss/night sweats.

Gastrointestinal Problems

29 conditions
Anal fissure
  • Ix: clinical; inspect gently; avoid PR if severe pain; consider IBD/STI/malignancy if lateral, multiple, painless or non-healing.
  • Tx: fibre, fluids, stool softener e.g. macrogol 1–3 sachets OD; topical lidocaine short-term; GTN 0.4% ointment BD up to 8 weeks.
  • Escalate: colorectal referral if persistent >6–8 weeks, recurrent, atypical fissure, suspected Crohn’s, abscess or cancer concern.
  • Mimics: haemorrhoids, perianal abscess, Crohn’s fissure, anal cancer, herpes/STI ulcer.
  • Red flags: weight loss, rectal mass, persistent bleeding, lateral fissure, fever, immunosuppression, severe constant pain.
Appendicitis
  • Ix: FBC, CRP, U&E, LFT, amylase/lipase, urinalysis, pregnancy test; USS/CT depending age/sex/pregnancy and diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Tx: NBM, IV fluids, analgesia, antiemetic; IV antibiotics if perforation/sepsis; laparoscopic appendicectomy standard.
  • Escalate: same-day surgical admission for suspected appendicitis; urgent theatre if peritonitis, perforation, sepsis or abscess.
  • Mimics: gastroenteritis, mesenteric adenitis, UTI, renal colic, ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion/cyst, Crohn’s.
  • Red flags: guarding, rigidity, sepsis, persistent vomiting, pregnancy, elderly, immunosuppression, pain migration to RIF.
Coeliac disease
  • Ix: tTG-IgA + total IgA while eating gluten; EMA if needed; duodenal biopsy confirms in adults; check FBC, ferritin, folate, B12, LFT, vitamin D.
  • Tx: lifelong strict gluten-free diet; dietitian; replace deficiencies; calcium/vitamin D if needed; pneumococcal vaccine if hyposplenism.
  • Escalate: gastroenterology for diagnosis; urgent if severe malnutrition, refractory symptoms, weight loss, GI bleeding, suspected lymphoma.
  • Mimics: IBS, IBD, lactose intolerance, chronic pancreatitis, microscopic colitis, giardiasis.
  • Red flags: anaemia, weight loss, persistent diarrhoea, low albumin, night sweats, abdominal mass, symptoms despite strict gluten-free diet.
Colorectal cancer
  • Ix: FIT where appropriate, FBC/ferritin; colonoscopy + biopsy; CT colonography if unsuitable; CT chest/abdomen/pelvis for staging.
  • Tx: MDT: surgery ± chemo/radiotherapy depending site/stage; treat obstruction/bleeding/anaemia urgently.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway for rectal mass, iron-deficiency anaemia, persistent rectal bleeding/change in bowel habit, positive FIT.
  • Mimics: haemorrhoids, diverticular disease, IBD, IBS, fissure, infectious colitis.
  • Red flags: rectal mass, weight loss, IDA, persistent altered bowel habit, tenesmus, obstructive symptoms, unexplained abdominal mass.
Constipation
  • Ix: clinical; PR if red flags/faecal impaction; FBC, U&E, calcium, TFT if persistent; colon investigation if cancer features.
  • Tx: fibre/fluids/mobility; macrogol 1–3 sachets OD; add stimulant e.g. senna 7.5– 15 mg nocte or bisacodyl 5–10 mg nocte.
  • Escalate: urgent if obstruction, severe pain/distension, vomiting, GI bleeding, new constipation with cancer red flags.
  • Mimics: bowel obstruction, IBS-C, colorectal cancer, hypothyroidism, hypercalcaemia, opioid effect, Parkinson’s.
  • Red flags: weight loss, rectal bleeding, IDA, palpable mass, new onset age >50, vomiting, absolute constipation.
Diverticular disease / diverticulitis
  • Ix: FBC, CRP, U&E, LFT; CT abdomen/pelvis if suspected acute diverticulitis, severe disease or diagnostic doubt.
  • Tx: uncomplicated mild: fluids/analgesia ± no antibiotics if systemically well; if antibiotics: co-amoxiclav 625 mg TDS 5 days, local policy applies.
  • Escalate: admit if sepsis, peritonitis, abscess, perforation, obstruction, immunosuppression, failed oral treatment.
  • Mimics: colorectal cancer, IBD, appendicitis, gastroenteritis, ischaemic colitis, renal colic, gynae pathology.
  • Red flags: guarding, fever/sepsis, persistent vomiting, rectal bleeding, weight loss, mass, pneumaturia/faecaluria.
Gastric cancer
  • Ix: urgent upper GI endoscopy + biopsy; FBC/ferritin, LFT; CT chest/abdomen/pelvis ± staging laparoscopy.
  • Tx: MDT: surgery ± perioperative chemo; palliative stent/radiotherapy/nutrition support where advanced.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway for dysphagia, weight loss with upper abdominal pain/reflux/dyspepsia, persistent vomiting, IDA.
  • Mimics: peptic ulcer disease, gastritis, GORD, gallbladder disease, pancreatic cancer, functional dyspepsia.
  • Red flags: dysphagia, weight loss, early satiety, vomiting, melaena, IDA, epigastric mass, Virchow node.
GI bleeding
  • Ix: ABCDE, stool/vomit description, PR, FBC, U&E, LFT, coag, group & save/crossmatch; Glasgow-Blatchford for UGIB; CT angiography if major LGIB.
  • Tx: resuscitate, IV access, transfuse if unstable/major bleed; stop anticoagulants if appropriate; variceal suspected: terlipressin + IV antibiotics.
  • Escalate: urgent endoscopy if unstable/severe UGIB; colonoscopy/CTA/interventional radiology for major LGIB; ICU if shock.
  • Mimics: iron/bismuth stool, haemoptysis swallowed, epistaxis, beetroot, vaginal bleeding.
  • Red flags: haematemesis, melaena, shock, syncope, anticoagulation, liver disease, severe anaemia, ongoing brisk bleeding.
GI perforation
  • Ix: ABCDE, lactate/VBG, FBC/U&E/LFT/coag/group & save; erect CXR may show free air; CT abdomen/pelvis usually confirms.
  • Tx: NBM, IV fluids, broad-spectrum IV antibiotics, analgesia, NG tube if vomiting/obstruction; urgent surgical source control.
  • Escalate: immediate surgical admission; ICU if sepsis, shock, high lactate, frailty or multi-organ dysfunction.
  • Mimics: pancreatitis, biliary colic/cholecystitis, renal colic, MI, AAA, severe gastroenteritis.
  • Red flags: sudden severe pain, rigid abdomen, shoulder tip pain, fever, tachycardia, hypotension, free air.
GORD / Barrett oesophagus
  • Ix: clinical if typical; endoscopy if alarm symptoms, age-risk dyspepsia, refractory symptoms; Barrett diagnosed by endoscopy + biopsy.
  • Tx: lifestyle, weight loss; omeprazole 20 mg OD 4–8 weeks then lowest effective dose; alginate PRN.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway for dysphagia, weight loss, haematemesis/melaena, persistent vomiting, IDA; gastro if refractory/Barrett.
  • Mimics: ACS, peptic ulcer, gallstones, oesophageal cancer, functional dyspepsia, eosinophilic oesophagitis.
  • Red flags: dysphagia, odynophagia, weight loss, GI bleeding, persistent vomiting, anaemia, new symptoms age >55.
Haemorrhoids
  • Ix: inspect + PR; proctoscopy if available; exclude cancer/IBD if bleeding pattern atypical or red flags.
  • Tx: fibre, fluids, avoid straining, stool softener; topical analgesic/steroid short course; rubber band ligation for persistent grade I–III.
  • Escalate: colorectal referral if persistent bleeding, prolapse, thrombosed external haemorrhoid severe pain, anaemia or cancer concern.
  • Mimics: fissure, rectal cancer, IBD, rectal prolapse, perianal abscess, warts.
  • Red flags: weight loss, altered bowel habit, IDA, rectal mass, persistent bleeding, age-risk new symptoms.
Hernias
  • Ix: clinical standing/cough impulse; USS if occult groin hernia uncertain; CT if obstruction/complex abdominal wall hernia.
  • Tx: elective repair if symptomatic/large/risk; watchful waiting sometimes for minimally symptomatic inguinal hernia.
  • Escalate: same-day surgery/ED if irreducible, obstructed or strangulated; femoral hernias need prompt surgical opinion.
  • Mimics: lymph nodes, lipoma, varicocele, hydrocele, saphena varix, abscess, testicular torsion.
  • Red flags: severe pain, irreducible tender lump, vomiting, distension, skin erythema, fever, obstructive symptoms.
Hiatus hernia
  • Ix: clinical reflux pattern; endoscopy if alarm/refractory; barium swallow/manometry/pH studies if surgery considered.
  • Tx: lifestyle + PPI e.g. omeprazole 20 mg OD; weight loss; avoid late meals; treat associated GORD.
  • Escalate: gastro/surgery if para-oesophageal hernia, volvulus risk, refractory symptoms, bleeding/anaemia, dysphagia.
  • Mimics: GORD without hernia, ACS, peptic ulcer, oesophageal cancer, biliary colic.
  • Red flags: dysphagia, vomiting, chest/epigastric severe pain, GI bleeding, IDA, weight loss.
Infectious colitis including C. difficile
  • Ix: stool culture/PCR, C. difficile toxin/PCR if recent antibiotics/hospital/PPI risk; FBC/U&E/CRP; CT if severe pain, distension or toxic features.
  • Tx: fluids/electrolytes; C. difficile: stop culprit antibiotics if possible, oral vancomycin 125 mg QDS 10 days or fidaxomicin per local policy.
  • Escalate: admit if severe dehydration, sepsis, AKI, bloody diarrhoea, immunosuppression, suspected toxic megacolon.
  • Mimics: IBD flare, ischaemic colitis, diverticulitis, colorectal cancer, IBS, microscopic colitis.
  • Red flags: high fever, severe pain, distension, bloody diarrhoea, shock, WCC very high, rising creatinine, low albumin.
Infectious diarrhoea
  • Ix: assess hydration; stool culture if blood, fever, travel, outbreak, immunocompromised or persistent >7 days; U&E if dehydrated.
  • Tx: oral rehydration; avoid loperamide in bloody diarrhoea/high fever; antibiotics only selected severe/travel/pathogen-specific cases.
  • Escalate: same-day/admit if severe dehydration, sepsis, bloody diarrhoea, severe abdominal pain, elderly/frail, pregnancy or immunocompromised.
  • Mimics: IBD, IBS, coeliac disease, C. difficile, appendicitis, ischaemic colitis, thyrotoxicosis.
  • Red flags: blood, persistent fever, oliguria, confusion, severe pain, recent antibiotics, foreign travel, outbreak, symptoms >7–10 days.
Inflammatory bowel disease
  • Ix: FBC, CRP/ESR, U&E/LFT, ferritin, B12/folate, stool culture/C. diff, faecal calprotectin; colonoscopy + biopsy confirms.
  • Tx: UC mild–moderate: mesalazine; Crohn flare: steroids e.g. prednisolone 40 mg OD taper; biologics/immunomodulators specialist-led.
  • Escalate: urgent/admit if acute severe UC, toxic megacolon, obstruction, abscess, severe bleeding, sepsis, malnutrition.
  • Mimics: infectious colitis, IBS, coeliac disease, colorectal cancer, ischaemic colitis, diverticular disease.
  • Red flags: bloody diarrhoea, nocturnal stooling, weight loss, fever, tachycardia, anaemia, perianal sepsis, distension.
Intestinal ischaemia
  • Ix: high suspicion; lactate/VBG, FBC/U&E/coag, amylase, ECG for AF; CT angiography abdomen/pelvis urgently.
  • Tx: NBM, IV fluids, broad-spectrum antibiotics, analgesia; IV heparin if arterial/venous occlusion and no contraindication.
  • Escalate: immediate surgical/vascular review; ICU if shock/acidosis; revascularisation or laparotomy for infarction/peritonitis.
  • Mimics: gastroenteritis, pancreatitis, perforation, obstruction, diverticulitis, renal colic.
  • Red flags: pain out of proportion, AF, severe acidosis/lactate, bloody diarrhoea, peritonitis, shock, elderly vascular patient.
Intestinal obstruction / ileus
  • Ix: FBC/U&E/CRP, lactate, VBG, group & save; CT abdomen/pelvis identifies level/cause/ischaemia; AXR has limited use.
  • Tx: NBM, IV fluids, NG tube if vomiting/distension, analgesia/antiemetic, correct electrolytes; antibiotics if strangulation/perforation/sepsis.
  • Escalate: surgical admission; urgent theatre if closed-loop obstruction, strangulation, perforation, peritonitis, ischaemia.
  • Mimics: constipation, ileus, gastroenteritis, volvulus, pseudo-obstruction, DKA, pancreatitis.
  • Red flags: absolute constipation, faeculent vomiting, severe continuous pain, fever, tachycardia, guarding, high lactate.
Irritable bowel syndrome
  • Ix: positive diagnosis if recurrent abdominal pain with bowel change; FBC, CRP/ESR, coeliac serology, faecal calprotectin if IBD concern.
  • Tx: diet/lifestyle, low-FODMAP trial dietitian-led; loperamide for diarrhoea, macrogol for constipation, antispasmodic e.g. mebeverine 135 mg TDS.
  • Escalate: gastro referral if red flags, diagnostic doubt, refractory severe symptoms, abnormal inflammatory markers/calprotectin.
  • Mimics: IBD, coeliac disease, colorectal cancer, microscopic colitis, bile acid diarrhoea, endometriosis.
  • Red flags: rectal bleeding, weight loss, nocturnal symptoms, IDA, family history CRC/IBD/coeliac, abdominal mass, onset age >50.
Malabsorption
  • Ix: FBC, ferritin, B12/folate, vitamin D, calcium, LFT, albumin, INR; coeliac serology, stool elastase, faecal fat, stool OCP/giardia if indicated.
  • Tx: treat cause; replace deficiencies; pancreatic insufficiency: pancreatic enzyme replacement with meals; dietitian input.
  • Escalate: gastro referral if weight loss, severe deficiencies, persistent diarrhoea, low albumin, suspected pancreatic/IBD/malignancy cause.
  • Mimics: IBS-D, IBD, coeliac, chronic pancreatitis, bile acid diarrhoea, giardiasis, hyperthyroidism.
  • Red flags: weight loss, steatorrhoea, oedema, night diarrhoea, anaemia, bleeding, hypoalbuminaemia, severe vitamin deficiency.
Mallory-Weiss tear
  • Ix: suspect after retching/vomiting with haematemesis; FBC, U&E, clotting, group & save; endoscopy if ongoing/significant bleed.
  • Tx: ABC resus if bleeding; antiemetic, PPI often used; most stop spontaneously; endoscopic haemostasis if active bleeding.
  • Escalate: admission/endoscopy if haemodynamic instability, ongoing haematemesis, anaemia, anticoagulation, liver disease.
  • Mimics: peptic ulcer bleed, varices, oesophagitis, gastric cancer, Boerhaave syndrome.
  • Red flags: shock, severe chest pain after vomiting, surgical emphysema, melaena, known cirrhosis, persistent bleeding.
Oesophageal cancer
  • Ix: urgent upper GI endoscopy + biopsy; CT chest/abdomen/pelvis, PET-CT/EUS for staging; assess nutrition.
  • Tx: MDT: oesophagectomy, chemoradiotherapy, stent/laser/palliation depending stage and fitness.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway for dysphagia at any age; urgent if complete obstruction, aspiration, severe malnutrition, haematemesis.
  • Mimics: GORD/stricture, achalasia, oesophagitis, globus, pharyngeal pouch, benign peptic stricture.
  • Red flags: progressive dysphagia solids then liquids, weight loss, odynophagia, hoarseness, haematemesis, smoking/alcohol history.
Peptic ulcer disease / gastritis
  • Ix: H. pylori stool antigen/urea breath test; FBC if bleeding; endoscopy if alarm symptoms, older age risk or refractory symptoms.
  • Tx: stop NSAIDs if possible; PPI e.g. omeprazole 20 mg OD 4–8 weeks; H. pylori eradication PPI + amoxicillin + clarithromycin 7 days, local policy.
  • Escalate: urgent endoscopy/admission if GI bleed, perforation, obstruction, severe anaemia, weight loss, persistent vomiting.
  • Mimics: GORD, gastric cancer, biliary disease, pancreatitis, ACS, functional dyspepsia.
  • Red flags: melaena, haematemesis, sudden severe pain, weight loss, dysphagia, persistent vomiting, IDA.
Perianal abscesses and fistulae
  • Ix: clinical; PR if tolerated; MRI pelvis/EUA for fistula or complex/recurrent disease; screen Crohn’s if multiple/recurrent.
  • Tx: abscess: urgent incision and drainage; antibiotics only if cellulitis, sepsis, immunocompromised, diabetes or valvular risk.
  • Escalate: same-day colorectal/surgical for abscess; urgent if sepsis, severe pain, nec fasc concern, immunosuppression.
  • Mimics: fissure, thrombosed haemorrhoid, pilonidal abscess, hidradenitis, Bartholin abscess, anal cancer.
  • Red flags: fever, spreading cellulitis, severe constant pain, crepitus, diabetes, immunosuppression, recurrent fistulae.
Peritonitis
  • Ix: ABCDE; FBC/U&E/LFT/amylase/lactate/VBG, blood cultures, group & save; CT abdomen/pelvis if stable, but don’t delay surgery in extremis.
  • Tx: NBM, IV fluids, broad-spectrum IV antibiotics, analgesia, NG tube/urinary catheter as needed; urgent source control.
  • Escalate: immediate surgical review; ICU if sepsis, shock, high lactate, organ dysfunction.
  • Mimics: pancreatitis, severe gastroenteritis, renal colic, MI, DKA, ruptured AAA.
  • Red flags: guarding, rigidity, rebound, absent bowel sounds, fever, tachycardia, hypotension, confusion.
Rectal prolapse
  • Ix: clinical, examine straining if needed; PR; assess constipation/incontinence; colonoscopy if bleeding/change in bowel habit/red flags.
  • Tx: reduce if acute viable; stool softeners/fibre; pelvic floor support; definitive rectopexy/perineal repair if full-thickness/symptomatic.
  • Escalate: urgent surgery if irreducible, strangulated, ulcerated, ischaemic or severe bleeding; routine colorectal for recurrent prolapse.
  • Mimics: prolapsing haemorrhoids, rectal polyp/cancer, intussusception, mucosal prolapse.
  • Red flags: dusky/black mucosa, severe pain, irreducible prolapse, bleeding, weight loss, rectal mass.
Toxic megacolon
  • Ix: suspected severe colitis + systemic toxicity; AXR/CT shows colonic dilatation >6 cm; FBC/U&E/CRP, cultures, stool C. diff.
  • Tx: NBM, IV fluids, correct electrolytes, VTE prophylaxis; IV hydrocortisone 100 mg QDS if IBD flare; antibiotics if infection/sepsis.
  • Escalate: emergency gastro + colorectal surgery; colectomy if perforation, uncontrolled bleeding or no improvement within 48–72 h.
  • Mimics: obstruction, ileus, severe infectious colitis, pseudo-obstruction, volvulus.
  • Red flags: fever, tachycardia, anaemia, shock, peritonitis, colonic diameter >6 cm, rising CRP/WCC, perforation.
Variceal disease
  • Ix: ABCDE, FBC/U&E/LFT/coag, group & save, lactate; urgent endoscopy; assess cirrhosis severity and encephalopathy.
  • Tx: resuscitate; terlipressin 2 mg IV then 1–2 mg 4–6 hourly + IV antibiotics e.g. ceftriaxone per local policy; endoscopic band ligation.
  • Escalate: immediate admission; ICU if shock, airway risk, massive bleed; TIPS if uncontrolled/recurrent bleeding.
  • Mimics: peptic ulcer bleed, Mallory-Weiss tear, gastritis, gastric cancer, swallowed epistaxis.
  • Red flags: haematemesis in cirrhosis, melaena, shock, confusion, ascites, coagulopathy, active alcohol-related liver disease.
Volvulus
  • Ix: AXR may show sigmoid “coffee bean”; CT abdomen/pelvis confirms site/ischaemia/perforation; FBC/U&E/lactate/VBG.
  • Tx: sigmoid volvulus stable: flexible sigmoidoscopic decompression + flatus tube; caecal volvulus usually surgery; resus/NG if obstructed.
  • Escalate: emergency surgery if peritonitis, perforation, ischaemia, failed endoscopic decompression, caecal volvulus.
  • Mimics: bowel obstruction, pseudo-obstruction, severe constipation, ileus, toxic megacolon.
  • Red flags: severe pain, fever, tachycardia, peritonitis, high lactate, shock, marked distension, obstipation.

Genetic Disorders

7 conditions
Cystic fibrosis
  • Ix: sweat chloride + CFTR genotyping; sputum culture, spirometry, CXR/CT if indicated; screen CFRD, pancreatic insufficiency, liver disease, osteoporosis.
  • Tx: specialist CF MDT; airway clearance, exercise, pancreatic enzymes, fatsoluble vitamins, nebulised mucolytics/antibiotics; CFTR modulators by genotype.
  • Escalate: CF centre for all; admit if pulmonary exacerbation, hypoxia, haemoptysis, pneumothorax, DIOS, sepsis or rapid FEV1 decline.
  • Mimics: primary ciliary dyskinesia, bronchiectasis, asthma, recurrent aspiration, immunodeficiency, chronic infection.
  • Red flags: failure to thrive, recurrent chest infections, malabsorption/steatorrhoea, clubbing, haemoptysis, CF diabetes, infertility in males.
Down syndrome
  • Ix: antenatal screening/NIPT then diagnostic CVS/amniocentesis; postnatal karyotype; echo, thyroid, hearing, vision, growth and sleep apnoea screening.
  • Tx: MDT support; annual health checks, vaccines, treat cardiac/GI/thyroid/hearing/vision issues; speech, physio, educational and social support.
  • Escalate: cardiology if congenital heart disease; urgent if leukaemia signs, atlantoaxial instability, regression/dementia, severe OSA.
  • Mimics: other trisomies, congenital hypothyroidism, fetal alcohol syndrome, other syndromic learning disability.
  • Red flags: cyanosis/HF, neck pain or neuro signs, bruising/pallor, recurrent infections, dysphagia/aspiration, loss of skills.
Ehlers-Danlos syndrome
  • Ix: clinical Beighton score + 2017 criteria; genetic testing if vascular/classical/rare EDS suspected; echo/vascular imaging if high-risk phenotype.
  • Tx: physio, joint protection, pacing, analgesia, treat dysautonomia/GI symptoms; avoid unnecessary surgery and repeated joint manipulation.
  • Escalate: genetics if vascular features, severe skin fragility, organ rupture, arterial event, strong family history or diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Mimics: hypermobility spectrum disorder, Marfan syndrome, Loeys-Dietz, osteogenesis imperfecta, fibromyalgia, inflammatory arthritis.
  • Red flags: sudden chest/abdo pain, arterial rupture/dissection, bowel/uterine rupture, translucent skin, easy severe bruising, pneumothorax.
Huntington disease
  • Ix: clinical chorea/cognitive/psychiatric features + family history; confirm with HTT CAG repeat genetic test after counselling; MRI to exclude mimics.
  • Tx: MDT neurology/genetics; chorea: tetrabenazine or antipsychotic specialistled; treat depression/psychosis, nutrition, swallowing and safety.
  • Escalate: genetics before predictive testing; urgent mental health if suicidality, psychosis, aggression, safeguarding or severe carer strain.
  • Mimics: tardive dyskinesia, Wilson disease, drug-induced chorea, thyrotoxicosis, SLE/APLS, CJD, frontotemporal dementia.
  • Red flags: suicidal ideation, dysphagia/aspiration, rapid weight loss, falls, psychosis, loss of capacity, family planning implications.
Klinefelter syndrome
  • Ix: karyotype confirms 47,XXY; testosterone low with raised LH/FSH; semen analysis, DEXA, metabolic screen, breast exam.
  • Tx: testosterone replacement from puberty/adulthood if hypogonadal; fertility referral for sperm retrieval/ICSI; speech/learning/psych support.
  • Escalate: endocrinology/andrology for diagnosis, delayed puberty, infertility, hypogonadism; urgent if breast lump or VTE symptoms.
  • Mimics: constitutional delayed puberty, primary testicular failure, hypogonadotropic hypogonadism, androgen insensitivity, obesity-related low testosterone.
  • Red flags: small firm testes, infertility, gynaecomastia, osteoporosis, diabetes/metabolic syndrome, VTE, breast cancer risk.
Marfan syndrome
  • Ix: Ghent criteria, FBN1 testing if needed; echo for aortic root/valves, eye exam for lens dislocation, family screening.
  • Tx: beta-blocker or ARB e.g. losartan specialist-led to slow aortic dilatation; avoid high-intensity/contact sport; planned aortic surgery by threshold.
  • Escalate: cardiology/genetics for all; emergency if suspected aortic dissection, rapid aortic growth, severe AR or pregnancy with aortic dilatation.
  • Mimics: Loeys-Dietz, vascular EDS, MASS phenotype, homocystinuria, familial thoracic aortic aneurysm.
  • Red flags: tearing chest/back pain, syncope, new AR murmur, lens dislocation, pneumothorax, family sudden death, aortic root dilatation.
Turner syndrome
  • Ix: karyotype confirms; echo/cardiac MRI for bicuspid valve/coarctation/aorta, renal USS, TFT, hearing, BP, coeliac and metabolic screening.
  • Tx: childhood growth hormone; oestrogen induction then cyclical oestrogen/progestogen HRT until usual menopause age; fertility counselling.
  • Escalate: endocrinology/cardiology/genetics; urgent if chest/back pain, severe hypertension, pregnancy with aortopathy, heart failure symptoms.
  • Mimics: constitutional short stature, familial short stature, Noonan syndrome, hypogonadotropic hypogonadism, primary ovarian insufficiency.
  • Red flags: coarctation, bicuspid aortic valve, aortic dilatation/dissection, hypertension, primary amenorrhoea, hearing loss, hypothyroidism.

Lady Problems

Bacterial vaginosis
  • Ix: clinical thin grey/white discharge + fishy odour; vaginal pH >4.5; NAAT/microscopy if recurrent, pregnant, STI risk or diagnostic doubt.
  • Tx: metronidazole 400 mg BD 5–7 days; avoid alcohol; alternatives topical metronidazole/clindamycin; local policy applies.
  • Escalate: sexual health/gynae if recurrent, pregnancy complications, pelvic pain, post-procedure infection risk.
  • Mimics: candidiasis, trichomoniasis, chlamydia/gonorrhoea, retained tampon, physiological discharge.
  • Red flags: pelvic pain, fever, pregnancy, postcoital/intermenstrual bleeding, offensive discharge with systemic illness.
Cervical cancer
  • Ix: speculum exam; do not rely on screening if symptomatic; colposcopy + biopsy confirms; MRI pelvis/CT/PET-CT for staging.
  • Tx: MDT: excision for early disease; surgery, chemoradiotherapy or palliative treatment depending stage.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway for abnormal-looking cervix or symptoms suspicious for cervical cancer.
  • Mimics: cervical ectropion, cervicitis, polyps, trauma, endometrial cancer, vaginal/vulval cancer.
  • Red flags: postcoital bleeding, intermenstrual bleeding, postmenopausal bleeding, offensive discharge, pelvic pain, visible cervical lesion.
Cervical ectropion
  • Ix: speculum: red glandular epithelium around os; exclude pregnancy, STI/cervicitis, polyps and malignancy if bleeding.
  • Tx: reassure if asymptomatic; treat infection if present; cautery/cryotherapy if persistent troublesome discharge or bleeding.
  • Escalate: gynae/colposcopy if suspicious cervix, unexplained bleeding, abnormal screening, persistent symptoms despite benign exam.
  • Mimics: cervical cancer, cervicitis, cervical polyp, pregnancy-related cervical change, trauma.
  • Red flags: postcoital bleeding, IMB/PMB, contact bleeding with abnormal cervix, pelvic pain, offensive discharge.
Cervical screening / HPV
  • Ix: primary high-risk HPV test on cervical sample; cytology only if HPV positive; abnormal results triaged to repeat testing or colposcopy.
  • Tx: treat CIN by excision/ablation if indicated; HPV vaccination prevention; screening interval varies by UK nation/programme.
  • Escalate: colposcopy for high-grade cytology, persistent HPV, glandular abnormality or suspicious cervix.
  • Mimics: screening is not diagnostic testing for symptomatic cancer, because apparently this still needs saying.
  • Red flags: symptoms need assessment regardless of screening result: PCB, IMB, PMB, pelvic pain, visible lesion, offensive discharge.
Endometrial cancer
  • Ix: PMB pathway: urgent TVUSS for endometrial thickness; endometrial biopsy/hysteroscopy confirms; FBC if bleeding/anaemia.
  • Tx: MDT; usually hysterectomy + BSO ± lymph node staging; radiotherapy/chemo/hormonal therapy depending stage/fitness.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway for postmenopausal bleeding; urgent gynae if heavy bleeding, anaemia or unstable.
  • Mimics: atrophic vaginitis, endometrial polyp, fibroids, HRT bleeding, cervical cancer, anticoagulant bleeding.
  • Red flags: PMB, persistent IMB age >45, obesity/PCOS/tamoxifen, diabetes, Lynch syndrome, pelvic mass.
Endometriosis
  • Ix: clinical cyclical pain; pelvic exam; TVUSS for endometrioma/deep disease; laparoscopy if imaging negative but symptoms persist.
  • Tx: NSAID ± paracetamol; hormonal suppression: COCP or progestogen e.g. desogestrel 75 micrograms OD; GnRH analogues specialist-led.
  • Escalate: gynae if severe/refractory pain, infertility, endometrioma, bowel/bladder symptoms, suspected deep disease.
  • Mimics: IBS, PID, adenomyosis, fibroids, ovarian cysts, interstitial cystitis, ectopic pregnancy.
  • Red flags: acute severe pain, pregnancy, fever, weight loss, rectal bleeding, haematuria, obstructive bowel/urinary symptoms.
Fibroids
  • Ix: pelvic exam, FBC/ferritin, pregnancy test if relevant; TVUSS first-line; MRI if complex/surgical planning.
  • Tx: NSAIDs/tranexamic acid 1 g TDS–QDS during bleeding; LNG-IUS if cavity suitable; hormonal therapy, UAE or surgery if symptomatic.
  • Escalate: gynae if large uterus, pressure symptoms, infertility, anaemia, rapid growth, failed medical treatment.
  • Mimics: pregnancy, adenomyosis, ovarian mass, endometrial cancer, uterine sarcoma, pelvic kidney.
  • Red flags: PMB, rapidly enlarging mass, severe anaemia, urinary retention, hydronephrosis, persistent pelvic pain.
Genitourinary syndrome of menopause
  • Ix: clinical vulvovaginal dryness, dyspareunia, urinary urgency/recurrent UTI; examine vulva/vagina; exclude infection, LS and malignancy.
  • Tx: vaginal moisturisers/lubricants; local oestrogen e.g. estradiol 10 microgram pessary daily 2 weeks then twice weekly.
  • Escalate: gynae if bleeding, persistent symptoms despite treatment, breast cancer complexity, vulval dermatosis or suspected malignancy.
  • Mimics: candidiasis, lichen sclerosus, vulvodynia, recurrent UTI, contact dermatitis, vulval cancer.
  • Red flags: PMB, vulval ulcer/lump, bloodstained discharge, severe pain, recurrent unexplained UTI, non-healing fissures.
Lichen sclerosus
  • Ix: clinical porcelain-white vulval plaques, itch, fissures; biopsy if atypical, pigmented, ulcerated, treatment-resistant or cancer concern.
  • Tx: ultra-potent topical steroid: clobetasol propionate 0.05% ointment taper regimen; emollients, avoid irritants, lifelong self-exam.
  • Escalate: gynae/dermatology if diagnostic doubt, scarring, urinary/sexual dysfunction, poor response, suspected VIN/SCC.
  • Mimics: lichen planus, eczema, candidiasis, psoriasis, vulval intraepithelial neoplasia, vulval cancer.
  • Red flags: persistent ulcer, lump, induration, bleeding, architectural loss, new focal pain, non-response to steroid.
Menopause / perimenopause
  • Ix: clinical if age >45 with vasomotor symptoms/cycle change; FSH only if age 40–45 or suspected POI; exclude pregnancy/thyroid/anaemia if atypical.
  • Tx: lifestyle; HRT first-line for troublesome symptoms: transdermal estradiol + progestogen if uterus; vaginal oestrogen for GSM.
  • Escalate: menopause specialist if POI <40, complex cancer/VTE history, refractory symptoms, early menopause, unscheduled bleeding on HRT.
  • Mimics: pregnancy, thyroid disease, depression/anxiety, hyperprolactinaemia, medication effects, malignancy causing bleeding.
  • Red flags: PMB, heavy/prolonged bleeding, pelvic mass, VTE symptoms, migraine with new neuro signs, POI/amenorrhoea <40.
Ovarian cancer
  • Ix: CA125 + pelvic/abdominal USS; CT staging if suspicious; FBC/U&E/LFT; diagnosis by histology after MDT surgery/biopsy.
  • Tx: MDT cytoreductive surgery + platinum-based chemo; manage ascites, bowel obstruction, nutrition/palliation.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway if persistent bloating, early satiety, pelvic/abdo pain, urinary frequency, raised CA125 or suspicious USS.
  • Mimics: IBS, ovarian cyst, fibroids, ascites from liver disease, pregnancy, endometrioma, diverticular disease.
  • Red flags: persistent distension, weight loss, early satiety, ascites, pelvic mass, postmenopausal new IBS-type symptoms.
Ovarian cysts
  • Ix: pregnancy test, pelvic exam; TVUSS characterises simple/complex; CA125/RMI if postmenopausal or suspicious.
  • Tx: simple premenopausal cysts often observe/repeat USS; analgesia; surgery if large, persistent, symptomatic or suspicious.
  • Escalate: same-day if torsion/rupture/ectopic concern; gynae oncology pathway if complex solid areas, ascites, high CA125/RMI.
  • Mimics: ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion, endometrioma, tubo-ovarian abscess, fibroid, appendicitis.
  • Red flags: acute severe pain, collapse, shoulder-tip pain, fever, postmenopausal cyst, ascites, solid/complex USS features.
Ovarian torsion
  • Ix: pregnancy test, FBC/CRP, U&E; pelvic USS with Doppler may help but normal flow does not exclude torsion.
  • Tx: NBM, IV analgesia/antiemetic, fluids; urgent laparoscopy with detorsion ± cystectomy.
  • Escalate: immediate gynae/surgical admission; do not delay for perfect imaging, because ovaries dislike bureaucracy.
  • Mimics: ectopic pregnancy, appendicitis, renal colic, ruptured cyst, PID, gastroenteritis.
  • Red flags: sudden unilateral pelvic pain, vomiting, adnexal mass, pregnancy/ovulation induction, peritonism, fever or shock.
Pelvic inflammatory disease
  • Ix: clinical pelvic pain + cervical motion/adnexal tenderness; pregnancy test, NAAT chlamydia/gonorrhoea, HIV/syphilis screen; swabs do not rule out PID.
  • Tx: ceftriaxone 1 g IM stat + doxycycline 100 mg BD 14 days + metronidazole 400 mg BD 14 days; local sexual health policy applies.
  • Escalate: admit if pregnancy, severe pain, sepsis, tubo-ovarian abscess, vomiting, failed oral therapy, diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Mimics: ectopic pregnancy, appendicitis, ovarian torsion, endometriosis, UTI, ruptured cyst.
  • Red flags: positive pregnancy test, peritonism, fever/sepsis, adnexal mass, RUQ pain, severe vomiting, immunosuppression.
Polycystic ovary syndrome
  • Ix: Rotterdam: oligo/anovulation, hyperandrogenism, polycystic ovaries; exclude TFT, prolactin, CAH, Cushing; HbA1c/lipids/BP.
  • Tx: weight management; COCP for cycle control/hirsutism; metformin if insulin resistance/impaired glucose; fertility: letrozole specialist-led.
  • Escalate: endocrine/gynae if virilisation, severe hyperandrogenism, infertility, diagnostic uncertainty, prolonged amenorrhoea.
  • Mimics: CAH, androgen-secreting tumour, Cushing, hyperprolactinaemia, hypothyroidism, hypothalamic amenorrhoea.
  • Red flags: rapid virilisation, pelvic mass, amenorrhoea >3 months repeatedly, abnormal bleeding, diabetes/metabolic syndrome, infertility distress.
Termination of pregnancy
  • Ix: confirm intrauterine pregnancy/date with LMP ± USS; assess ectopic risk, safeguarding/coercion, rhesus status, STI screen, baseline Hb if indicated.
  • Tx: options: medical abortion with mifepristone then misoprostol, or surgical vacuum aspiration/D&E depending gestation/preference/service.
  • Escalate: urgent gynae/abortion service; ED if heavy bleeding, severe pain, fever, syncope, suspected ectopic or sepsis.
  • Mimics: miscarriage, ectopic pregnancy, molar pregnancy, coercion/sexual exploitation, pregnancy of unknown location.
  • Red flags: unilateral pain, shoulder-tip pain, collapse, soaking pads rapidly, fever/offensive discharge, safeguarding concern.
Vaginal prolapse
  • Ix: pelvic exam with Valsalva; assess urinary/bowel/sexual symptoms; urinalysis if LUTS; POP-Q staging in specialist care.
  • Tx: pelvic floor muscle training; vaginal pessary; topical oestrogen if atrophic; surgery if symptomatic and conservative treatment fails.
  • Escalate: urogynae if bothersome prolapse, ulceration, urinary retention, recurrent UTI, failed pessary, desire for surgery.
  • Mimics: rectal prolapse, cervical polyp/cancer, vaginal cyst, fibroid polyp, enterocele.
  • Red flags: vaginal bleeding, ulcerated prolapse, urinary retention, hydronephrosis, pelvic mass, postmenopausal new symptoms.
Vulval cancer
  • Ix: careful vulval/groin exam; biopsy of suspicious lesion confirms; MRI/CT/PET- CT for staging if indicated.
  • Tx: MDT: wide local excision/radical vulvectomy ± sentinel node/groin node surgery; radiotherapy/chemo for advanced disease.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway for unexplained vulval lump, ulceration, bleeding, persistent pain/itch or lesion not responding.
  • Mimics: lichen sclerosus, VIN, Bartholin cyst, herpes, eczema, candidiasis, vulval Crohn’s.
  • Red flags: persistent vulval itch/pain, ulcer, lump, bleeding, colour change, groin node, non-healing lesion especially with LS.

Pregnancy Problems

26 conditions
Antepartum haemorrhage
  • Ix: ABCDE, quantify bleed, maternal obs, abdominal exam, CTG if viable fetus, FBC/coag/group & save, Kleihauer if RhD-negative.
  • Tx: left lateral tilt, IV access, fluids/blood if unstable; do not do digital vaginal exam until placenta praevia excluded by USS.
  • Escalate: same-day obstetric admission for any APH; major haemorrhage protocol if heavy bleeding, shock, abruption or placenta/vasa praevia.
  • Mimics: cervical ectropion/polyp, cervicitis, trauma, labour show, miscarriage, urinary/rectal bleeding.
  • Red flags: maternal shock, abdominal pain, uterine tenderness, fetal distress, recurrent/heavy bleed, placenta praevia, reduced fetal movements.
Blood group incompatibility in pregnancy
  • Ix: booking ABO/RhD + antibody screen; repeat screen at 28 weeks; antibody titres/levels and fetal MCA Doppler if significant alloimmunisation.
  • Tx: anti-D immunoglobulin for unsensitised RhD-negative women after sensitising events and routine antenatal prophylaxis; dose/timing per local policy.
  • Escalate: fetal medicine if clinically significant antibodies, rising titres, previous affected pregnancy, hydrops or abnormal MCA Doppler.
  • Mimics: ABO incompatibility, hereditary spherocytosis, G6PD deficiency, neonatal sepsis, physiological jaundice.
  • Red flags: anti-D/anti-c/anti-K antibodies, fetal anaemia, hydrops, reduced movements, previous neonatal exchange transfusion/stillbirth.
Chorioamnionitis
  • Ix: maternal temp, HR, uterine tenderness, liquor smell/colour, fetal tachycardia; FBC/CRP, cultures if septic; CTG if viable.
  • Tx: urgent IV broad-spectrum antibiotics per local policy; fluids/antipyretics; expedite birth if maternal/fetal compromise.
  • Escalate: immediate obstetric + neonatal review; sepsis pathway if fever, tachycardia, hypotension, lactate high or altered mental state.
  • Mimics: UTI/pyelonephritis, viral illness, epidural fever, appendicitis, placental abruption, COVID/flu.
  • Red flags: fetal tachycardia, offensive liquor, uterine tenderness, sepsis, reduced fetal movements, PPROM, maternal collapse.
Complications of labour
  • Ix: continuous CTG where indicated; confirm presentation by exam/USS; assess cord visibility/pulsation, station, progress, maternal obs.
  • Tx: cord prolapse: call help, elevate presenting part, knee-chest/left lateral, avoid cord handling, urgent birth; shoulder dystocia: McRoberts + suprapubic pressure.
  • Escalate: emergency obstetric team for cord prolapse, shoulder dystocia, obstructed labour, fetal compromise, malpresentation in labour.
  • Mimics: slow labour from analgesia, OP position, CPD, uterine rupture, placental abruption, fetal distress from other causes.
  • Red flags: pathological CTG, cord seen/felt, turtle sign, failed restitution, transverse lie, preterm labour, maternal exhaustion/sepsis.
Diabetes in pregnancy
  • Ix: OGTT for gestational diabetes risk; HbA1c pre-existing diabetes; home glucose monitoring; retinal/renal checks in pre-existing diabetes.
  • Tx: diet/exercise + glucose monitoring; metformin if targets unmet, insulin if needed; pre-existing diabetes usually insulin-led.
  • Escalate: joint antenatal-diabetes clinic; urgent if ketones, vomiting, DKA concern, reduced fetal movements, macrosomia/polyhydramnios, severe hypoglycaemia.
  • Mimics: steroid hyperglycaemia, undiagnosed T2DM, MODY, type 1 diabetes/LADA, Cushing syndrome.
  • Red flags: ketones, dehydration, fetal growth abnormality, polyhydramnios, preeclampsia, recurrent hypoglycaemia, previous stillbirth/macrosomia.
Ectopic pregnancy
  • Ix: pregnancy test, TVUSS, serial β-hCG if pregnancy of unknown location, FBC/group & save; assess haemodynamic stability.
  • Tx: unstable/ruptured: resus + emergency laparoscopy/laparotomy; stable selected: expectant, methotrexate or surgery per criteria.
  • Escalate: same-day early pregnancy/gynae for pain/bleeding with positive test; emergency if collapse, peritonism or shoulder-tip pain.
  • Mimics: miscarriage, corpus luteum cyst rupture, ovarian torsion, PID, appendicitis, renal colic.
  • Red flags: syncope, hypotension, shoulder-tip pain, guarding, unilateral pelvic pain, empty uterus with high β-hCG, adnexal mass.
Hyperemesis gravidarum
  • Ix: assess dehydration/weight loss; U&E, ketones if used locally, LFT, TFT if severe, urine culture; USS if molar/multiple pregnancy suspected.
  • Tx: oral/IV fluids; thiamine before dextrose; antiemetics e.g. cyclizine/prochlorperazine/promethazine, then metoclopramide/ondansetron if needed.
  • Escalate: admit if unable to keep fluids down, dehydration, electrolyte derangement, weight loss >5%, ketonuria per local use, comorbidity.
  • Mimics: gastroenteritis, UTI/pyelonephritis, hepatitis, pancreatitis, DKA, molar pregnancy, multiple pregnancy.
  • Red flags: confusion, Wernicke risk, severe hypokalaemia, haematemesis, abdominal pain, jaundice, fever, DKA symptoms.
Hypertension in pregnancy
  • Ix: BP repeat, urine protein ACR/PCR, FBC, U&E/creatinine, LFT, platelets; fetal growth scan/Dopplers if pre-eclampsia concern.
  • Tx: labetalol first-line; nifedipine/methyldopa alternatives; eclampsia/severe PE: magnesium sulfate 4 g IV load then 1 g/h.
  • Escalate: same-day obstetric assessment if BP ≥140/90 with proteinuria/symptoms; emergency if BP ≥160/110, eclampsia, HELLP or fetal compromise.
  • Mimics: chronic HTN, renal disease, white-coat HTN, migraine, AFLP, TTP/HUS, primary liver disease.
  • Red flags: headache, visual symptoms, RUQ/epigastric pain, vomiting, clonus, low platelets, raised ALT, AKI, reduced fetal movements.
Jaundice in pregnancy
  • Ix: LFT pattern, bilirubin, FBC, clotting, U&E, bile acids, hepatitis screen, haemolysis screen; USS liver/biliary tree if obstructive pattern.
  • Tx: cause-specific; obstetric cholestasis: ursodeoxycholic acid for itch may help symptoms; vitamin K if coagulopathy; delivery timing specialist-led.
  • Escalate: same-day obstetric/medical review for jaundice, abnormal clotting, severe LFT derangement, suspected AFLP/HELLP/hepatitis.
  • Mimics: gallstones, viral hepatitis, obstetric cholestasis, HELLP, acute fatty liver, haemolysis, drug-induced liver injury.
  • Red flags: hypoglycaemia, encephalopathy, coagulopathy, severe abdominal pain, hypertension/proteinuria, platelets low, bile acids very high.
Miscarriage and intrauterine death
  • Ix: pregnancy test, TVUSS, FBC/group & save; serial β-hCG if uncertain; IUFD: USS confirmation by trained clinician, CTG not diagnostic.
  • Tx: miscarriage: expectant, medical misoprostol or surgical management; give anti-D to RhD-negative women where indicated; IUFD care specialist-led.
  • Escalate: early pregnancy/gynae same-day if heavy bleeding, severe pain, infection, haemodynamic instability or ectopic not excluded.
  • Mimics: ectopic pregnancy, molar pregnancy, implantation bleeding, cervical pathology, UTI/rectal bleeding.
  • Red flags: soaking pads, syncope, fever/offensive discharge, severe unilateral pain, shoulder-tip pain, sepsis, coagulopathy in prolonged IUFD.
Multiple pregnancy
  • Ix: early USS for chorionicity/amnionicity; serial growth scans, Dopplers, BP/urine, anaemia screen; screen TTTS in monochorionic twins.
  • Tx: consultant-led antenatal care; folic acid/iron as indicated; aspirin if PE risk; birth timing and mode by chorionicity/presentation.
  • Escalate: fetal medicine if monochorionic complications, growth discordance, TTTS/TAPS, abnormal Dopplers, preterm labour.
  • Mimics: wrong dates, fibroids, polyhydramnios, large-for-gestational-age fetus.
  • Red flags: reduced movements, rapid abdominal distension, discordant growth, monochorionic twins, preterm contractions, hypertension.
Obesity in pregnancy
  • Ix: booking BMI; screen BP, urine, VTE risk, GDM risk, anaesthetic risk; growth scans if fundal height unreliable.
  • Tx: healthy diet/activity, aspirin if PE risk, thromboprophylaxis if risk score indicates; higher folic acid 5 mg OD preconception–12 weeks if BMI ≥30.
  • Escalate: consultant-led care if BMI ≥40 or significant comorbidity; anaesthetic review if high BMI or airway/epidural concern.
  • Mimics: oedema from PE, macrosomia, polyhydramnios, hypothyroidism, Cushing, fluid overload.
  • Red flags: VTE symptoms, hypertension/proteinuria, GDM, OSA, reduced fetal movements, wound infection risk, labour dystocia.
Placenta praevia
  • Ix: TVUSS accurately locates placenta; avoid digital vaginal exam; FBC/group & save if bleeding; MRI if accreta suspected.
  • Tx: pelvic rest advice if bleeding risk; planned caesarean if placenta covers/near os late pregnancy; steroids if preterm birth risk.
  • Escalate: immediate obstetric admission for bleeding; major haemorrhage protocol if heavy/recurrent bleed or maternal instability.
  • Mimics: placental abruption, vasa praevia, cervical lesion, labour show, uterine rupture.
  • Red flags: painless bright red bleeding, previous caesarean, low-lying placenta, accreta risk, malpresentation, fetal compromise.
Placental abruption
  • Ix: clinical diagnosis; CTG, maternal obs, abdominal exam, FBC/coag/fibrinogen/group & save, Kleihauer if RhD-negative; USS may miss it.
  • Tx: resuscitate, IV access, blood products if needed; expedite birth if fetal/maternal compromise; manage DIC.
  • Escalate: immediate obstetric emergency; senior obstetric/anaesthetic/neonatal review; ICU if shock, DIC or massive haemorrhage.
  • Mimics: placenta praevia, uterine rupture, labour, appendicitis, renal colic, trauma.
  • Red flags: painful bleeding, tense tender uterus, fetal distress/death, concealed bleeding, shock, hypertension, cocaine/trauma history.
Polyhydramnios / oligohydramnios
  • Ix: USS amniotic fluid index/deepest pool; assess fetal anatomy/growth, Dopplers; screen diabetes, infection, rupture of membranes where relevant.
  • Tx: treat cause; increased surveillance; severe symptomatic polyhydramnios may need amnioreduction/specialist therapy; oligohydramnios depends gestation/cause.
  • Escalate: fetal medicine if severe, early onset, fetal anomaly, growth restriction, abnormal Dopplers, suspected PPROM.
  • Mimics: wrong dates, multiple pregnancy, maternal habitus, ruptured membranes, fetal growth restriction.
  • Red flags: reduced fetal movements, PPROM, abnormal CTG/Dopplers, severe maternal breathlessness, malpresentation, cord prolapse risk.
Postpartum haemorrhage
  • Ix: call help; estimate blood loss, vitals, uterine tone, genital tract trauma, placenta completeness; FBC/coag/fibrinogen/group & crossmatch.
  • Tx: uterine massage, oxytocin 5 IU IV/IM, IV fluids/blood; TXA 1 g IV as early as possible; treat 4 Ts: tone, trauma, tissue, thrombin.
  • Escalate: major obstetric haemorrhage protocol; senior obstetric/anaesthetic/haematology; theatre, balloon tamponade, B-Lynch, IR or hysterectomy if refractory.
  • Mimics: normal lochia, concealed genital tract haematoma, uterine inversion, ruptured uterus, coagulopathy.
  • Red flags: ongoing bleeding, shock, atonic uterus, retained placenta, genital tract trauma, fibrinogen low, anticoagulants, sepsis.
Vasa praevia
  • Ix: antenatal diagnosis by TVUSS with colour Doppler; suspect with bleeding after ROM + fetal bradycardia; test blood as fetal if available.
  • Tx: planned caesarean before labour/ROM if diagnosed; if bleeding/ROM/fetal compromise: emergency caesarean.
  • Escalate: immediate obstetric emergency if suspected intrapartum; fetal medicine for antenatal diagnosis and delivery planning.
  • Mimics: placenta praevia, abruption, cervical bleeding, labour show.
  • Red flags: painless bleeding with ruptured membranes, fetal bradycardia, velamentous cord insertion, bilobed/succenturiate placenta, IVF pregnancy.
VTE in pregnancy and puerperium
  • Ix: suspected DVT: compression USS; suspected PE: ECG/CXR, CTPA or V/Q scan; do not use D-dimer to rule out casually in pregnancy.
  • Tx: start LMWH immediately while awaiting imaging unless contraindicated; enoxaparin weight-based, commonly 1 mg/kg BD for treatment.
  • Escalate: same-day obstetric/medical assessment for suspected VTE; ICU/thrombolysis discussion if massive PE with shock.
  • Mimics: pregnancy leg oedema, cellulitis, varicose veins, round ligament pain, pneumonia, asthma, panic, ACS.
  • Red flags: unilateral leg swelling/pain, pleuritic chest pain, haemoptysis, syncope, hypoxia, tachycardia, postpartum state, previous VTE/thrombophilia.
Benign prostatic hyperplasia
  • Ix: IPSS, DRE, urinalysis, PSA if cancer concern/shared decision, U&E/eGFR if renal risk; bladder scan/PVR if retention suspected.
  • Tx: lifestyle, reduce evening fluids/caffeine; tamsulosin 400 micrograms OD; add finasteride 5 mg OD if enlarged prostate/high progression risk.
  • Escalate: urology if retention, recurrent UTI, haematuria, renal impairment, stones, high PVR, failed meds; urgent if acute retention.
  • Mimics: prostate cancer, UTI, prostatitis, urethral stricture, bladder cancer, overactive bladder, neurogenic bladder.
  • Red flags: painless visible haematuria, abnormal DRE, bone pain, weight loss, recurrent retention, obstructive AKI.
Hydrocoele / varicocoele / epididymal cyst
  • Ix: scrotal exam; transillumination for hydrocoele; “bag of worms” varicocoele; scrotal USS if uncertain, painful, solid or testis not palpable.
  • Tx: reassure if asymptomatic; supportive underwear/analgesia; surgery/aspiration rarely for symptomatic hydrocoele, varicocoele or cyst.
  • Escalate: urology if symptomatic, large, fertility concern, adolescent varicocoele, uncertain diagnosis, testis obscured by hydrocoele.
  • Mimics: testicular cancer, inguinal hernia, epididymo-orchitis, torsion, spermatocele, scrotal oedema.
  • Red flags: intratesticular mass, acute pain, non-reducible hernia, sudden rightsided varicocoele, weight loss, haematuria.
Penile cancer
  • Ix: inspect glans/foreskin/shaft; palpate inguinal nodes; biopsy confirms; MRI/CT/PET-CT staging specialist-led.
  • Tx: MDT: penile-sparing excision/laser/topical therapy for selected early lesions; partial/total penectomy for invasive disease; nodal management.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway for persistent penile ulcer/lump/induration, unexplained bleeding, or symptoms not resolving after STI treatment.
  • Mimics: balanitis, genital warts, lichen sclerosus, herpes/syphilis ulcer, psoriasis, fixed drug eruption.
  • Red flags: painless ulcer, bleeding, foul discharge, phimosis hiding lesion, palpable inguinal nodes, non-healing “wart”.
Phimosis / paraphimosis
  • Ix: clinical; assess urinary obstruction, balanitis, lichen sclerosus, diabetes; avoid forceful retraction, because foreskins are not zip ties.
  • Tx: phimosis: hygiene, treat balanitis; topical steroid e.g. betamethasone 0.05% BD 4–8 weeks; paraphimosis: analgesia + urgent manual reduction.
  • Escalate: same-day urology/ED if paraphimosis, urinary retention, ischaemia; routine urology if pathological/recurrent phimosis or LS.
  • Mimics: balanitis, penile cancer, lichen sclerosus, genital warts, frenulum breve.
  • Red flags: swollen painful trapped foreskin, glans dusky/blue, urinary retention, recurrent infections, ulcer/mass under foreskin.
Prostate cancer
  • Ix: PSA after counselling, DRE; mpMRI before biopsy; transperineal/TRUS biopsy for diagnosis; staging CT/bone scan/PSMA PET if indicated.
  • Tx: MDT: active surveillance/watchful waiting, prostatectomy, radiotherapy ± androgen deprivation, systemic therapy if advanced.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway if abnormal DRE or raised age-adjusted PSA; urgent if cord compression, obstructive AKI, severe bone pain.
  • Mimics: BPH, prostatitis, UTI, urinary retention, urethral stricture, bladder cancer.
  • Red flags: hard irregular prostate, bone/back pain, weight loss, haematuria, LUTS with abnormal DRE, neurological symptoms/cord compression.
Prostatitis, epididymitis and orchitis
  • Ix: urinalysis/culture; STI NAAT if risk; DRE may show tender prostate, no prostatic massage; scrotal USS only if torsion/abscess/mass doubt.
  • Tx: acute prostatitis: antibiotics 14 days then review, e.g. ciprofloxacin/trimethoprim guided by local policy; epididymo-orchitis: STI/enteric regimen per policy.
  • Escalate: admit if sepsis, urinary retention, immunocompromised, severe pain/vomiting, abscess, failed oral antibiotics; torsion doubt = emergency exploration.
  • Mimics: testicular torsion, testicular cancer, UTI, renal colic, incarcerated hernia, mumps orchitis.
  • Red flags: sudden severe testicular pain, high-riding testis, absent cremasteric reflex, fever/sepsis, retention, scrotal abscess.
Testicular cancer
  • Ix: testicular exam; urgent scrotal USS; tumour markers AFP, β-hCG, LDH; staging CT chest/abdomen/pelvis after diagnosis.
  • Tx: radical inguinal orchidectomy; chemo/radiotherapy/surveillance depending histology and stage; sperm banking before treatment.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway for non-painful enlargement or change in shape/texture of testis; same-day if acute pain/torsion doubt.
  • Mimics: epididymal cyst, hydrocoele, varicocoele, epididymo-orchitis, torsion, hernia.
  • Red flags: hard intratesticular lump, testicular heaviness, gynaecomastia, back pain, weight loss, haemoptysis, supraclavicular nodes.
Testicular torsion
  • Ix: clinical emergency; sudden pain, high-riding/transverse testis, absent cremasteric reflex; Doppler USS only if it will not delay theatre.
  • Tx: immediate scrotal exploration + detorsion and bilateral orchidopexy; manual detorsion may be attempted if theatre delay, but surgery still needed.
  • Escalate: same-day emergency urology for any torsion suspicion; time-critical, ideally surgery within 6 hours.
  • Mimics: epididymo-orchitis, torsion of appendix testis, incarcerated hernia, renal colic, trauma, tumour haemorrhage.
  • Red flags: sudden severe unilateral pain, nausea/vomiting, high-riding testis, adolescent/young adult, pain after exercise/sleep, no urinary symptoms.

Brain Problems

26 conditions
Brain abscess
  • Ix: urgent MRI brain with contrast or CT if unavailable; FBC/CRP/blood cultures; avoid LP if raised ICP/mass effect.
  • Tx: neurosurgical + microbiology emergency; IV ceftriaxone/cefotaxime + metronidazole often used, local policy applies; drain if large/mass effect.
  • Escalate: same-day neurosurgery/ID; ICU if reduced GCS, seizures, sepsis, hydrocephalus or significant mass effect.
  • Mimics: brain tumour, encephalitis, meningitis, stroke, toxoplasmosis, subdural empyema.
  • Red flags: fever + focal neurology, seizure, papilloedema, immunosuppression, otitis/sinusitis/dental source, reduced consciousness.
Brain tumours
  • Ix: MRI brain with contrast; CT if acute; visual fields if pituitary/optic pathway; biopsy/resection for histology; staging if metastasis suspected.
  • Tx: dexamethasone for symptomatic oedema/mass effect; surgery/radiotherapy/chemo MDT-led; anticonvulsants only if seizures.
  • Escalate: urgent neuro-oncology/neurosurgery; same-day if raised ICP, new seizure, rapid neuro deficit, hydrocephalus or cord compression.
  • Mimics: abscess, stroke, MS, migraine, subdural, encephalitis, metastasis.
  • Red flags: morning headache/vomiting, papilloedema, new seizure, personality change, progressive focal deficit, cancer history.
Cerebral palsy / hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy
  • Ix: paeds context; developmental exam, neonatal history, MRI brain; assess hearing/vision, feeding, epilepsy, hip surveillance.
  • Tx: MDT: physio/OT/SALT, nutrition, spasticity management; baclofen oral or botulinum toxin specialist-led; treat seizures/reflux.
  • Escalate: paeds neurology/developmental paeds; urgent if status, aspiration, feeding failure, safeguarding concern, acute regression.
  • Mimics: genetic/metabolic disease, neuromuscular disorder, leukodystrophy, spinal pathology, non-accidental injury.
  • Red flags: loss of milestones, progressive weakness, bulbar dysfunction, seizures, abnormal head growth, unexplained bruising.
CVST
  • Ix: CT/MR venography; FBC/U&E/coag, pregnancy test if relevant, thrombophilia screen selected; check infection, malignancy, OCP/pregnancy risks.
  • Tx: therapeutic LMWH even if venous haemorrhage present, unless contraindicated; treat seizures/raised ICP and underlying cause.
  • Escalate: same-day stroke/neurology; ICU/neurosurgery if reduced GCS, large haemorrhage, herniation, uncontrolled seizures.
  • Mimics: migraine, SAH, IIH, meningitis, encephalitis, arterial stroke, tumour.
  • Red flags: new severe headache, papilloedema, seizure, focal deficit, pregnancy/puerperium, OCP, thrombophilia, cancer.
Delirium
  • Ix: acute fluctuating attention/cognition; 4AT/CAM; FBC/U&E/LFT/CRP/glucose/TFT if needed, urine, ECG, CXR/cultures guided by symptoms.
  • Tx: treat cause; reorientation, hydration, glasses/hearing aids, sleep; severe distress/risk: low-dose haloperidol 0.5 mg PO/IM if appropriate.
  • Escalate: admit/same-day if new delirium, sepsis, hypoxia, head injury, withdrawal, unsafe at home, rapid deterioration.
  • Mimics: dementia, depression, psychosis, stroke, non-convulsive seizures, intoxication/withdrawal.
  • Red flags: reduced GCS, fever, meningism, focal neurology, hypoxia, hypoglycaemia, head trauma, anticholinergic/opioid toxicity.
Dementias
  • Ix: collateral + cognitive screen; FBC/U&E/LFT/TFT/B12/folate/calcium/glucose; CT/MRI brain; assess mood, drugs, alcohol, function.
  • Tx: Alzheimer/Lewy body/Parkinson dementia: donepezil/rivastigmine/galantamine; memantine moderate–severe; vascular risk reduction.
  • Escalate: memory clinic; urgent if rapid progression, young onset, focal neurology, hallucinations with risk, safeguarding/driving concern.
  • Mimics: delirium, depression, hypothyroid, B12 deficiency, normal pressure hydrocephalus, subdural, alcohol-related cognitive disorder.
  • Red flags: rapid decline, seizures, gait + urinary symptoms, parkinsonism early, prominent visual hallucinations, personality change age <65.
Encephalitis
  • Ix: ABCDE; MRI brain, LP CSF after imaging if indicated, HSV/VZV PCR, blood cultures, HIV; EEG if seizures/diagnostic doubt.
  • Tx: start IV aciclovir 10 mg/kg TDS immediately if suspected HSV/VZV; add antibiotics if meningitis cannot be excluded.
  • Escalate: same-day admission/neurology/ID; ICU if seizures, reduced GCS, respiratory compromise, raised ICP.
  • Mimics: meningitis, delirium, sepsis, autoimmune encephalitis, intoxication, stroke, metabolic encephalopathy.
  • Red flags: fever + confusion, seizures, focal neurology, personality change, reduced GCS, immunosuppression, temporal lobe signs.
Epilepsy
  • Ix: witness history, ECG, glucose, FBC/U&E/Ca/Mg; MRI brain + EEG after first unprovoked seizure; classify seizure type.
  • Tx: safety/driving advice; focal seizures often lamotrigine or levetiracetam; generalised tonic-clonic often sodium valproate unless pregnancy potential.
  • Escalate: neurology first seizure clinic; urgent if status, recurrent seizures, pregnancy, injury, focal deficit, suspected CNS infection/tumour.
  • Mimics: syncope, PNES, hypoglycaemia, arrhythmia, TIA, migraine aura, panic attack.
  • Red flags: seizure >5 min, repeated without recovery, new focal deficit, fever/meningism, head injury, first seizure age >25, pregnancy.
Essential tremor
  • Ix: clinical action/postural tremor; check TFT, glucose, medication/caffeine/alcohol; neuro exam to exclude parkinsonism/cerebellar signs.
  • Tx: if functionally troublesome: propranolol 40 mg BD titrate if suitable; primidone alternative specialist/experienced use.
  • Escalate: neurology if diagnostic doubt, young onset, cerebellar signs, parkinsonism, rapid progression or treatment failure.
  • Mimics: Parkinson disease, hyperthyroidism, drug-induced tremor, cerebellar disease, anxiety, alcohol withdrawal, Wilson disease.
  • Red flags: rest tremor, rigidity/bradykinesia, ataxia, dystonia, weakness, sudden onset, systemic thyrotoxic signs.
Extradural haemorrhage
  • Ix: trauma ABCDE, GCS/pupils, CT head urgent; FBC/U&E/coag/group & save; Cspine imaging as indicated.
  • Tx: immobilise C-spine, reverse anticoagulation, manage raised ICP; definitive neurosurgical evacuation if significant.
  • Escalate: immediate neurosurgery/trauma team; ICU if reduced GCS, pupillary change, mass effect or polytrauma.
  • Mimics: subdural haemorrhage, SAH, concussion, stroke, intoxication, seizure.
  • Red flags: lucid interval, worsening headache/vomiting, falling GCS, unequal pupils, temporal bone fracture, seizure.
Idiopathic intracranial hypertension
  • Ix: fundoscopy/OCT, visual fields; MRI/MRV to exclude mass/CVST; LP opening pressure high with normal CSF.
  • Tx: weight loss; acetazolamide specialist-led; manage headache; urgent optic nerve sheath fenestration/CSF diversion if vision threatened.
  • Escalate: urgent ophthalmology/neurology if papilloedema, reduced vision, diplopia; emergency if rapid visual loss.
  • Mimics: CVST, brain tumour, malignant hypertension, medication-induced raised ICP, migraine, optic disc drusen.
  • Red flags: papilloedema, transient visual obscurations, VI palsy, pulsatile tinnitus, worsening vision, severe morning headache.
Meningitis
  • Ix: sepsis ABCDE; blood cultures, FBC/U&E/CRP/coag/glucose; LP if safe after antibiotics only if no delay; CT before LP if raised ICP/focal signs.
  • Tx: IV ceftriaxone 2 g BD immediately; add amoxicillin if Listeria risk; dexamethasone 10 mg IV QDS if suspected bacterial meningitis.
  • Escalate: immediate admission/ICU if shock, seizures, reduced GCS, purpura, respiratory compromise; notify public health if meningococcal.
  • Mimics: encephalitis, SAH, migraine, sepsis, viral illness, brain abscess.
  • Red flags: fever, neck stiffness, photophobia, non-blanching rash, confusion, seizure, hypotension, immunosuppression.
Motor neurone disease
  • Ix: progressive UMN + LMN signs; EMG/NCS; MRI brain/spine to exclude mimics; FVC/sniff nasal pressure, swallow/nutrition assessment.
  • Tx: riluzole 50 mg BD; MDT with respiratory, nutrition, SALT, physio, palliative care; NIV if respiratory weakness.
  • Escalate: neurology urgently if progressive weakness, bulbar symptoms, respiratory involvement; same-day if aspiration or respiratory failure.
  • Mimics: cervical myelopathy, MS, myasthenia, neuropathy, Kennedy disease, motor neuropathy, B12 deficiency.
  • Red flags: dysphagia, dysarthria, weight loss, orthopnoea, weak cough, fasciculations with weakness, rapid progression.
Multiple sclerosis
  • Ix: MRI brain/spine with dissemination in time/space; CSF oligoclonal bands; evoked potentials if needed; exclude B12, HIV, lupus, NMOSD/MOGAD.
  • Tx: relapse: methylprednisolone 500 mg PO/IV OD 5 days; disease-modifying therapy specialist-led; symptomatic rehab/spasticity/bladder care.
  • Escalate: neurology for suspected MS; urgent if optic neuritis severe, myelitis, brainstem signs, pregnancy relapse, diagnostic doubt.
  • Mimics: migraine, stroke, functional disorder, B12 deficiency, NMOSD, sarcoid, vasculitis, Lyme.
  • Red flags: bilateral optic neuritis, complete transverse myelitis, encephalopathy, systemic symptoms, progressive course from onset.
Parkinsonism
  • Ix: clinical bradykinesia plus rigidity/rest tremor; medication review; dopamine transporter scan if uncertain; screen cognition, mood, falls, autonomic symptoms.
  • Tx: levodopa first-line if motor symptoms affect quality of life; dopamine agonist/MAO-B inhibitor selected cases; physio/OT/SALT.
  • Escalate: Parkinson specialist; urgent if neuroleptic malignant syndrome, severe dysphagia/aspiration, falls, psychosis, autonomic failure.
  • Mimics: drug-induced parkinsonism, essential tremor, vascular parkinsonism, PSP, MSA, corticobasal syndrome, normal pressure hydrocephalus.
  • Red flags: early falls, gaze palsy, severe autonomic symptoms, rapid progression, poor levodopa response, cerebellar signs.
Primary headache disorders
  • Ix: clinical pattern; migraine unilateral throbbing ± aura/nausea; cluster severe orbital autonomic; tension bilateral pressing; neuro exam normal.
  • Tx: migraine: NSAID/paracetamol + triptan e.g. sumatriptan 50 mg; cluster: 100% oxygen 12–15 L/min + SC sumatriptan 6 mg.
  • Escalate: same-day/ED if thunderclap, neuro deficit, meningism, papilloedema, pregnancy/postpartum, cancer/immunosuppression.
  • Mimics: SAH, meningitis, GCA, CVST, brain tumour, glaucoma, sinusitis, medication-overuse headache.
  • Red flags: sudden onset, worst ever, exertional/sex trigger, new >50, progressive, fever, visual loss, jaw claudication, focal signs.
Raised intracranial pressure
  • Ix: urgent neuro exam, pupils/GCS, fundoscopy; CT/MRI brain; avoid LP until mass effect excluded; identify tumour, bleed, hydrocephalus, infection.
  • Tx: head up 30°, oxygenation, avoid hypotension; dexamethasone if tumour oedema; hypertonic saline/mannitol specialist-led if impending herniation.
  • Escalate: immediate neurosurgery/ICU if reduced GCS, pupillary signs, hydrocephalus, mass effect, herniation.
  • Mimics: migraine, meningitis, malignant hypertension, IIH, glaucoma, medication toxicity.
  • Red flags: morning headache/vomiting, papilloedema, sixth nerve palsy, reduced consciousness, Cushing response, unequal pupils.
Spinal cord compression
  • Ix: full neuro exam incl. PR tone if indicated; MRI whole spine same day; FBC/U&E/CRP/calcium, myeloma/prostate/lung/breast work-up if cancer suspected.
  • Tx: if malignant suspected: dexamethasone 16 mg PO/IV stat then daily; analgesia, immobilise if instability; surgery/radiotherapy specialist-led.
  • Escalate: same-day emergency oncology/neurosurgery/spinal team; timecritical before legs and bladder file their resignation.
  • Mimics: cauda equina, disc prolapse, epidural abscess, MS, transverse myelitis, stroke.
  • Red flags: new limb weakness, sensory level, bladder/bowel dysfunction, saddle anaesthesia, back pain with cancer, bilateral sciatica.
Spinal cord injury
  • Ix: ATLS ABCDE, full neuro exam/ASIA score, CT spine; MRI if neuro deficit/ligamentous injury; monitor respiratory function.
  • Tx: immobilise spine, maintain oxygenation/perfusion, analgesia, catheterise if retention; avoid hypotension; definitive spinal surgery/rehab.
  • Escalate: trauma/spinal surgery immediately; ICU if high cervical injury, respiratory compromise, neurogenic shock.
  • Mimics: stroke, functional weakness, peripheral nerve injury, spinal epidural haematoma, intoxication.
  • Red flags: priapism, diaphragmatic breathing, hypotension + bradycardia, sensory level, limb paralysis, loss of anal tone.
Status epilepticus
  • Ix: seizure >5 min or recurrent without recovery; ABCDE, glucose, VBG, U&E/Ca/Mg, AED levels, infection/toxicology if indicated.
  • Tx: protect airway; lorazepam 4 mg IV, repeat once after 10 min; if no IV: buccal midazolam 10 mg; then IV levetiracetam/phenytoin/valproate.
  • Escalate: 999/ALS/ICU if ongoing after benzodiazepines, airway compromise, pregnancy, head injury, CNS infection, metabolic cause.
  • Mimics: PNES, syncope with movements, rigors, dystonia, hypoglycaemia, arrhythmia.
  • Red flags: no recovery, hypoxia, hyperthermia, acidosis, trauma, pregnancy, meningism, focal deficit.
Stroke
  • Ix: FAST/NIHSS, capillary glucose, CT head immediately; CTA if thrombectomy candidate; ECG, FBC/U&E/coag/lipids/HbA1c.
  • Tx: thrombolysis if eligible within window; thrombectomy for large vessel occlusion; aspirin 300 mg once haemorrhage excluded if no thrombolysis.
  • Escalate: 999/hyperacute stroke unit for any suspected stroke; ICU/neurosurgery for reduced GCS, cerebellar stroke, large bleed.
  • Mimics: hypoglycaemia, seizure/Todd paresis, migraine, Bell palsy, functional disorder, tumour, subdural.
  • Red flags: sudden weakness/speech/vision loss, posterior circulation symptoms, reduced GCS, severe headache, anticoagulant use.
Subarachnoid haemorrhage
  • Ix: non-contrast CT head urgent; if CT negative and suspicion persists, LP xanthochromia after appropriate timing or CTA per pathway.
  • Tx: ABCDE, analgesia/antiemetic, BP control specialist-led; nimodipine 60 mg 4hourly; secure aneurysm by coiling/clipping.
  • Escalate: immediate neurosurgery/neurocritical care; ICU if reduced GCS, hydrocephalus, rebleed, vasospasm.
  • Mimics: migraine, meningitis, CVST, dissection, exertional headache, pituitary apoplexy.
  • Red flags: thunderclap headache, neck stiffness, vomiting, collapse, seizure, reduced GCS, sentinel headache.
Subdural haemorrhage
  • Ix: CT head; check anticoagulants/antiplatelets/coag/FBC; assess falls, alcohol, safeguarding, GCS/focal neurology.
  • Tx: reverse anticoagulation if indicated; neurosurgical evacuation if large/symptomatic; observe chronic small collections if stable.
  • Escalate: urgent neurosurgery if reduced GCS, focal deficit, midline shift, large haematoma, seizures.
  • Mimics: dementia, delirium, stroke, tumour, intoxication, normal pressure hydrocephalus.
  • Red flags: elderly/anticoagulated fall, fluctuating confusion, progressive headache, focal deficit, seizure, personality change.
TIA
  • Ix: clinical focal symptoms resolved; exclude hypoglycaemia; ECG, BP, FBC/U&E/lipids/HbA1c; brain imaging if diagnostic uncertainty; carotid imaging if anterior circulation.
  • Tx: aspirin 300 mg daily immediately unless contraindicated; high-intensity statin, BP/risk-factor control; anticoagulate AF after imaging/specialist plan.
  • Escalate: same-day specialist TIA/stroke assessment; 999 if symptoms ongoing or recurrent crescendo attacks.
  • Mimics: migraine aura, focal seizure, syncope, hypoglycaemia, vestibular disorder, functional symptoms.
  • Red flags: weakness/speech disturbance, AF, carotid bruit, recurrent same-day events, symptoms >1 h, anticoagulant use.
Vestibular schwannoma
  • Ix: audiometry for unilateral sensorineural hearing loss; MRI internal auditory meatus with contrast confirms.
  • Tx: observation with serial MRI if small/stable; stereotactic radiotherapy or microsurgery if growing/symptomatic.
  • Escalate: ENT/neurosurgery; urgent if brainstem compression, hydrocephalus, rapidly progressive neuro signs.
  • Mimics: Ménière disease, labyrinthitis, ototoxicity, acoustic trauma, MS, cerebellopontine angle meningioma.
  • Red flags: unilateral hearing loss/tinnitus, imbalance, facial numbness/weakness, cerebellar signs, headache/vomiting.
Wernicke encephalopathy / Korsakoff syndrome
  • Ix: clinical; don’t wait for thiamine levels; consider alcohol use, malnutrition, hyperemesis, bariatric surgery; check glucose/U&E/Mg/LFT.
  • Tx: suspected Wernicke: IV Pabrinex high-dose per local protocol before glucose/carbohydrate; replace Mg; treat withdrawal/nutrition.
  • Escalate: same-day admission if confusion, ataxia, eye signs, malnutrition, alcohol withdrawal, unsafe intake.
  • Mimics: intoxication, hepatic encephalopathy, hypoglycaemia, stroke, delirium, encephalitis, dementia.
  • Red flags: confusion, ophthalmoplegia/nystagmus, ataxia, hypothermia, hypotension, memory impairment, malnourished state.

Neuromuscular Problems

9 conditions
Autonomic neuropathy
  • Ix: lying/standing BP + HR, ECG, HbA1c, B12, TFT, U&E; screen bladder, bowel, erectile, sweating and gastroparesis symptoms.
  • Tx: treat cause; fluids/salt if safe, compression stockings; midodrine/fludrocortisone specialist-led; manage constipation, retention, ED.
  • Escalate: neurology/cardiology if syncope, severe postural hypotension, arrhythmia, rapid progression or diagnostic doubt.
  • Mimics: dehydration, adrenal insufficiency, drugs, Parkinson/MSA, vasovagal syncope, POTS, diabetic neuropathy.
  • Red flags: recurrent syncope, falls, urinary retention, severe BP swings, weight loss, rapid onset, bulbar/limb weakness.
Bell palsy
  • Ix: clinical LMN facial weakness; check forehead involvement, ear vesicles, parotid, neuro exam; glucose/HbA1c if diabetes risk.
  • Tx: within 72 h: prednisolone 50 mg OD 10 days; eye care: lubricants, tape eye shut at night if incomplete closure.
  • Escalate: same-day stroke/ENT/neuro if UMN signs, forehead spared, gradual onset, bilateral palsy, recurrent palsy, vesicles, severe pain.
  • Mimics: stroke, Ramsay Hunt, Lyme disease, parotid tumour, otitis media/mastoiditis, GBS, MS.
  • Red flags: limb weakness, dysarthria, diplopia, ataxia, bilateral facial palsy, cancer history, no improvement by 3 weeks.
Guillain-Barré / inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy
  • Ix: FVC/resp monitoring, ECG, FBC/U&E/LFT/CRP; LP shows high protein with low cells later; NCS/EMG confirms demyelination/axonal pattern.
  • Tx: admit; IVIg 0.4 g/kg/day for 5 days or plasma exchange; VTE prophylaxis, physio, analgesia, autonomic monitoring.
  • Escalate: ICU if FVC falling, bulbar weakness, weak cough, hypoxia, autonomic instability, rapid progression or aspiration risk.
  • Mimics: spinal cord compression, myasthenia, botulism, tick paralysis, porphyria, B12 deficiency, CIDP.
  • Red flags: ascending weakness, areflexia, facial/bulbar weakness, dysautonomia, respiratory weakness, progression over hours-days.
Muscular dystrophies
  • Ix: CK, LFT pattern, genetic testing, EMG ± muscle biopsy; ECG/echo, spirometry/FVC, sleep hypoventilation screen.
  • Tx: MDT neuromuscular care; physio, splints, respiratory support, cardiomyopathy treatment; DMD steroids specialist-led.
  • Escalate: neuromuscular genetics; urgent if respiratory failure, dysphagia/aspiration, arrhythmia, cardiomyopathy, rhabdomyolysis.
  • Mimics: inflammatory myopathy, metabolic myopathy, SMA, congenital myopathy, endocrine myopathy, drug-induced myopathy.
  • Red flags: Gowers sign, progressive proximal weakness, calf pseudohypertrophy, family history, raised CK, nocturnal hypoventilation.
Myasthenia gravis
  • Ix: fatigable weakness; AChR/MuSK antibodies, repetitive nerve stimulation/single-fibre EMG; CT thorax for thymoma; bedside FVC if severe.
  • Tx: pyridostigmine 30–60 mg QDS titrate; steroids/immunosuppression specialist-led; crisis: ICU, IVIg/plasma exchange, ventilatory support.
  • Escalate: same-day if bulbar symptoms, dyspnoea, weak cough, rapidly worsening weakness, infection, pregnancy or suspected crisis.
  • Mimics: Lambert-Eaton, botulism, stroke, motor neurone disease, thyroid eye disease, mitochondrial myopathy.
  • Red flags: dysphagia, dysarthria, nasal speech, ptosis with diplopia, respiratory weakness, sepsis trigger, thymoma symptoms.
Peripheral nerve injuries / palsies
  • Ix: map motor/sensory deficit to nerve/root; check pulses/compartment signs; Xray/USS/MRI if trauma/compression; NCS/EMG after ~2–3 weeks if persistent.
  • Tx: remove compression, splint, analgesia, physio/OT; neuropathic pain meds if needed; repair explored nerve laceration early.
  • Escalate: urgent plastics/ortho/neuro if open injury, progressive deficit, compartment syndrome, brachial plexus injury, cauda equina signs.
  • Mimics: radiculopathy, stroke, plexopathy, mononeuritis multiplex, functional weakness, MS.
  • Red flags: severe pain, vascular compromise, rapidly progressive weakness, trauma, bilateral deficits, bladder/bowel symptoms.
Peripheral neuropathy
  • Ix: pattern and tempo; FBC, U&E, LFT, HbA1c, B12/folate, TFT, ESR/CRP, SPEP; NCS/EMG if atypical or progressive.
  • Tx: treat cause; foot care; painful neuropathy: amitriptyline, duloxetine, gabapentin or pregabalin; replace B12 if deficient.
  • Escalate: neurology if motor, asymmetrical, rapid, autonomic, systemic features, young onset or unexplained progressive neuropathy.
  • Mimics: radiculopathy, myelopathy, MS, B12 deficiency, diabetic neuropathy, alcohol neuropathy, vasculitis.
  • Red flags: acute/subacute weakness, foot drop, weight loss, fever, autonomic symptoms, saddle anaesthesia, monoclonal protein.
Radiculopathies
  • Ix: dermatomal pain/paraesthesia, myotomal weakness/reflex loss; MRI if severe/progressive, cancer/infection signs or persistent disabling symptoms.
  • Tx: keep mobile, NSAID/paracetamol if safe, neuropathic agent if severe; physio; epidural/surgery if persistent or neuro deficit.
  • Escalate: same-day spinal/ED if cauda equina, cord compression, infection, malignancy, major trauma or progressive motor weakness.
  • Mimics: peripheral neuropathy, hip/shoulder disease, MSK pain, shingles, spinal stenosis, vascular claudication.
  • Red flags: saddle anaesthesia, urinary retention/incontinence, bilateral sciatica, fever, cancer history, night pain, progressive weakness.
Trigeminal neuralgia
  • Ix: clinical brief electric-shock facial pain in trigeminal distribution; neuro exam; MRI if atypical, young, sensory loss or poor response.
  • Tx: carbamazepine 100 mg BD, titrate to pain control; monitor FBC/LFT/U&E; specialist options include oxcarbazepine, surgery.
  • Escalate: neurology/pain if refractory, intolerant meds, atypical features; urgent if sensory loss, bilateral symptoms, neuro deficit or cancer concern.
  • Mimics: dental disease, TMJ dysfunction, migraine, cluster headache, postherpetic neuralgia, MS, tumour.
  • Red flags: persistent background pain, numbness, bilateral pain, age <40, abnormal neuro exam, jaw claudication, vesicular rash.

Psychiatric Problems

24 conditions
Acute stress reaction
  • Ix: clinical after trauma/stressor; assess sleep, dissociation, panic, substance use, safeguarding, self-harm/suicide risk.
  • Tx: psychological first aid, practical support, sleep hygiene; avoid routine benzodiazepines; trauma-focused CBT if symptoms persist or PTSD develops.
  • Escalate: urgent crisis/CMHT if suicidal, psychotic, severely dissociated, unsafe, safeguarding risk or unable to function.
  • Mimics: PTSD, adjustment disorder, panic disorder, depression, substance intoxication/withdrawal, delirium.
  • Red flags: suicidal intent, risk to others, domestic/sexual violence, psychosis, severe insomnia, substance escalation.
Alcohol withdrawal / opioid withdrawal
  • Ix: alcohol: AUDIT-C/CIWA-Ar, U&E/LFT/Mg/glucose, Wernicke risk; opioids: COWS, drug history, urine drug screen, BBV screen.
  • Tx: alcohol: thiamine before glucose, chlordiazepoxide symptom/fixed regimen per local detox policy; opioid: buprenorphine/methadone specialist-led.
  • Escalate: admit alcohol withdrawal if seizures, DTs, confusion, severe dependence, pregnancy, comorbidity; opioid crisis if overdose, pregnancy, polysubstance.
  • Mimics: sepsis, hypoglycaemia, head injury, hepatic encephalopathy, stimulant intoxication, anxiety, akathisia.
  • Red flags: delirium tremens, seizures, hallucinations, autonomic instability, Wernicke signs, respiratory depression, suicidal intent.
ADHD
  • Ix: developmental history, collateral/school history if possible, impairment in ≥2 settings; screen sleep, anxiety, depression, substance use, ASD.
  • Tx: psychoeducation, environmental adjustments; methylphenidate first-line in children/adolescents, lisdexamfetamine or methylphenidate in adults specialistled.
  • Escalate: ADHD specialist for diagnosis/meds; urgent if suicidality, severe risktaking, safeguarding, substance misuse or severe functional collapse.
  • Mimics: anxiety, depression, bipolar disorder, PTSD, sleep disorder, ASD, substance use, thyroid disease.
  • Red flags: mania/psychosis, cardiac history before stimulants, diversion/misuse risk, severe impulsivity, self-harm.
Autism spectrum disorder
  • Ix: developmental history, social communication/restricted behaviours, sensory profile, collateral; assess learning disability, ADHD, anxiety, epilepsy.
  • Tx: psychoeducation, reasonable adjustments, communication supports, occupational/sensory input; treat comorbid anxiety/depression/ADHD if present.
  • Escalate: autism diagnostic service; urgent crisis support if self-harm, severe distress, exploitation, safeguarding or carer breakdown.
  • Mimics: social anxiety, ADHD, learning disability, trauma, personality disorder, psychosis, selective mutism.
  • Red flags: regression, catatonia, suicidality, exploitation, severe eating restriction, aggression from unmet needs, diagnostic overshadowing.
Bipolar affective disorder
  • Ix: history of mania/hypomania/depression, sleep, psychosis, risk, substances; baseline FBC/U&E/LFT/TFT/calcium, pregnancy test, ECG if antipsychotic.
  • Tx: acute mania: stop antidepressant, antipsychotic e.g. olanzapine/quetiapine/haloperidol specialist-led; long-term lithium first-line where suitable.
  • Escalate: urgent crisis/CMHT or admission if mania, psychosis, severe depression, suicidality, risk to others, pregnancy/postpartum.
  • Mimics: ADHD, emotionally unstable personality disorder, substance-induced mood disorder, hyperthyroidism, psychosis, antidepressant activation.
  • Red flags: reduced need for sleep, grandiosity, reckless spending/sex/driving, psychosis, agitation, suicidal mixed state.
Delusional disorder
  • Ix: persistent delusion ≥1 month with otherwise relatively preserved function; assess psychosis, mood disorder, substances, dementia, neuro disease.
  • Tx: therapeutic engagement, CBTp; antipsychotic trial if distress/risk/impairment, usually specialist-led; address social/legal risk.
  • Escalate: urgent mental health if risk from delusion, violence, self-neglect, safeguarding, psychotic depression, command hallucinations.
  • Mimics: schizophrenia, dementia, delirium, substance-induced psychosis, bipolar mania, severe depression, OCD overvalued ideas.
  • Red flags: persecutory threat behaviour, weapons, stalking, self-neglect, nihilistic delusions, hallucinations, cognitive decline.
Depression
  • Ix: PHQ-9/severity, duration, function; ask directly about self-harm/suicide; screen bipolar, substances, grief, hypothyroid/anaemia if indicated.
  • Tx: active monitoring/CBT for less severe; SSRI e.g. sertraline 50 mg OD for moderate–severe or persistent symptoms; combine with therapy if severe.
  • Escalate: urgent crisis/CMHT if suicidal intent, psychosis, severe self-neglect, catatonia, bipolar depression, postpartum severe symptoms.
  • Mimics: bipolar disorder, hypothyroidism, anaemia, dementia, grief, PTSD, substance use, medication effects.
  • Red flags: suicidal plan/access, psychotic symptoms, severe weight loss, inability to care for self/dependants, agitation, postpartum onset.
Eating disorders
  • Ix: BMI/weight trajectory, SCOFF, binge/purge/exercise/laxatives; FBC/U&E/LFT/Mg/phosphate/glucose, ECG, BP/HR/temp, pregnancy test if relevant.
  • Tx: specialist ED service; nutritional rehabilitation, psychological therapy; correct electrolytes; thiamine/refeeding precautions if high risk.
  • Escalate: urgent admission if bradycardia, hypotension, syncope, hypokalaemia, ECG changes, rapid weight loss, suicidality, pregnancy.
  • Mimics: IBD, coeliac, hyperthyroidism, diabetes, malignancy, depression, OCD, ARFID.
  • Red flags: BMI very low, K low, QTc prolonged, dehydration, haematemesis, refeeding risk, self-harm, insulin omission.
Gambling disorder
  • Ix: screen losses, debt, chasing, concealment, crime, relationship/work harm; assess depression, suicidality, substance use, ADHD/bipolar.
  • Tx: CBT-based gambling treatment, financial blocking/self-exclusion, debt advice, family support; treat comorbid mental illness.
  • Escalate: urgent crisis if suicidal, domestic abuse, criminal exploitation, severe debt risk, safeguarding or homelessness.
  • Mimics: bipolar mania, ADHD impulsivity, substance use disorder, personality disorder, compulsive behaviour from dopamine agonists.
  • Red flags: suicidal thoughts after losses, illegal acts, violence/coercion, homelessness, relapse with escalating stakes, dopamine agonist use.
Gender dysphoria / incongruence
  • Ix: explore distress/goals, mental health, safeguarding, social support, capacity; do not treat identity itself as pathology, because that would be grim.
  • Tx: supportive, affirming care; referral to NHS gender identity clinic if desired; manage anxiety/depression; hormones/surgery specialist-led.
  • Escalate: urgent mental health if suicidality, self-harm, abuse, homelessness, safeguarding, unsafe self-medication or severe distress.
  • Mimics: body dysmorphic disorder, OCD, trauma-related distress, psychosis, depression, social anxiety.
  • Red flags: suicidality, hate crime/domestic abuse, coercion, unsafe hormones, severe isolation, eating disorder, safeguarding concerns.
Generalised anxiety disorder
  • Ix: excessive worry >6 months, GAD-7, functional impact; screen depression, panic, OCD, PTSD, substances, caffeine, thyroid disease.
  • Tx: guided self-help/CBT; SSRI e.g. sertraline 50 mg OD first-line drug option; avoid routine benzodiazepines except short-term crisis use.
  • Escalate: mental health referral if severe impairment, self-harm risk, substance misuse, diagnostic uncertainty or failed primary-care treatment.
  • Mimics: hyperthyroidism, arrhythmia, asthma, stimulant use, depression, PTSD, OCD, bipolar disorder.
  • Red flags: suicidal ideation, psychosis, mania, severe insomnia, alcohol/drug escalation, marked weight loss.
Obsessive-compulsive disorder
  • Ix: obsessions/compulsions, time spent, insight, avoidance; assess depression, tic disorder, ASD, psychosis, self-harm risk.
  • Tx: CBT with exposure and response prevention; SSRI e.g. fluoxetine/sertraline, often higher dose/longer trial than depression.
  • Escalate: specialist mental health if severe, poor insight, safeguarding risk, contamination causing self-neglect, intrusive harm thoughts with risk uncertainty.
  • Mimics: GAD, psychosis, ASD routines, eating disorder, body dysmorphic disorder, OCPD.
  • Red flags: suicidal ideation, severe self-neglect, psychotic-level beliefs, inability to eat/drink/leave home, safeguarding issues.
Panic disorder
  • Ix: recurrent unexpected panic attacks + anticipatory anxiety/avoidance; ECG/glucose/TFT if physical mimic suspected.
  • Tx: CBT first-line; SSRI e.g. sertraline 50 mg OD; breathing retraining; avoid longterm benzodiazepines.
  • Escalate: urgent if chest pain/arrhythmia/syncope, suicidality, severe agoraphobia, substance misuse or diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Mimics: ACS, SVT, PE, asthma, hypoglycaemia, hyperthyroidism, phaeochromocytoma, substance intoxication.
  • Red flags: exertional syncope, abnormal ECG, pleuritic chest pain, hypoxia, new onset age >45, suicidal thoughts.
Personality disorder
  • Ix: longitudinal pattern of interpersonal, affective, impulse and identity difficulties; assess trauma, self-harm, substance use, comorbid mood/psychosis.
  • Tx: structured psychological therapy e.g. DBT/MBT; crisis plan; avoid long-term medication for core symptoms, treat comorbid illness.
  • Escalate: crisis/CMHT if escalating self-harm, suicidality, risk to others, severe dissociation, safeguarding or unsafe discharge.
  • Mimics: bipolar disorder, ADHD, PTSD/CPTSD, autism, substance use, psychosis, depression.
  • Red flags: recent serious self-harm, suicidal plan, impulsive violence, exploitation, severe substance misuse, unstable housing.
Phobias including agoraphobia
  • Ix: specific trigger avoidance, anticipatory anxiety, impairment; assess panic disorder, depression, OCD, PTSD, substance use.
  • Tx: CBT with graded exposure first-line; SSRIs can help agoraphobia/panic; betablockers only for selected performance anxiety symptoms.
  • Escalate: mental health referral if housebound, severe impairment, self-neglect, suicidal thoughts or failed guided self-help/CBT.
  • Mimics: panic disorder, GAD, PTSD, OCD avoidance, social anxiety, agoraphobia secondary to physical illness.
  • Red flags: inability to leave home, severe weight loss/self-neglect, substance reliance, suicidality, safeguarding risk.
Post-traumatic stress disorder
  • Ix: trauma exposure + re-experiencing, avoidance, hyperarousal, negative mood/cognition >1 month; assess dissociation, substance use, suicide risk.
  • Tx: trauma-focused CBT or EMDR; SSRI e.g. sertraline/paroxetine if preferred or therapy unavailable; avoid psychological debriefing.
  • Escalate: urgent crisis if suicidal, psychotic, severe dissociation, ongoing abuse, safeguarding, complex PTSD with high risk.
  • Mimics: acute stress reaction, depression, GAD, panic disorder, substance use, emotionally unstable personality disorder.
  • Red flags: suicidal intent, violence risk, flashbacks causing unsafe behaviour, ongoing trauma, severe insomnia, substance escalation.
Postpartum depression
  • Ix: screen with Whooley/EPDS, assess bonding, intrusive thoughts, sleep, support, domestic abuse, suicide/infant harm risk.
  • Tx: psychological therapy for mild–moderate; SSRI e.g. sertraline often used in breastfeeding after risk discussion; practical support.
  • Escalate: perinatal mental health team if moderate–severe, complex history, bipolar risk; urgent crisis if suicidality, psychosis or infant safety concern.
  • Mimics: baby blues, postpartum psychosis, hypothyroidism, anaemia, PTSD birth trauma, adjustment disorder.
  • Red flags: suicidal thoughts, thoughts of harming baby, psychosis, severe insomnia, inability to care for baby, domestic abuse.
Postpartum psychosis
  • Ix: clinical emergency usually days–weeks postpartum; assess mania/psychosis, confusion, sleep, risk to mother/baby; exclude infection, eclampsia, thyroid disease.
  • Tx: same-day psychiatric assessment; antipsychotic/mood stabiliser/benzodiazepine specialist-led; mother-and-baby unit where possible.
  • Escalate: emergency mental health admission; 999/A&E if immediate risk, severe agitation, confusion, catatonia or suicidal/infanticidal thoughts.
  • Mimics: delirium, sepsis, eclampsia, thyroiditis, bipolar mania, substanceinduced psychosis, encephalitis.
  • Red flags: rapidly changing mood, bizarre beliefs about baby, command hallucinations, severe insomnia, confusion, suicidal/infanticidal ideas.
Psychotic disorder
  • Ix: hallucinations, delusions, disorganisation, negative symptoms; screen substances, mood disorder, delirium, neuro signs; baseline ECG/FBC/U&E/LFT/glucose/lipids.
  • Tx: antipsychotic + psychological/family intervention through early intervention/CMHT; address sleep, substance use and social risks.
  • Escalate: urgent same-day mental health if first-episode psychosis, risk, severe self-neglect, command hallucinations, agitation, safeguarding.
  • Mimics: delirium, bipolar mania, severe depression with psychosis, substanceinduced psychosis, dementia, epilepsy, autoimmune encephalitis.
  • Red flags: command hallucinations, persecutory delusions with threat, catatonia, suicidal intent, violence risk, rapid onset/confusion.
Schizoaffective disorder
  • Ix: episodes meeting mood disorder criteria plus schizophrenia symptoms, with psychosis also occurring outside mood episodes; collateral and longitudinal history essential.
  • Tx: antipsychotic core treatment; mood stabiliser or antidepressant depending bipolar/depressive type; psychological and relapse-prevention support.
  • Escalate: CMHT/early intervention; urgent if mania, severe depression, psychosis with risk, non-adherence relapse, safeguarding or self-neglect.
  • Mimics: schizophrenia, bipolar disorder with psychosis, psychotic depression, substance-induced psychosis, personality disorder.
  • Red flags: suicidal depression, manic disinhibition, command hallucinations, catatonia, poor intake, homelessness, risk to others.
Schizophrenia
  • Ix: symptoms >1 month with functional decline; exclude substances/organic causes; baseline metabolic, prolactin, ECG if antipsychotic planned.
  • Tx: oral antipsychotic with shared choice; CBTp/family intervention; depot/LAI if preference or adherence issue; physical health monitoring.
  • Escalate: early intervention for first episode; urgent crisis/admission if risk, severe self-neglect, catatonia, command hallucinations or relapse.
  • Mimics: bipolar psychosis, drug-induced psychosis, schizoaffective disorder, autism, dementia, delirium, temporal lobe epilepsy.
  • Red flags: command hallucinations, persecutory beliefs with weapons, suicide risk, poor intake, catatonia, severe withdrawal, substance misuse.
Self-harm
  • Ix: immediate physical injury/tox assessment; psychosocial assessment of intent, triggers, supports, safeguarding; do not use risk scales to predict suicide.
  • Tx: treat injuries/overdose; collaborative safety plan, means restriction, followup; address pain, intoxication, housing, abuse and mental illness.
  • Escalate: urgent mental health assessment/admission if ongoing intent, high lethality, psychosis, intoxication, unsafe home, safeguarding or repeated escalation.
  • Mimics: accidental injury, substance intoxication, dissociation, factitious disorder, interpersonal violence, neurodevelopmental distress.
  • Red flags: hanging/strangulation, drowning, high-place/traffic, large overdose, regret absent, isolation, final acts, access to lethal means.
Somatic symptom disorder
  • Ix: distressing physical symptoms with excessive health anxiety/behaviour; exclude urgent organic disease; review investigations to avoid duplication.
  • Tx: continuity with one clinician, validation, functional goals, scheduled reviews; CBT-based therapy; treat comorbid anxiety/depression.
  • Escalate: mental health if severe impairment, self-harm risk, medication misuse, safeguarding, diagnostic uncertainty or new objective signs.
  • Mimics: undiagnosed organic disease, health anxiety, depression, panic, functional neurological disorder, factitious disorder.
  • Red flags: new focal signs, weight loss, fever, GI bleeding, abnormal exam/tests, suicidal ideation, escalating opioid/benzo use.
Substance use disorder
  • Ix: substance, route, amount, dependence, withdrawal, overdose risk; urine drug screen if useful; BBV/STI screen, LFT/U&E/FBC, mental health risk.
  • Tx: harm reduction, naloxone for opioid risk, needle exchange, psychosocial treatment; opioid substitution with methadone/buprenorphine specialist-led.
  • Escalate: urgent if overdose, withdrawal seizure/DTs, pregnancy, psychosis, suicidality, injecting complications, safeguarding, polysubstance sedation.
  • Mimics: bipolar disorder, ADHD, psychosis, depression/anxiety, delirium, personality disorder, cognitive disorder.
  • Red flags: IV use, respiratory depression, endocarditis signs, abscess, BBV risk, homelessness, coercion/exploitation, relapse after abstinence.

Ent Problems

19 conditions
Airway compromise / management
  • Ix: ABCDE, look/listen/feel; assess stridor, voice, drooling, work of breathing, sats, GCS; identify obstruction, swelling, trauma, bleeding.
  • Tx: call anaesthetics/ENT early; high-flow O2, suction, positioning, airway manoeuvres, adjuncts, BVM if needed; treat cause.
  • Escalate: immediate crash call if stridor, exhaustion, cyanosis, reduced GCS, facial/neck swelling, burns, anaphylaxis or trauma.
  • Mimics: panic, asthma, COPD, vocal cord dysfunction, foreign body, epiglottitis, anaphylaxis.
  • Red flags: silent chest, drooling, tripod position, muffled voice, rapidly progressive swelling, hypoxia, bradycardia, impending arrest.
Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo
  • Ix: brief positional vertigo seconds–minutes; Dix-Hallpike confirms posterior canal BPPV; neuro exam normal.
  • Tx: Epley manoeuvre first-line; Brandt-Daroff exercises if recurrent; avoid routine vestibular suppressants.
  • Escalate: urgent neuro/ED if focal neuro signs, new severe headache, vertical nystagmus, ataxia, stroke risk, persistent continuous vertigo.
  • Mimics: vestibular neuritis, Ménière disease, vestibular migraine, posterior circulation stroke, acoustic neuroma.
  • Red flags: diplopia, dysarthria, limb weakness, severe gait ataxia, directionchanging nystagmus, new unilateral hearing loss.
Cholesteatoma
  • Ix: otoscopy: attic crust/retraction pocket, keratin debris, chronic discharge; audiometry; CT temporal bone specialist-led.
  • Tx: keep ear dry; topical antibiotics only if infected/discharging; definitive treatment usually surgical removal.
  • Escalate: ENT referral for all suspected; urgent if facial weakness, vertigo, mastoiditis, severe pain, intracranial signs.
  • Mimics: chronic otitis media, otitis externa, wax, foreign body, perforated eardrum, ear canal malignancy.
  • Red flags: foul persistent otorrhoea, hearing loss, vertigo, facial palsy, headache, fever, post-auricular swelling.
Epiglottitis
  • Ix: clinical emergency; do not distress patient or examine throat aggressively; lateral neck X-ray only if stable and diagnosis uncertain.
  • Tx: sit upright, high-flow O2, keep calm, prepare controlled airway; IV ceftriaxone/cefotaxime per local policy once airway safe.
  • Escalate: immediate anaesthetics/ENT/ICU; airway in theatre if deterioration; avoid lying flat unless necessary.
  • Mimics: croup, quinsy, deep neck abscess, anaphylaxis, foreign body, bacterial tracheitis.
  • Red flags: drooling, dysphagia, muffled voice, stridor, tripod posture, toxic appearance, rapidly worsening obstruction.
Epistaxis
  • Ix: ABCDE if heavy; ask anticoagulants/trauma; inspect nose; FBC/coag/group & save if severe, recurrent or anticoagulated.
  • Tx: sit forward, pinch soft nose 10–15 min; topical vasoconstrictor/local anaesthetic; cautery if visible point; pack if ongoing.
  • Escalate: ENT/ED if posterior bleed, haemodynamic compromise, recurrent packing failure, anticoagulated major bleed, suspected tumour.
  • Mimics: haemoptysis, haematemesis, nasal trauma, septal perforation, nasal cancer, hereditary haemorrhagic telangiectasia.
  • Red flags: shock, airway compromise, persistent bleeding >20–30 min, posterior bleed, unilateral recurrent bleed, facial trauma.
Head and neck cancer
  • Ix: full oral/neck exam, flexible nasendoscopy, FBC if anaemia; biopsy confirms; CT/MRI/PET-CT for staging.
  • Tx: MDT: surgery, radiotherapy, chemoradiotherapy, immunotherapy/palliation depending site/stage; smoking/alcohol cessation.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway for persistent oral ulcer >3 weeks, neck lump, hoarseness >3 weeks, dysphagia, unexplained oral lesion.
  • Mimics: dental infection, tonsillitis, salivary stone, thyroid nodule, lymphoma, TB, benign vocal cord lesion.
  • Red flags: weight loss, odynophagia, referred otalgia, stridor, haemoptysis, unilateral neck mass, cranial nerve palsy.
Labyrinthitis / vestibular neuritis
  • Ix: continuous vertigo days, nausea, gait instability; neuritis has no hearing loss, labyrinthitis has hearing symptoms; HINTS only if trained.
  • Tx: short course prochlorperazine 5 mg TDS or cyclizine 50 mg TDS for severe nausea; early mobilisation/vestibular rehab.
  • Escalate: same-day ED/neuro if stroke signs, severe ataxia, new headache, vertical nystagmus, vascular risk, sudden hearing loss.
  • Mimics: posterior circulation stroke, BPPV, Ménière disease, vestibular migraine, acoustic neuroma, drug toxicity.
  • Red flags: inability to walk unaided, focal neurology, direction-changing nystagmus, new unilateral deafness, severe headache.
Mastoiditis
  • Ix: clinical post-auricular swelling/tenderness, protruding pinna, fever; FBC/CRP/cultures; CT temporal bone if complication suspected.
  • Tx: same-day IV antibiotics per local policy; analgesia; myringotomy/mastoid surgery if abscess or poor response.
  • Escalate: urgent ENT admission for suspected mastoiditis; ICU/neurosurgery if intracranial spread, sepsis or facial palsy.
  • Mimics: otitis externa, lymphadenitis, cellulitis, parotitis, trauma, cholesteatoma.
  • Red flags: post-auricular swelling, pinna displacement, fever, severe otalgia, headache, meningism, facial weakness, vertigo.
Ménière disease
  • Ix: recurrent spontaneous vertigo 20 min–12 h + fluctuating hearing loss, tinnitus, aural fullness; audiometry confirms SNHL.
  • Tx: acute attacks: prochlorperazine/cyclizine short-term; prevention: lowsalt/caffeine reduction, betahistine specialist/local practice.
  • Escalate: ENT/audiovestibular referral; urgent if sudden hearing loss, neuro signs, persistent vertigo or diagnostic doubt.
  • Mimics: vestibular migraine, BPPV, acoustic neuroma, labyrinthitis, posterior circulation TIA/stroke.
  • Red flags: unilateral progressive hearing loss, focal neuro signs, severe headache, drop attacks, continuous vertigo.
Nasal polyps
  • Ix: anterior rhinoscopy; assess asthma/NSAID sensitivity, anosmia, chronic rhinosinusitis; unilateral polyp needs urgent assessment.
  • Tx: intranasal steroid e.g. mometasone 2 sprays each nostril OD; saline irrigation; short oral steroid course specialist/selected use.
  • Escalate: ENT if unilateral, bleeding, crusting, pain, recurrent despite treatment, obstruction severe or suspected malignancy.
  • Mimics: turbinate hypertrophy, allergic rhinitis, antrochoanal polyp, inverted papilloma, nasal cancer, foreign body.
  • Red flags: unilateral obstruction, epistaxis, facial pain/swelling, orbital symptoms, weight loss, cranial nerve signs.
Otitis externa
  • Ix: otoscopy: inflamed tender canal; tragal/pinna tenderness; check TM if visible; swab only recurrent, severe, immunocompromised or failed treatment.
  • Tx: keep ear dry, analgesia; topical antibiotic/steroid drops e.g. otomize/neomycin-steroid preparation 7 days if bacterial suspected.
  • Escalate: ENT if malignant otitis externa risk, diabetes/immunocompromised, severe pain, cranial nerve palsy, spreading cellulitis.
  • Mimics: otitis media, eczema, psoriasis, fungal otitis, foreign body, cholesteatoma, ear canal cancer.
  • Red flags: nocturnal severe pain, granulation tissue, facial palsy, diabetes, immunosuppression, fever, mastoid tenderness.
Otitis media
  • Ix: otoscopy: bulging red TM, effusion, reduced mobility; assess mastoid, perforation, systemic illness; hearing if persistent effusion.
  • Tx: analgesia first; antibiotics if systemically unwell/high-risk or persistent: amoxicillin 500 mg TDS 5–7 days; local policy applies.
  • Escalate: ENT/ED if mastoiditis, facial palsy, meningism, severe vertigo, immunocompromised, recurrent/persistent symptoms.
  • Mimics: otitis externa, glue ear, referred dental/TMJ pain, barotrauma, cholesteatoma.
  • Red flags: post-auricular swelling, protruding pinna, severe headache, confusion, facial weakness, persistent otorrhoea.
Perforated eardrum
  • Ix: otoscopy; document hearing; avoid irrigation; audiometry if persistent hearing loss; consider trauma/barotrauma/infection.
  • Tx: keep ear dry; analgesia; treat infection if present with non-ototoxic drops specialist/local guidance; most traumatic perforations heal.
  • Escalate: ENT if traumatic with hearing loss/vertigo, large or marginal perforation, persistent >6–8 weeks, recurrent discharge.
  • Mimics: wax/debris, cholesteatoma, otitis externa, foreign body, tympanosclerosis.
  • Red flags: vertigo, facial weakness, severe hearing loss, penetrating injury, CSF otorrhoea, persistent foul discharge.
Pharyngitis / tonsillitis
  • Ix: clinical; FeverPAIN/Centor guides antibiotics; throat swab if recurrent, severe, immunocompromised or outbreak; consider EBV if prolonged.
  • Tx: analgesia/fluids; antibiotics if high score/severe: phenoxymethylpenicillin 500 mg QDS 5–10 days; local policy applies.
  • Escalate: same-day if airway compromise, dehydration, sepsis, quinsy, immunosuppression or suspected glandular fever complications.
  • Mimics: viral URTI, EBV, COVID/flu, quinsy, epiglottitis, gonococcal pharyngitis, leukaemia.
  • Red flags: stridor, drooling, trismus, muffled voice, unilateral swelling, neck stiffness, rash, inability to swallow fluids.
Quinsy / peritonsillar abscess
  • Ix: clinical: unilateral sore throat, fever, trismus, “hot potato” voice, uvula deviation; bloods if septic; imaging only if deep neck spread suspected.
  • Tx: analgesia, IV fluids if dehydrated, antibiotics per local policy; needle aspiration or incision and drainage by ENT.
  • Escalate: same-day ENT/ED; emergency if airway compromise, neck swelling, sepsis, inability to swallow saliva.
  • Mimics: severe tonsillitis, parapharyngeal/retropharyngeal abscess, epiglottitis, dental abscess, lymphoma.
  • Red flags: trismus, drooling, stridor, torticollis, toxic appearance, uvula deviation, worsening despite antibiotics.
Rhinosinusitis
  • Ix: clinical; acute viral usually <10 days; bacterial suggested by severe symptoms, double-worsening or >10 days; imaging not routine.
  • Tx: saline irrigation, analgesia; intranasal steroid if prolonged; antibiotics only if severe/high-risk: phenoxymethylpenicillin 500 mg QDS 5 days.
  • Escalate: ENT/ED if orbital signs, severe frontal headache, meningism, immunocompromised, recurrent unilateral symptoms.
  • Mimics: migraine, dental infection, allergic rhinitis, nasal polyps, cluster headache, malignancy.
  • Red flags: periorbital swelling, diplopia, reduced vision, severe unilateral pain, forehead swelling, confusion, neck stiffness.
Thyroid cancer
  • Ix: neck exam, TFT usually normal; USS neck; FNA cytology for suspicious nodules/nodes; staging imaging MDT-led.
  • Tx: MDT: hemithyroidectomy/total thyroidectomy ± radioactive iodine; TSH suppression for selected differentiated cancers.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway for thyroid lump with hoarseness, rapid growth, stridor, dysphagia, cervical nodes, childhood irradiation.
  • Mimics: benign thyroid nodule, multinodular goitre, thyroiditis, lymphoma, branchial cyst, salivary lesion.
  • Red flags: hard fixed nodule, hoarseness, stridor, dysphagia, rapid enlargement, cervical lymphadenopathy, family MEN2.
Thyroid nodules
  • Ix: TFT first; USS risk stratification; FNA if suspicious USS/size criteria; radionuclide scan if TSH suppressed.
  • Tx: benign euthyroid nodules often observe; toxic nodule managed with radioiodine/surgery/antithyroid drugs specialist-led.
  • Escalate: ENT/endocrine if suspicious USS, compressive symptoms, abnormal nodes, hyperthyroidism, rapid growth or diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Mimics: thyroid cyst, lymph node, thyroglossal cyst, parathyroid adenoma, salivary mass, thyroid cancer.
  • Red flags: hoarseness, dysphagia, stridor, hard fixed mass, rapid growth, cervical nodes, prior neck irradiation.
Upper respiratory tract infection
  • Ix: clinical; check temp, sats, RR, hydration; test COVID/flu if highrisk/outbreak/management changes; exclude pneumonia, sepsis, epiglottitis.
  • Tx: self-care, fluids, paracetamol/ibuprofen, saline; no antibiotics unless bacterial complication or high-risk deterioration.
  • Escalate: same-day if hypoxia, sepsis, dehydration, immunosuppression, stridor, chest pain, severe headache/neck stiffness.
  • Mimics: influenza/COVID, pneumonia, allergic rhinitis, sinusitis, tonsillitis, asthma/COPD exacerbation.
  • Red flags: SpO2 <94%, RR high, confusion, non-blanching rash, drooling, severe unilateral throat pain, symptoms worsening after initial improvement.

Paediatric Presentations

19 conditions
Biliary atresia
  • Ix: conjugated bilirubin in any prolonged neonatal jaundice; pale stool/dark urine check; LFT, clotting, USS; HIDA/MRCP specialist-led.
  • Tx: fat-soluble vitamins, nutrition, correct coagulopathy; Kasai portoenterostomy ideally early, transplant if failed/late disease.
  • Escalate: urgent paediatric hepatology/surgical referral for conjugated jaundice or pale stools; time matters, because bile ducts lack patience.
  • Mimics: neonatal hepatitis, alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency, choledochal cyst, hypothyroidism, sepsis, metabolic liver disease.
  • Red flags: pale/acholic stools, dark urine, jaundice >14 days term or >21 days preterm, hepatomegaly, poor weight gain, bleeding.
Bronchiolitis
  • Ix: clinical in infants: coryza → cough/wheeze/crackles; assess RR, work of breathing, sats, feeding, apnoea risk; CXR/bloods not routine.
  • Tx: supportive; nasal saline/suction if secretions impair feeding; NG/IV fluids if poor intake; oxygen if persistent low sats per local threshold.
  • Escalate: admit if apnoea, severe distress, cyanosis, exhaustion, poor feeding <50–75%, dehydration, age <3 months or significant comorbidity.
  • Mimics: viral wheeze/asthma, pneumonia, sepsis, heart failure, foreign body, pertussis.
  • Red flags: apnoea, grunting, marked recession, SpO₂ persistently <90% if >6 weeks or <92% if <6 weeks/high-risk, silent chest.
Bronchopulmonary dysplasia
  • Ix: prematurity/oxygen history; sats including sleep/feeding, growth, CXR if deterioration; echo if pulmonary hypertension concern.
  • Tx: neonatal/respiratory follow-up; oxygen weaning plan, nutrition optimisation, vaccines incl. flu; diuretics/bronchodilators only selected specialist use.
  • Escalate: paeds respiratory/neonatal review if oxygen need persists, poor growth, recurrent admissions, pulmonary hypertension, aspiration concern.
  • Mimics: recurrent viral wheeze, aspiration, CF, congenital heart disease, airway malacia, immunodeficiency.
  • Red flags: increasing oxygen requirement, cyanosis, poor feeding/growth, recurrent apnoea, pulmonary hypertension, severe viral LRTI.
Childhood cancer
  • Ix: symptom-directed; FBC/film for pallor/bruising/bone pain; urgent imaging for mass/neuro signs; red reflex exam for retinoblastoma.
  • Tx: do not start steroids before oncology review if lymphoma/leukaemia possible; supportive care, infection treatment, pain relief, MDT therapy.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway/urgent paeds for unexplained mass, persistent bone pain, abnormal FBC, neuro signs, absent red reflex.
  • Mimics: infection, ITP, JIA, migraine, constipation, benign lymphadenopathy, trauma, viral illness, anaemia.
  • Red flags: weight loss, night sweats, persistent fever, bone pain, bruising, lymphadenopathy, morning vomiting/headache, leukocoria, proptosis.
Childhood hip / leg disorders
  • Ix: gait, hip ROM, neurovascular exam; AP pelvis/frog lateral X-rays for Perthes/SUFE; USS for DDH infants; FBC/CRP if infection.
  • Tx: SUFE: non-weight-bearing + urgent fixation; DDH: Pavlik harness if early; Perthes: analgesia/activity modification/orthopaedics.
  • Escalate: same-day ortho if unable to weight-bear, fever, severe pain, SUFE suspected, septic arthritis, trauma or neurovascular compromise.
  • Mimics: transient synovitis, septic arthritis, osteomyelitis, JIA, fracture, malignancy, non-accidental injury.
  • Red flags: painful restricted hip, knee pain with limp in obese adolescent, fever, night pain, weight loss, leg length/asymmetrical skin folds.
Cow milk protein allergy
  • Ix: clinical; IgE-mediated: immediate urticaria/vomiting/wheeze ± specific IgE/skin-prick; non-IgE: delayed GI/eczema, elimination then re-challenge.
  • Tx: breastfeeding: maternal dairy exclusion with dietitian; formula-fed: extensively hydrolysed formula, amino-acid formula if severe.
  • Escalate: paeds/allergy if anaphylaxis, faltering growth, blood in stool, multiple food allergy, severe eczema, diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Mimics: lactose intolerance, reflux, colic, viral gastroenteritis, coeliac disease, sepsis, IBD.
  • Red flags: anaphylaxis, wheeze/angioedema, poor growth, persistent vomiting, dehydration, rectal bleeding, lethargy.
Croup
  • Ix: clinical barking cough, hoarse voice, stridor; assess severity, sats, work of breathing; avoid throat exam if epiglottitis possible.
  • Tx: dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg PO stat; nebulised adrenaline for severe/lifethreatening croup with observation after.
  • Escalate: ED/admit if stridor at rest, moderate–severe distress, hypoxia, exhaustion, age <6 months, poor intake or diagnostic doubt.
  • Mimics: epiglottitis, bacterial tracheitis, foreign body, anaphylaxis, retropharyngeal abscess, diphtheria.
  • Red flags: drooling, toxic child, tripod position, muffled voice, cyanosis, silent chest, reduced consciousness.
Cryptorchidism
  • Ix: newborn/6–8 week check and recheck at 3 months; examine warmth-relaxed child; distinguish retractile vs undescended/impalpable testis.
  • Tx: no hormonal treatment; orchidopexy usually from 6 months and ideally before 12–18 months.
  • Escalate: paediatric surgery/urology if not descended by 3–6 months, newly ascended, bilateral impalpable testes or ambiguous genitalia.
  • Mimics: retractile testis, ectopic testis, anorchia, inguinal hernia, testicular torsion.
  • Red flags: bilateral impalpable testes, hypospadias/ambiguous genitalia, painful swollen groin/scrotum, vomiting, incarcerated hernia.
Faltering growth
  • Ix: plot weight/length/head circumference on UK-WHO chart; feeding history/observation; urine dip; targeted FBC/U&E/LFT/TFT/coeliac if indicated.
  • Tx: feeding support, optimise calories, treat underlying illness; dietitian/health visitor input; oral nutritional supplement only if persistent despite measures.
  • Escalate: paeds if severe weight loss, dehydration, safeguarding concern, developmental delay, vomiting/diarrhoea, recurrent infection, suspected neglect.
  • Mimics: normal centile crossing, prematurity, poor intake, reflux, CMPA, coeliac, chronic infection, cardiac/renal disease.
  • Red flags: weight loss crossing ≥2 centile spaces, poor linear/head growth, lethargy, dehydration, abuse/neglect signs, dysmorphism.
Febrile convulsions
  • Ix: diagnose seizure with fever age 6 months–5 years; assess infection source, meningitis/sepsis signs, glucose if prolonged/reduced consciousness.
  • Tx: if seizure >5 min: emergency benzodiazepine per paeds protocol, e.g. buccal midazolam; antipyretics for comfort not seizure prevention.
  • Escalate: same-day paeds if <18 months, complex seizure, recurrent same illness, no source, meningism, sepsis, delayed recovery.
  • Mimics: rigors, epilepsy, meningitis/encephalitis, syncope, hypoglycaemia, breath-holding attack.
  • Red flags: focal seizure, >15 min, repeated seizures, age <6 months, nonblanching rash, neck stiffness, persistent drowsiness.
Kawasaki disease
  • Ix: fever ≥5 days plus conjunctivitis, mucosal change, rash, extremity change, cervical node; FBC/CRP/ESR/LFT/urine; echo for coronaries.
  • Tx: admit; IVIG 2 g/kg once + aspirin high-dose acute then antiplatelet dose specialist-led; steroids if high-risk/resistant.
  • Escalate: urgent paeds/cardiology for suspected complete or incomplete Kawasaki; treat early, ideally within 10 days.
  • Mimics: scarlet fever, measles, adenovirus, toxic shock, sepsis, MIS-C/PIMS-TS, drug reaction.
  • Red flags: fever >5 days, red cracked lips/strawberry tongue, non-purulent conjunctivitis, swollen hands/feet, coronary aneurysm risk.
Mesenteric adenitis
  • Ix: clinical after viral illness; FBC/CRP/urine; USS if appendicitis uncertainty; diagnosis of exclusion, because abdomens are liars.
  • Tx: supportive: fluids, paracetamol/ibuprofen if safe, safety-net; antibiotics not routine.
  • Escalate: surgical/paeds review if worsening pain, guarding, persistent vomiting, fever, dehydration or appendicitis cannot be excluded.
  • Mimics: appendicitis, gastroenteritis, UTI, constipation, IBD, ovarian/testicular pathology, intussusception.
  • Red flags: localised progressive RIF pain, peritonism, bilious vomiting, bloody stool, sepsis, weight loss, persistent symptoms.
Necrotising enterocolitis
  • Ix: preterm/unwell neonate with distension/feed intolerance/bloody stool; FBC/CRP/U&E/blood gas/lactate/cultures; abdominal X-ray for pneumatosis/free air.
  • Tx: NBM, NG decompression, IV fluids/TPN, broad-spectrum IV antibiotics per neonatal policy, correct shock/coagulopathy.
  • Escalate: neonatal surgical/tertiary NICU urgently; emergency surgery if perforation, peritonitis, necrosis or deterioration.
  • Mimics: sepsis ileus, milk intolerance, Hirschsprung enterocolitis, volvulus, spontaneous intestinal perforation.
  • Red flags: abdominal wall erythema, shock, apnoea, metabolic acidosis, thrombocytopenia, portal venous gas, pneumoperitoneum.
Neonatal jaundice
  • Ix: measure bilirubin, plot against age-in-hours threshold; direct/conjugated bilirubin if prolonged; blood group/DAT/FBC/retics if haemolysis.
  • Tx: physiological: monitor/feeding support; phototherapy if threshold met; exchange transfusion/IVIG for severe haemolysis per neonatal protocol.
  • Escalate: same-day paeds if jaundice <24 h, very high/rising bilirubin, prolonged jaundice, pale stools/dark urine, unwell baby.
  • Mimics: breastfeeding jaundice, haemolysis, sepsis, hypothyroid, biliary atresia, G6PD deficiency, bruising/cephalohaematoma.
  • Red flags: jaundice in first 24 h, poor feeding/lethargy, high-pitched cry, opisthotonos, pale stool, dark urine, kernicterus signs.
Neonatal surgical emergencies
  • Ix: pyloric stenosis: projectile non-bilious vomiting + hypochloraemic alkalosis, USS; intussusception: episodic pain, pallor, currant jelly stool, USS.
  • Tx: NBM, IV fluids/electrolyte correction; pyloric stenosis needs pyloromyotomy after resus; intussusception air/contrast enema if stable.
  • Escalate: urgent paeds surgery; immediate theatre if peritonitis, perforation, shock, failed enema, suspected volvulus/malrotation.
  • Mimics: reflux, gastroenteritis, sepsis, CMPA, Hirschsprung disease, volvulus, incarcerated hernia.
  • Red flags: bilious vomiting, distension, shock, bloody stool, lethargy, dehydration, olive mass, recurrent drawing up legs.
Prematurity
  • Ix: gestational age, birthweight, temperature/glucose/sats; screen RDS, sepsis, NEC, IVH, ROP, jaundice, feeding issues.
  • Tx: neonatal unit care; thermoregulation, respiratory support/surfactant if RDS, caffeine for apnoea, breast milk/fortifier, infection prevention.
  • Escalate: NICU if very preterm, respiratory distress, apnoea, hypoglycaemia, hypothermia, sepsis, NEC, seizures or poor feeding.
  • Mimics: small for gestational age term infant, congenital infection, metabolic disease, birth asphyxia.
  • Red flags: apnoea, cyanosis, temperature instability, poor perfusion, bilious vomiting, feed intolerance, seizures, rapidly rising bilirubin.
Scarlet fever
  • Ix: clinical fever, sore throat, sandpaper rash, strawberry tongue; throat swab if uncertain/outbreak; assess sepsis/iGAS features.
  • Tx: phenoxymethylpenicillin 10 days; age dosing per BNF/local policy; macrolide if penicillin allergy; notify outbreaks per UKHSA rules.
  • Escalate: same-day if toxic, dehydration, breathing difficulty, soft tissue pain/swelling, immunocompromised, suspected invasive GAS.
  • Mimics: viral exanthem, measles, Kawasaki, drug rash, EBV, toxic shock, meningococcaemia.
  • Red flags: severe limb pain, hypotension, confusion, non-blanching rash, neck swelling, persistent fever, poor oral intake.
Viral exanthema
  • Ix: clinical rash pattern + fever/coryza; check vaccine status, exposure/travel/pregnancy contacts; test/notify suspected measles/rubella per public health.
  • Tx: supportive; isolate measles/varicella; varicella antivirals only selected highrisk/early severe cases; avoid aspirin in children.
  • Escalate: same-day if unwell child, immunocompromised, neonate, pregnancy exposure, measles suspected, non-blanching rash or respiratory distress.
  • Mimics: scarlet fever, Kawasaki, meningococcaemia, drug rash, allergy, HSP/IgA vasculitis, sepsis.
  • Red flags: non-blanching rash, mucosal involvement, lethargy, neck stiffness, dyspnoea, dehydration, vesicles near eye, immunosuppression.
Whooping cough
  • Ix: paroxysmal cough, whoop, post-tussive vomiting, apnoea in infants; nasopharyngeal PCR/culture early or serology later; notify UKHSA.
  • Tx: macrolide if within 21 days of cough onset or high-risk/transmission risk; supportive; post-exposure prophylaxis for selected vulnerable contacts.
  • Escalate: admit infants <6 months, apnoea/cyanosis, poor feeding, dehydration, pneumonia, seizures, significant comorbidity.
  • Mimics: viral URTI, bronchiolitis, asthma, pneumonia, foreign body, reflux, TB.
  • Red flags: apnoea, cyanosis, bradycardia, exhaustion, weight loss, seizures, infant age, incomplete immunisation.

Abscess
  • Ix: clinical fluctuant tender lump; USS if deep/uncertain; swab pus if recurrent, severe, MRSA risk or immunocompromised.
  • Tx: incision and drainage is definitive; antibiotics only if cellulitis/systemic illness/high-risk, e.g. flucloxacillin per local policy.
  • Escalate: same-day surgery/ED if face/hand/perianal/breast, sepsis, immunosuppression, diabetes, deep abscess or nec fasc concern.
  • Mimics: cellulitis, epidermoid cyst, hidradenitis, lymph node, haematoma, malignancy.
  • Red flags: fever, rapidly spreading erythema, severe pain, crepitus, immunosuppression, recurrent abscesses.
Candidiasis
  • Ix: clinical; vaginal pH usually normal; swab if recurrent, pregnancy, treatment failure, diagnostic doubt or non-albicans suspected.
  • Tx: vulvovaginal: clotrimazole 500 mg pessary stat or fluconazole 150 mg PO stat; oral: nystatin QDS or miconazole gel.
  • Escalate: same-day if systemic candidiasis suspected; specialist if recurrent ≥4/year, immunocompromised, pregnancy complexity, severe symptoms.
  • Mimics: BV, trichomoniasis, dermatitis, lichen sclerosus, HSV, aphthous ulcers.
  • Red flags: fever, dysphagia/odynophagia, immunosuppression, pregnancy, recurrent unexplained thrush, vulval ulceration.
Chlamydia
  • Ix: NAAT on first-catch urine or vulvovaginal/endocervical swab; test GC/HIV/syphilis; pregnancy test if relevant.
  • Tx: doxycycline 100 mg BD 7 days first-line; azithromycin/alternative in pregnancy or contraindication; partner notification and abstain until treated.
  • Escalate: sexual health if pregnancy, PID, epididymo-orchitis, under-16 safeguarding, recurrent infection or treatment failure.
  • Mimics: gonorrhoea, UTI, BV, candidiasis, PID, reactive arthritis.
  • Red flags: pelvic pain, fever, testicular pain, pregnancy, rectal symptoms, conjunctivitis, safeguarding concern.
COVID-19
  • Ix: test if result changes management, high-risk, hospital/care setting or outbreak; assess sats, RR, NEWS2, frailty, comorbidity.
  • Tx: self-care if mild; antivirals e.g. nirmatrelvir/ritonavir only for eligible high-risk groups and within time window; check interactions.
  • Escalate: same-day/999 if hypoxia, worsening breathlessness, chest pain, confusion, dehydration, frailty, pregnancy or immunosuppression.
  • Mimics: influenza, pneumonia, asthma/COPD exacerbation, PE, heart failure, sepsis.
  • Red flags: SpO₂ <94% or falling, cyanosis, haemoptysis, pleuritic pain, collapse, severe drowsiness, persistent high fever.
Gonorrhoea
  • Ix: NAAT from genital/rectal/pharyngeal sites by exposure; culture for antimicrobial susceptibility before treatment where possible.
  • Tx: ceftriaxone 1 g IM stat commonly first-line; ciprofloxacin 500 mg PO stat only if susceptible; sexual health/local policy applies.
  • Escalate: sexual health for all; urgent if PID, epididymo-orchitis, disseminated GC, pregnancy, sexual assault or safeguarding.
  • Mimics: chlamydia, UTI, candidiasis, BV, trichomoniasis, reactive arthritis.
  • Red flags: pelvic/testicular pain, fever, joint pain/rash, pregnancy, rectal pain/discharge, neonatal exposure.
Helicobacter pylori
  • Ix: urea breath test or stool antigen; stop PPI 2 weeks and antibiotics 4 weeks before testing if feasible; endoscopy if alarm features.
  • Tx: PPI + amoxicillin 1 g BD + clarithromycin 500 mg BD 7 days; penicillin allergy/resistance regimens vary, follow local policy.
  • Escalate: urgent endoscopy/cancer pathway if dysphagia, weight loss, GI bleed, persistent vomiting, IDA or epigastric mass.
  • Mimics: GORD, functional dyspepsia, peptic ulcer, gastric cancer, gallstones, pancreatitis.
  • Red flags: melaena, haematemesis, weight loss, dysphagia, anaemia, persistent vomiting, severe sudden pain.
Herpes simplex virus
  • Ix: clinical vesicles/ulcers; PCR swab lesion if genital, severe, atypical, pregnancy or immunocompromised; STI screen if genital.
  • Tx: genital first episode: aciclovir 400 mg TDS 5 days; recurrent episodic/suppressive regimens via sexual health/local guidance.
  • Escalate: same-day if neonatal HSV, encephalitis, keratitis, eczema herpeticum, pregnancy near term, immunocompromised severe disease.
  • Mimics: syphilis, chancroid, aphthous ulcer, Behçet, shingles, candidiasis fissuring.
  • Red flags: eye pain/redness, confusion/seizure, widespread painful lesions, urinary retention, pregnancy, neonate, immunosuppression.
Hospital-acquired infections
  • Ix: infection ≥48 h after admission or post-procedure; cultures before antibiotics if safe: blood/urine/sputum/wound/line; NEWS2/sepsis screen.
  • Tx: source control, remove infected lines/catheters where possible; antibiotics guided by site, severity, resistance risk and local policy.
  • Escalate: sepsis/ICU if shock, hypoxia, AKI, lactate high, neutropenia, C. diff severe, line sepsis or surgical complication.
  • Mimics: drug fever, VTE, atelectasis, transfusion reaction, inflammatory disease, malignancy fever.
  • Red flags: hypotension, confusion, oliguria, rigors with line, diarrhoea after antibiotics, wound dehiscence, resistant organism risk.
HIV
  • Ix: 4th-generation HIV Ag/Ab test; HIV RNA if acute infection suspected; baseline CD4, viral load, resistance, BBV/STI screen.
  • Tx: combination ART specialist-led; PEP ideally within 24 h and no later than 72 h after significant exposure; PrEP for ongoing risk.
  • Escalate: urgent HIV/ID if acute seroconversion, opportunistic infection, pregnancy, needle-stick/sexual exposure needing PEP, safeguarding.
  • Mimics: EBV/CMV, flu/COVID, lymphoma, TB, autoimmune disease, drug reaction.
  • Red flags: fever/rash/ulcers after exposure, weight loss, night sweats, oral candidiasis, PCP symptoms, meningitis, CD4 very low.
Human papilloma virus infection
  • Ix: genital warts usually clinical; cervical HPV via screening programme; biopsy atypical, pigmented, ulcerated or treatment-resistant lesions.
  • Tx: warts: podophyllotoxin, imiquimod or cryotherapy; HPV vaccination prevention; cervical abnormalities managed by colposcopy pathway.
  • Escalate: sexual health/gynae if pregnancy, immunosuppressed, extensive lesions, anal/cervical/vulval dysplasia, cancer concern.
  • Mimics: molluscum, seborrhoeic keratosis, skin tags, syphilis condylomata lata, SCC/VIN/AIN.
  • Red flags: bleeding, ulceration, induration, pigmentation, rapid growth, cervical symptoms, immunosuppression.
Infectious mononucleosis
  • Ix: FBC lymphocytosis/atypical lymphocytes, LFT, heterophile antibody/Monospot; EBV/CMV serology if unclear.
  • Tx: supportive; analgesia/fluids; avoid contact sports for at least 3–4 weeks and until splenomegaly resolves.
  • Escalate: same-day if airway obstruction, severe dehydration, jaundice, severe abdominal pain, haemolysis/thrombocytopenia, diagnostic doubt.
  • Mimics: acute HIV, CMV, toxoplasmosis, viral hepatitis, tonsillitis, lymphoma/leukaemia.
  • Red flags: stridor, drooling, LUQ pain, collapse, severe tonsillar swelling, prolonged fever/weight loss, hepatosplenomegaly.
Influenza
  • Ix: clinical in season; PCR if hospital/high-risk/outbreak or result changes treatment; assess sats, pneumonia/sepsis risk.
  • Tx: supportive; oseltamivir 75 mg BD 5 days if high-risk, severe or within treatment window per UKHSA/NICE; local policy applies.
  • Escalate: same-day/admit if hypoxia, pneumonia, sepsis, pregnancy, frailty, immunosuppression, dehydration or deterioration.
  • Mimics: COVID-19, RSV, pneumonia, meningitis, malaria/travel infection, sepsis.
  • Red flags: SpO₂ low, dyspnoea, chest pain, confusion, cyanosis, haemoptysis, neck stiffness, persistent fever.
Malaria
  • Ix: urgent thick/thin blood films and rapid antigen test; repeat films if negative but suspicion persists; FBC/U&E/LFT/glucose/lactate.
  • Tx: falciparum or severe malaria is emergency; IV artesunate for severe disease; uncomplicated regimen specialist/travel-medicine guided.
  • Escalate: same-day hospital assessment for any suspected malaria; ICU if severe anaemia, acidosis, renal failure, shock, cerebral malaria.
  • Mimics: flu, COVID, sepsis, typhoid, viral hepatitis, dengue, meningitis.
  • Red flags: fever after travel, confusion, seizures, jaundice, hypoglycaemia, bleeding, AKI, breathlessness, parasitaemia high.
Measles
  • Ix: clinical fever, cough/coryza/conjunctivitis, Koplik spots, maculopapular rash; PCR oral fluid/throat/urine; notify public health urgently.
  • Tx: isolate; supportive fluids/antipyretics; vitamin A in selected severe/high-risk cases specialist-led; manage complications.
  • Escalate: urgent if infant, pregnancy, immunocompromised, pneumonia, encephalitis, dehydration, severe illness or outbreak risk.
  • Mimics: rubella, scarlet fever, parvovirus B19, drug rash, Kawasaki, COVID/viral exanthem.
  • Red flags: photophobia, respiratory distress, confusion, seizure, pregnancy exposure, immunosuppression, incomplete vaccination.
Mumps
  • Ix: parotitis + fever; PCR buccal/oral fluid; notify public health; check vaccination and complications.
  • Tx: supportive analgesia/fluids; isolate; no antibiotic unless bacterial parotitis suspected.
  • Escalate: same-day if meningitis/encephalitis, orchitis severe, pancreatitis, hearing loss, pregnancy or immunocompromised.
  • Mimics: bacterial parotitis, salivary stone, dental abscess, EBV, HIV, lymphoma, Sjögren.
  • Red flags: neck stiffness, severe headache, testicular pain/swelling, abdominal pain, deafness, confusion, dehydration.
Pyrexia of unknown origin
  • Ix: confirm fever >38.3°C recurrent/prolonged; FBC/film, U&E/LFT/CRP/ESR, blood/urine cultures, CXR, HIV/TB/travel screen guided by history.
  • Tx: avoid blind antibiotics unless unstable/immunocompromised; treat identified cause; stop non-essential fever-causing drugs.
  • Escalate: admit if sepsis, neutropenia, immunosuppression, endocarditis suspicion, travel malaria risk, weight loss/night sweats or unstable.
  • Mimics: malignancy, autoimmune disease, drug fever, factitious fever, endocrine disease, occult abscess.
  • Red flags: rigors, murmur, embolic signs, lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly, weight loss, TB exposure, recent travel.
Rubella
  • Ix: fever/rash/lymphadenopathy; PCR/serology via public health; pregnancy exposure needs urgent risk assessment.
  • Tx: supportive; isolate; notify public health; no specific antiviral.
  • Escalate: urgent public health/obstetric advice if pregnant or exposed in pregnancy; specialist if encephalitis/thrombocytopenia.
  • Mimics: measles, parvovirus B19, scarlet fever, drug rash, EBV, enterovirus.
  • Red flags: pregnancy, non-immune exposure, arthralgia with rash, thrombocytopenic bleeding, encephalitis signs.
Sepsis
  • Ix: ABCDE, NEWS2, lactate/VBG, FBC/U&E/LFT/coag/glucose/CRP, cultures, urine, CXR/source imaging; identify source fast.
  • Tx: high-risk: IV broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour, oxygen if hypoxic, IV fluids, cultures, lactate, catheter/output monitoring.
  • Escalate: immediate senior/ICU if shock, lactate high, hypoxia, oliguria, confusion, mottling; neutropenic sepsis = IV antibiotics immediately.
  • Mimics: PE, pancreatitis, DKA, adrenal crisis, anaphylaxis, drug reaction, haemorrhage.
  • Red flags: SBP ≤90, RR high, SpO₂ low, new confusion, non-blanching rash, rigors, anuria, immunosuppression, neutrophils ≤0.5.
Surgical site infection
  • Ix: wound exam for erythema, warmth, pain, discharge, dehiscence; swab pus not dry wounds; FBC/CRP/cultures if systemic.
  • Tx: open/drain pus if collection; dressings and source control; antibiotics only if cellulitis/systemic/deep infection, local policy applies.
  • Escalate: urgent surgical review if deep/organ-space infection, sepsis, nec fasc, implant/prosthesis, dehiscence or failed oral therapy.
  • Mimics: haematoma, seroma, contact dermatitis, normal inflammation, stitch abscess, DVT.
  • Red flags: severe pain, crepitus, necrosis, spreading erythema, fever, wound breakdown, pus, hypotension.
Syphilis
  • Ix: serology: treponemal EIA/TPPA + non-treponemal RPR/VDRL; dark-field/PCR lesion where available; test HIV/GC/CT.
  • Tx: benzathine benzylpenicillin G 2.4 million units IM stat for early syphilis; later/neuro/pregnancy regimens specialist-led.
  • Escalate: sexual health for all; urgent if neuro/ocular/otic syphilis, pregnancy, congenital exposure, treatment allergy.
  • Mimics: HSV, chancroid, aphthous ulcer, psoriasis, pityriasis rosea, drug rash, mono.
  • Red flags: painless chancre, palmar/plantar rash, neuro/visual/hearing symptoms, aortitis signs, pregnancy, HIV coinfection.
Tetanus
  • Ix: clinical trismus, spasms, rigidity; wound history/vaccine status; cultures not reliable; notify public health.
  • Tx: wound debridement, metronidazole, human tetanus immunoglobulin, vaccine course; benzodiazepines for spasms, ICU support.
  • Escalate: immediate ICU/ID if suspected; airway/autonomic instability can deteriorate quickly.
  • Mimics: dystonic reaction, strychnine poisoning, hypocalcaemia tetany, meningitis, rabies, dental infection.
  • Red flags: lockjaw, dysphagia, opisthotonos, autonomic instability, respiratory spasm, contaminated wound, incomplete immunisation.
Toxic shock syndrome
  • Ix: clinical shock + fever + rash/multisystem involvement; FBC/U&E/LFT/coag/CK/lactate, blood/wound/vaginal cultures; source search.
  • Tx: sepsis resus, remove source e.g. tampon/packing/wound focus; IV broadspectrum antibiotics incl. clindamycin, local policy; IVIG specialist-led if severe.
  • Escalate: ICU immediately if shock, organ failure, lactate high, nec fasc concern, postpartum/post-op source.
  • Mimics: meningococcaemia, sepsis, drug reaction/SJS, Kawasaki, scarlet fever, viral exanthem.
  • Red flags: hypotension, diffuse erythematous rash, vomiting/diarrhoea, confusion, AKI, desquamation, tampon/wound/packing risk.
Toxoplasmosis
  • Ix: serology IgG/IgM with avidity if pregnancy; PCR amniotic fluid if fetal concern; CT/MRI brain + serology/PCR if immunocompromised.
  • Tx: immunocompetent usually supportive; pregnancy/immunocompromised treatment specialist-led, e.g. spiramycin or pyrimethamine-sulfadiazine regimens.
  • Escalate: urgent ID/obstetrics if pregnancy, immunocompromised, ocular disease, CNS signs, congenital infection concern.
  • Mimics: EBV/CMV, lymphoma, TB, HIV-related CNS lesions, cat-scratch disease, toxocariasis.
  • Red flags: pregnancy exposure, visual symptoms, seizures, focal neurology, HIV/immunosuppression, fetal abnormality.
Trichomonas vaginalis
  • Ix: NAAT preferred; wet mount if available but less sensitive; test for chlamydia/gonorrhoea/HIV/syphilis.
  • Tx: metronidazole 400 mg BD 5–7 days or 2 g stat; treat partners and abstain until treatment complete; local sexual health policy applies.
  • Escalate: sexual health if pregnancy, recurrent infection, treatment failure, PID symptoms, safeguarding concern.
  • Mimics: BV, candidiasis, cervicitis, gonorrhoea/chlamydia, retained tampon.
  • Red flags: pelvic pain, fever, pregnancy, postcoital bleeding, offensive discharge, sexual assault/safeguarding concern.
Tuberculosis
  • Ix: CXR, sputum AFB smear/culture/PCR x3 if pulmonary; IGRA/TST for latent TB; HIV test; CT/biopsy for extrapulmonary disease.
  • Tx: active TB: RIPE therapy 6 months typical, specialist/public-health supervised; latent TB regimens specialist/local pathway.
  • Escalate: TB team/public health for all; urgent if meningitis, miliary TB, spinal TB, haemoptysis, respiratory failure or immunosuppression.
  • Mimics: lung cancer, pneumonia, sarcoidosis, lymphoma, fungal infection, IBD if GI TB.
  • Red flags: cough >3 weeks, haemoptysis, night sweats, weight loss, fever, TB contact, homelessness/prison/migration risk.
Typhoid
  • Ix: travel/food exposure; blood cultures early, stool/urine cultures later; FBC/LFT; notify public health.
  • Tx: antibiotics guided by travel region/resistance and sensitivities, often ceftriaxone or azithromycin; local/imported infection advice applies.
  • Escalate: admit if toxic, dehydration, confusion, GI bleeding/perforation, pregnancy, immunocompromised or unable oral intake.
  • Mimics: malaria, dengue, viral hepatitis, gastroenteritis, sepsis, rickettsial disease, leptospirosis.
  • Red flags: fever after travel, relative bradycardia, abdominal pain, GI bleed, peritonitis, confusion, shock.
Varicella zoster virus
  • Ix: clinical chickenpox/shingles; PCR vesicle swab if atypical, severe, pregnant or immunocompromised; assess exposure risk.
  • Tx: shingles: aciclovir 800 mg five times daily 7 days if within 72 h/high-risk; chickenpox antivirals for selected adult/high-risk cases.
  • Escalate: urgent if pregnancy, neonate, immunocompromised, ophthalmic shingles, disseminated rash, pneumonia, encephalitis.
  • Mimics: HSV, impetigo, insect bites, contact dermatitis, scabies, hand-footmouth, drug eruption.
  • Red flags: eye involvement, breathlessness, confusion, ataxia, widespread haemorrhagic lesions, pregnancy, immunosuppression, neonate exposure.
Zoonotic infections including Lyme disease
  • Ix: exposure history: ticks, animals, farms, travel; Lyme erythema migrans is clinical; serology if no classic rash or later disease.
  • Tx: Lyme: doxycycline 100 mg BD usually 21 days for erythema migrans in adults; alternatives in pregnancy/children; local guidance applies.
  • Escalate: ID/public health if neuroborreliosis, carditis, pregnancy, severe systemic illness, rabies/brucella/leptospirosis suspicion.
  • Mimics: cellulitis, tinea, viral illness, meningitis, facial palsy, autoimmune disease, rickettsial infection.
  • Red flags: facial palsy, meningitis symptoms, AV block/syncope, severe headache, jaundice/AKI after animal water exposure, imported animal bite.

  • Ix: clinical; assess sepsis, lactation, nipple trauma; breast USS if abscess suspected; milk/pus culture if severe, recurrent or not improving.
  • Tx: continue breastfeeding/expressing if lactating; analgesia/warm compresses; flucloxacillin 500 mg QDS 10–14 days if bacterial mastitis, local policy applies.

  • Tx: MDT: surgery ± radiotherapy, chemo, endocrine therapy, HER2-targeted therapy; manage genetics/fertility/lymphoedema risk where relevant.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway if age ≥30 with unexplained breast lump, any suspicious skin changes, age ≥50 with unilateral nipple change/discharge.
  • Mimics: cyst, fibroadenoma, mastitis, fat necrosis, duct ectasia, abscess, gynaecomastia in men.

  • Ix: breast clinic triple assessment if new lump; USS first-line in younger patients, mammogram often if older; aspiration if symptomatic or diagnostic uncertainty.

  • Mimics: fibroadenoma, cancer, abscess, galactocele, fat necrosis, duct ectasia.
  • Red flags: postmenopausal new cystic mass, fixed/irregular lump, skin/nipple change, axillary node, bloodstained aspirate, recurrence.
Fibroadenoma
  • Ix: clinical mobile smooth “breast mouse”; USS ± mammogram by age/risk; core biopsy if imaging not clearly benign or enlarging.
  • Tx: reassure if confirmed benign and stable; observe; excision/vacuum-assisted removal if large, growing, painful or patient preference.
  • Escalate: breast clinic for any new unexplained lump; urgent if age ≥30 unexplained lump, rapid growth, atypical imaging or cancer features.
  • Mimics: phyllodes tumour, breast cancer, cyst, fat necrosis, hamartoma, lactating adenoma.
  • Red flags: rapid enlargement, size >3–5 cm, hard/fixed mass, skin/nipple change, axillary node, new lump after menopause.

Renal Problems

20 conditions
Acid-base abnormality
  • Ix: ABG/VBG, U&E, glucose, ketones, lactate, chloride, bicarbonate, anion gap; ECG if K/Ca abnormal.
  • Tx: treat cause: sepsis, DKA, renal failure, diarrhoea, vomiting, salicylates, respiratory failure; oxygen/ventilation if respiratory failure.
  • Escalate: ICU/renal if pH <7.1, lactate high, severe K abnormality, shock, reduced GCS, poisoning or renal failure.
  • Mimics: mixed disorders, compensation misread as primary disease, lab artefact, anxiety hyperventilation.
  • Red flags: severe acidosis/alkalosis, arrhythmia, seizures, hypotension, hyperkalaemia, rising CO₂, toxic ingestion.
Acute kidney injury
  • Ix: compare creatinine baseline, U&E, urine output, urinalysis, ACR, FBC/CRP, CK if rhabdo; renal USS if obstruction possible.
  • Tx: stop nephrotoxins/adjust doses, treat sepsis, restore volume if hypovolaemic, relieve obstruction, monitor fluid balance and K.
  • Escalate: renal/acute med if AKI stage 2–3, hyperkalaemia, acidosis, pulmonary oedema, oliguria/anuria, intrinsic renal disease.
  • Mimics: CKD, dehydration without AKI, lab error, high muscle mass, trimethoprim effect, obstructive uropathy.
  • Red flags: K ≥6.0, ECG changes, anuria, fluid overload, urea symptoms, haematuria + proteinuria, obstructed solitary kidney.
Bladder cancer
  • Ix: urine dip/culture, FBC/U&E; cystoscopy + biopsy confirms; CT urogram/USS upper tracts; urine cytology selected cases.
  • Tx: MDT; TURBT for diagnosis/staging; intravesical chemo/BCG for non-muscleinvasive; cystectomy/radiotherapy/systemic therapy if invasive.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway: visible haematuria age ≥45, or age ≥60 with unexplained non-visible haematuria plus dysuria/raised WCC.
  • Mimics: UTI, stones, BPH, renal cancer, prostatitis, glomerulonephritis, anticoagulant bleeding.
  • Red flags: painless visible haematuria, recurrent UTI symptoms with negative culture, weight loss, pelvic pain, hydronephrosis.
Chronic kidney disease
  • Ix: eGFR + urine ACR, repeat ≥3 months; BP, HbA1c, lipids, U&E, FBC, Ca/phosphate/PTH if advanced; renal USS if indicated.
  • Tx: optimise BP, diabetes, smoking/CV risk; ACEi/ARB if albuminuria; SGLT2 if CKD criteria met; avoid NSAIDs, dose-adjust drugs.
  • Escalate: renal if eGFR <30, ACR ≥70, rapid decline, resistant HTN, suspected renal artery stenosis, haematuria + proteinuria.
  • Mimics: AKI, dehydration, high creatinine from muscle mass, age-related eGFR decline without markers, obstructive uropathy.
  • Red flags: rapid eGFR fall, nephrotic-range proteinuria, refractory hyperkalaemia, pulmonary oedema, uraemic symptoms.
End-stage renal disease
  • Ix: eGFR trend, U&E, bicarbonate, Ca/phosphate/PTH, FBC/iron, viral screen/transplant work-up; access planning: fistula/PD catheter.
  • Tx: renal replacement planning: haemodialysis, peritoneal dialysis, transplant or conservative care; manage anaemia, bone-mineral disease, acidosis.
  • Escalate: urgent renal/admission if refractory hyperkalaemia, pulmonary oedema, severe acidosis, uraemic pericarditis/encephalopathy, sepsis.
  • Mimics: AKI on CKD, cardiorenal syndrome, obstructive uropathy, myeloma kidney.
  • Red flags: pericarditic chest pain, confusion, seizures, K high, fluid overload, severe itch/anorexia, failing dialysis access.
Glomerulonephritis
  • Ix: urine dip microscopy, ACR/PCR, U&E/eGFR, BP, FBC; complements, ANA/ANCA/anti-GBM, hepatitis/HIV, ASO; renal biopsy often confirms.
  • Tx: renal-led; control BP/proteinuria with ACEi/ARB if safe; immunosuppression/plasma exchange for selected causes.
  • Escalate: same-day renal if AKI, rapidly rising creatinine, nephritic syndrome, pulmonary haemorrhage, anti-GBM/vasculitis suspicion.
  • Mimics: UTI, stones, menstruation contamination, IgA vasculitis, interstitial nephritis, malignant HTN.
  • Red flags: cola urine, proteinuria + haematuria, hypertension, oedema, AKI, oliguria, haemoptysis.
Hydronephrosis
  • Ix: renal USS first-line; CT KUB/CT urogram depending stone/cancer concern; U&E/eGFR, urinalysis/culture.
  • Tx: treat cause; analgesia/fluids for stones; catheter if bladder outlet obstruction; nephrostomy/stent if upper tract obstruction.
  • Escalate: immediate urology if pyonephrosis, sepsis, AKI, bilateral obstruction, solitary kidney obstruction or uncontrolled pain.
  • Mimics: renal cysts, parapelvic cysts, extrarenal pelvis, reflux nephropathy, pregnancy physiological dilatation.
  • Red flags: fever, rigors, anuria, AKI, solitary kidney, bilateral hydronephrosis, severe flank pain, cancer symptoms.
Hypercalcaemia / hypocalcaemia
  • Ix: adjusted Ca, phosphate, Mg, PTH, vitamin D, U&E/eGFR, ECG; myeloma/malignancy screen if PTH suppressed.
  • Tx: severe hypercalcaemia: IV 0.9% saline ± IV bisphosphonate; symptomatic hypocalcaemia: calcium gluconate 10% 10–20 mL IV.
  • Escalate: admit if Ca >3.0, confusion, dehydration, AKI, arrhythmia, seizures, tetany, laryngospasm or pancreatitis.
  • Mimics: lab albumin effect, malignancy, primary hyperparathyroidism, vitamin D disorders, CKD-MBD, hypomagnesaemia.
  • Red flags: shortened/long QT, seizures, tetany, renal stones, vomiting, confusion, severe weakness, arrhythmia.
Hyperkalaemia / hypokalaemia
  • Ix: repeat urgent U&E if unexpected, ECG, VBG, glucose, Mg, renal function, medication review; check haemolysis artefact.
  • Tx: hyperK with ECG/K ≥6.5: IV calcium, insulin-glucose, salbutamol neb, remove K; hypoK: oral/IV KCl + Mg correction.
  • Escalate: immediate admission/renal/ICU if ECG changes, K ≥6.5, K <2.5, arrhythmia, AKI, digoxin toxicity, paralysis.
  • Mimics: haemolysed sample, thrombocytosis/leucocytosis pseudohyperK, acidbase shifts, diuretics, adrenal disease.
  • Red flags: peaked T waves, broad QRS, weakness/paralysis, palpitations, syncope, severe AKI, ongoing losses.
Hyponatraemia / hypernatraemia
  • Ix: serum osmolality, urine osmolality, urine Na, glucose, U&E, TFT, cortisol; assess volume status and drug causes.
  • Tx: hyponatraemia: fluid restrict SIADH; severe symptomatic needs hypertonic saline specialist-led; hypernatraemia: slow free-water replacement.
  • Escalate: admit if Na <125, Na >155, seizures, confusion, coma, rapid change, severe dehydration or high overcorrection risk.
  • Mimics: pseudohyponatraemia, hyperglycaemia-related low Na, SIADH, adrenal insufficiency, diuretics, diabetes insipidus.
  • Red flags: seizure, reduced GCS, severe headache/vomiting, focal neurology, rapid correction, malnutrition/alcohol dependence.
IgA vasculitis
  • Ix: clinical palpable purpura + abdo/joint/renal features; BP, urinalysis, ACR/PCR, U&E/eGFR, FBC/coag; biopsy if atypical.
  • Tx: analgesia, hydration, monitor urine/BP; steroids specialist-led for severe abdominal, renal or testicular involvement.
  • Escalate: paeds/renal same-day if nephritis, hypertension, AKI, severe abdo pain, GI bleeding, testicular pain or diagnostic doubt.
  • Mimics: meningococcaemia, ITP, leukaemia, HUS, ANCA vasculitis, drug eruption, non-accidental injury.
  • Red flags: haematuria/proteinuria, hypertension, AKI, intussusception, GI bleed, scrotal pain, widespread necrotic purpura.
Nephrotic syndrome
  • Ix: urine ACR/PCR, serum albumin, lipids, U&E/eGFR; screen diabetes, lupus, hepatitis/HIV, myeloma; renal biopsy often adult standard.
  • Tx: salt restriction, diuretics for oedema, ACEi/ARB for proteinuria if safe, statin if indicated; anticoagulation risk assessed.
  • Escalate: renal referral for all adults; urgent if AKI, severe oedema, thrombosis, infection, very low albumin or suspected systemic disease.
  • Mimics: heart failure, liver cirrhosis, malnutrition, lymphoedema, venous insufficiency.
  • Red flags: frothy urine, massive oedema, albumin very low, PE/DVT, cellulitis/sepsis, AKI, haematuria.
Polycystic kidney disease
  • Ix: family history, BP, U&E/eGFR, urine ACR; renal USS diagnostic by age/family criteria; genetic testing selected cases.
  • Tx: BP control, ACEi/ARB often used, CV risk reduction, avoid nephrotoxins; tolvaptan specialist-led for rapid progressors.
  • Escalate: renal/genetics if young diagnosis, declining eGFR, haematuria, recurrent infection/stones, family planning or aneurysm family history.
  • Mimics: simple renal cysts, acquired cystic kidney disease, tuberous sclerosis, von Hippel-Lindau, medullary cystic disease.
  • Red flags: thunderclap headache, visible haematuria, infected cyst fever, severe flank pain, hypertension, family SAH, CKD progression.
Pyelonephritis
  • Ix: urine dip/culture before antibiotics if possible; FBC/CRP/U&E if systemically unwell; pregnancy test; imaging if obstruction/sepsis/recurrent.
  • Tx: oral if stable: cefalexin/co-amoxiclav/ciprofloxacin depending local policy; severe: IV antibiotics + fluids.
  • Escalate: admit if sepsis, vomiting, pregnancy, AKI, obstruction, immunocompromised, frailty, failed oral antibiotics.
  • Mimics: renal colic, appendicitis, PID, cholecystitis, diverticulitis, pneumonia, shingles.
  • Red flags: rigors, flank pain + fever, hypotension, confusion, pregnancy, solitary kidney, obstruction, diabetes.
Renal cancer
  • Ix: urine dip, FBC/U&E/LFT/calcium; CT renal protocol; biopsy selected cases; CT chest for staging.
  • Tx: MDT: partial/radical nephrectomy or ablation if localised; immunotherapy/targeted therapy for advanced disease.
  • Escalate: suspected cancer pathway for visible haematuria or renal mass; urgent if hypercalcaemia, clot retention, severe pain or metastatic cord symptoms.
  • Mimics: renal cyst, stones, UTI, pyelonephritis, angiomyolipoma, bladder cancer, glomerulonephritis.
  • Red flags: haematuria, flank mass/pain, weight loss, fever, night sweats, polycythaemia, hypercalcaemia, varicocoele new onset.
Rhabdomyolysis
  • Ix: CK, U&E/eGFR, K/Ca/phosphate, LFT, urine dip positive blood with few RBCs, ECG; identify trauma, seizures, drugs, statins, heat.
  • Tx: aggressive IV crystalloid if significant CK/AKI risk, monitor urine output/electrolytes; stop culprit drugs; treat hyperkalaemia urgently.
  • Escalate: admit if CK high/rising, AKI, hyperkalaemia, acidosis, compartment syndrome, heat stroke, seizures or crush injury.
  • Mimics: myositis, MI with raised CK, haematuria, statin myalgia, polymyalgia rheumatica, viral myalgia.
  • Red flags: dark urine, severe muscle pain/swelling, weakness, K high, AKI, compartment pain, collapse/heat illness.
Urinary incontinence
  • Ix: classify stress/urge/mixed/overflow; bladder diary, urinalysis, post-void residual if overflow; pelvic exam/prolapse and medication review.
  • Tx: pelvic floor muscle training for stress; bladder training for urge; consider antimuscarinic or mirabegron if lifestyle/training fails.
  • Escalate: urology/urogynae if haematuria, recurrent UTI, retention, prolapse, neuro signs, fistula, failed conservative management.
  • Mimics: UTI, diabetes, diuretics, constipation, prolapse, neurogenic bladder, overflow from BPH.
  • Red flags: painless haematuria, new saddle anaesthesia, urinary retention, recurrent infections, pelvic mass, continuous leakage after surgery/birth.
Urinary retention / obstructive uropathy
  • Ix: bladder scan, catheter residual, U&E/eGFR, urinalysis/culture; renal USS if AKI, chronic retention or upper tract concern.
  • Tx: acute retention: urethral catheter; suprapubic if failed/contraindicated; treat cause; alpha-blocker e.g. tamsulosin 400 micrograms OD in BPH.
  • Escalate: same-day urology if catheter fails, sepsis, AKI, anuria, obstructed solitary kidney, cauda equina signs or post-obstructive diuresis.
  • Mimics: constipation, UTI, prostatitis, neurogenic bladder, urethral stricture, pelvic mass, drug-induced retention.
  • Red flags: severe suprapubic pain, anuria, AKI, fever, saddle anaesthesia, bilateral hydronephrosis, high-pressure chronic retention.
Urinary tract calculi
  • Ix: urinalysis, pregnancy test, U&E, FBC/CRP if infected; non-contrast CT KUB first-line in adults; USS if pregnancy.
  • Tx: NSAID first-line e.g. diclofenac 75 mg IM or ibuprofen if safe; fluids to thirst; tamsulosin may help distal ureteric stone passage.
  • Escalate: emergency urology if sepsis, obstruction, solitary kidney, bilateral stones, AKI, uncontrolled pain/vomiting.
  • Mimics: AAA, pyelonephritis, appendicitis, diverticulitis, testicular torsion, ectopic pregnancy, biliary colic.
  • Red flags: fever/rigors, anuria, hypotension, solitary kidney, pregnancy, severe persistent pain, stone with infection.
Urinary tract infection
  • Ix: dipstick in non-pregnant women if uncertain; urine culture if male, pregnant, recurrent, catheter, pyelo, treatment failure or atypical.
  • Tx: uncomplicated lower UTI: nitrofurantoin 100 mg MR BD 3 days if eGFR adequate; trimethoprim 200 mg BD if low resistance risk; local policy.
  • Escalate: same-day if pyelonephritis/sepsis, pregnancy, male systemic symptoms, catheter-associated sepsis, AKI, obstruction or immunocompromised.
  • Mimics: STI/urethritis, vaginitis, overactive bladder, interstitial cystitis, stones, bladder cancer, prostatitis.
  • Red flags: fever, flank pain, rigors, vomiting, pregnancy, visible haematuria, recurrent culture-negative symptoms, confusion in frail older adult.

  • Red flags: saddle anaesthesia, urinary retention, bilateral sciatica, fever, cancer history, night pain, progressive weakness.
Joint dislocation / subluxation

  • Mimics: RA, myositis, hypothyroidism, malignancy, infection, fibromyalgia, statin myopathy.
  • Red flags: headache, jaw claudication, visual symptoms, weight loss, fever, true weakness, very high inflammatory markers.
Polymyositis / dermatomyositis
  • Ix: CK, LFT, ESR/CRP, myositis antibodies, ANA; EMG/MRI muscle; muscle/skin biopsy; malignancy and ILD screening.
  • Tx: high-dose corticosteroid specialist-led ± methotrexate/azathioprine/mycophenolate; physio; treat dysphagia/ILD urgently.
  • Escalate: rheum/neurology urgently if proximal weakness, dysphagia, respiratory symptoms, rash with weakness, CK very high.
  • Mimics: statin myopathy, muscular dystrophy, endocrine myopathy, MND, myasthenia, PMR.
  • Red flags: dysphagia, dyspnoea, aspiration, Gottron papules/heliotrope rash, rapidly progressive weakness, malignancy features.
Raynaud phenomenon
  • Ix: history triphasic colour change; nailfold capillaroscopy if available; FBC/ESR/CRP/ANA if secondary suspected.
  • Tx: keep warm, stop smoking, avoid triggers; nifedipine MR 10–20 mg OD/BD if troublesome and BP allows.
  • Escalate: rheum/vascular if digital ulcers, tissue loss, abnormal ANA/capillaries, late onset, asymmetry or systemic sclerosis features.
  • Mimics: acrocyanosis, peripheral vascular disease, thoracic outlet syndrome, chilblains, vasculitis, drug-induced vasospasm.
  • Red flags: onset >30, severe pain, ulcers, gangrene, asymmetrical attacks, puffy fingers, skin thickening, dysphagia.
Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Ix: urgent referral based on synovitis; RF/anti-CCP, ESR/CRP, FBC; X-rays hands/feet if persistent; don’t wait for serology.
  • Tx: DMARD within specialist care, usually methotrexate first-line; NSAID/short steroid bridge for symptoms if safe.
  • Escalate: rheum within 3 weeks if persistent synovitis, small joints, >1 joint or delay ≥3 months; urgent if systemic/eye/lung signs.
  • Mimics: OA, psoriatic arthritis, viral arthritis, gout/CPPD, lupus, septic arthritis.
  • Red flags: hot single joint, eye pain/redness, breathlessness, vasculitic rash, neuropathy, cervical myelopathy symptoms.
Scleroderma / mixed connective tissue disease
  • Ix: ANA, ENA incl. anti-centromere/Scl-70/RNP, FBC/U&E/LFT/CRP, urinalysis; capillaroscopy, PFT/DLCO, HRCT/echo if systemic.
  • Tx: rheum-led organ-specific therapy; Raynaud control, PPI for reflux, immunosuppression for ILD/skin; ACEi for renal crisis.
  • Escalate: urgent if new hypertension/AKI, breathlessness, chest pain, digital ischaemia, dysphagia/weight loss or pulmonary HTN.
  • Mimics: primary Raynaud, lupus, dermatomyositis, Sjogren, eosinophilic fasciitis, fibromyalgia.
  • Red flags: scleroderma renal crisis, digital ulcers, pulmonary hypertension, ILD, rapidly progressive skin thickening, severe reflux/aspiration.
Septic arthritis
  • Ix: emergency joint aspiration before antibiotics if possible: Gram stain, culture, crystals; FBC/CRP/blood cultures; X-ray/USS if needed.
  • Tx: IV antibiotics immediately after cultures/aspiration, local policy; joint washout/drainage, analgesia, immobilise then physio.
  • Escalate: same-day ED/ortho/rheum for any suspected septic arthritis; prosthetic joint = urgent orthopaedics.
  • Mimics: gout/CPPD, reactive arthritis, trauma, haemarthrosis, cellulitis, bursitis.
  • Red flags: hot swollen joint, fever, inability to weight-bear, immunosuppression, prosthetic joint, IV drug use, very high CRP.
Seronegative spondyloarthropathies
  • Ix: inflammatory back pain, enthesitis/dactylitis/uveitis/psoriasis/IBD/STI history; CRP, HLA-B27, pelvic X-ray or MRI SI joints.
  • Tx: exercise/physio; NSAID first-line if safe, e.g. naproxen; biologics specialist-led if active disease persists.
  • Escalate: rheum if inflammatory back pain, uveitis, dactylitis, enthesitis, psoriasis/IBD arthritis or reactive arthritis severe.
  • Mimics: mechanical back pain, RA, gout, septic arthritis, fibromyalgia, OA.
  • Red flags: acute red painful eye, severe night pain, neurological deficit, weight loss, fever, hot swollen joint, IBD flare.
Sjogren syndrome
  • Ix: Schirmer test, ocular staining, salivary flow; ANA/ENA anti-Ro/La, RF, immunoglobulins; consider salivary gland USS/biopsy.
  • Tx: artificial tears/saliva, dental fluoride, pilocarpine if suitable; treat systemic disease rheum-led.
  • Escalate: rheum/ophthalmology if systemic features, severe dry eye, corneal damage, parotid swelling, neuropathy or renal disease.
  • Mimics: medication anticholinergic effects, dehydration, menopause, diabetes, hepatitis C, sarcoidosis, IgG4 disease.
  • Red flags: persistent parotid enlargement, lymphadenopathy, purpura, neuropathy, renal tubular acidosis, weight loss, lymphoma risk.
Soft tissue injury
  • Ix: mechanism, function, neurovascular status; Ottawa rules for ankle/knee where relevant; USS/MRI if tendon rupture suspected.
  • Tx: protection, relative rest, ice/compression/elevation short term, analgesia, early mobilisation/physio; avoid prolonged immobilisation unless unstable.
  • Escalate: ED/ortho if complete tendon rupture, neurovascular deficit, open wound, severe swelling, dislocation/fracture concern.
  • Mimics: fracture, dislocation, compartment syndrome, septic arthritis, DVT, tendon rupture.
  • Red flags: inability to weight-bear, gross instability, deformity, numbness, absent pulse, severe escalating pain, Achilles gap.
Systemic lupus erythematosus
  • Ix: ANA, anti-dsDNA, ENA, complements, FBC/U&E/LFT/ESR/CRP, urinalysis/ACR; assess renal, skin, joints, neuro, serositis.
  • Tx: sun protection; hydroxychloroquine usually baseline specialist therapy; steroids/immunosuppression for flares/organ disease.
  • Escalate: rheum for suspected SLE; urgent if nephritis, CNS disease, chest pain/serositis, cytopenias, pregnancy, thrombosis.
  • Mimics: viral arthritis, RA, fibromyalgia, Sjogren, vasculitis, drug-induced lupus, antiphospholipid syndrome.
  • Red flags: proteinuria/haematuria, seizures/psychosis, pleuritic chest pain, severe cytopenias, thrombosis, fever, pregnancy.
Vasculitis / vasculitides
  • Ix: FBC/U&E/LFT/ESR/CRP, urinalysis/ACR, ANCA/ANA/complements, hepatitis/HIV, CXR; biopsy or angiography where indicated.
  • Tx: depends vessel/organ; high-dose steroids for organ-threatening disease; cyclophosphamide/rituximab specialist-led; treat infection mimics first.
  • Escalate: urgent rheum/renal/ED if renal impairment, haemoptysis, mononeuritis multiplex, CNS signs, digital ischaemia or systemic toxicity.
  • Mimics: infection/endocarditis, malignancy, drug reaction, emboli, SLE, antiphospholipid syndrome.
  • Red flags: purpura with AKI, pulmonary haemorrhage, neuropathy, visual loss, limb ischaemia, stroke, severe abdominal pain.

Toxicology / Drug Reactionsinferred heading

4 conditions
Adverse drug effects
  • Ix: full drug history (prescribed, OTC, herbal, recreational), timing of symptoms, FBC/U&E/LFT, ECG and drug levels where relevant.
  • Tx: stop/reduce suspected drug if safe; supportive treatment; document allergy/intolerance clearly and report serious reactions (Yellow Card).
  • Escalate: admit/specialist review for anaphylaxis, SJS/TEN, DRESS, agranulocytosis, severe hepatotoxicity, arrhythmia, organ failure.
  • Mimics: sepsis, autoimmune disease, infection, malignancy, metabolic disorders, progression of underlying illness.
  • Red flags: airway swelling, widespread blistering rash, eosinophilia, jaundice, neutropenia, QT prolongation, hypotension.
Drug overdose
  • Ix: ABCDE, ECG, capillary glucose, VBG/ABG, U&E, paracetamol level, toxicology history; targeted drug levels if available.
  • Tx: supportive care first; activated charcoal if appropriate timing; antidotes where indicated (e.g. naloxone, N-acetylcysteine).
  • Escalate: ICU/toxicology if reduced GCS, seizures, arrhythmia, respiratory failure, severe acidosis, shock or prolonged-release ingestion.
  • Mimics: stroke, sepsis, hypoglycaemia, meningitis, intoxication with other substances, psychiatric illness alone.
  • Red flags: airway compromise, QRS/QT prolongation, refractory hypotension, hyperthermia, serotonin/NMS features, self-harm intent.
Neuroleptic malignant syndrome
  • Ix: recent dopamine antagonist use or dopamine-withdrawal; CK, U&E, LFT, FBC, CRP, ABG; assess for rhabdomyolysis and AKI.
  • Tx: stop causative drug immediately; aggressive IV fluids, cooling, electrolyte correction; bromocriptine or dantrolene specialist-led.
  • Escalate: ICU if hyperthermia, autonomic instability, rigidity, AKI, respiratory failure or markedly elevated CK.
  • Mimics: serotonin syndrome, malignant hyperthermia, sepsis, heat stroke, encephalitis, catatonia.
  • Red flags: temperature >38.5°C, lead-pipe rigidity, confusion, diaphoresis, labile BP, tachycardia, CK markedly raised.
Serotonin syndrome
  • Ix: recent serotonergic drug exposure; clinical diagnosis: clonus, hyperreflexia, agitation, autonomic features; ECG, CK, U&E, temperature.
  • Tx: stop serotonergic drugs; IV fluids, benzodiazepines for agitation/tremor; cooling if hyperthermic; cyproheptadine specialist-led in severe cases.
  • Escalate: ICU if severe hyperthermia, rigidity, seizures, rhabdomyolysis, shock or reduced consciousness.
  • Mimics: NMS, anticholinergic toxicity, sepsis, stimulant toxicity, meningitis, heat stroke.
  • Red flags: inducible/spontaneous clonus, hyperreflexia, temperature >38.5°C, seizures, rapidly worsening autonomic instability, severe agitation.

General / Acute Presentationsinferred heading

17 conditions
Allergic disorder including food allergy
  • Ix: clinical trigger–symptom timing; allergy-focused history; specific IgE/skinprick if IgE-mediated suspected; avoid broad “allergy panels”.
  • Tx: avoidance plan, non-sedating antihistamine e.g. cetirizine 10 mg OD PRN; prescribe adrenaline auto-injector if anaphylaxis risk.
  • Escalate: allergy clinic if anaphylaxis, asthma + food allergy, multiple foods, unclear trigger, occupational allergy or venom allergy.
  • Mimics: food intolerance, panic, vasovagal episode, chronic urticaria, asthma, mast-cell disorder, scombroid poisoning.
  • Red flags: airway/breathing/circulation symptoms, nut/seafood/venom trigger, asthma, hypotension, biphasic reaction, recurrent unexplained episodes.
Anaphylaxis
  • Ix: clinical ABCDE diagnosis; airway/breathing/circulation compromise after likely allergen; tryptase 1–2 h after onset and baseline later.
  • Tx: IM adrenaline 500 micrograms 1:1000 into anterolateral thigh; repeat every 5 min if needed; lie flat, high-flow O2, IV fluids.
  • Escalate: 999/ED for all; ICU if refractory, airway oedema, hypotension, severe asthma, repeated adrenaline or biphasic risk.
  • Mimics: panic attack, vasovagal syncope, asthma, angioedema without anaphylaxis, PE, sepsis, scombroid poisoning.
  • Red flags: stridor, wheeze, hypoxia, hypotension, collapse, confusion, tongue swelling, severe abdominal symptoms after allergen.
Carbon monoxide poisoning
  • Ix: suspect with headache/dizziness/nausea in shared environment; COHb on blood gas; ECG/troponin if cardiac symptoms; check pregnancy.
  • Tx: remove exposure, 100% high-flow oxygen; treat collapse/seizures/arrhythmia; notify gas emergency services/public health route.
  • Escalate: ED for suspected significant exposure; hyperbaric advice if neuro signs, syncope, cardiac ischaemia, severe acidosis or pregnancy.
  • Mimics: viral illness, migraine, food poisoning, alcohol intoxication, sepsis, flu, depression/fatigue.
  • Red flags: multiple people/pets affected, confusion, syncope, chest pain, pregnancy, metabolic acidosis, focal neurology, coma.
Hyperthermia / hypothermia
  • Ix: core temperature, ABCDE, glucose, ECG, U&E, CK, coag, ABG/VBG; identify sepsis, heat stroke, drugs, exposure, endocrine causes.
  • Tx: heat stroke: rapid active cooling + IV fluids; hypothermia: gentle handling, remove wet clothes, warmed fluids/air, CPR if arrest.
  • Escalate: ICU/ED if temp >40°C, CNS dysfunction, rhabdo, AKI, arrhythmia, temp <32°C, arrest or exposure/trauma.
  • Mimics: sepsis, NMS, serotonin syndrome, thyroid storm, alcohol/drug intoxication, hypoglycaemia, adrenal crisis.
  • Red flags: confusion, seizures, collapse, hot dry or sweating skin, shivering stops, bradyarrhythmia, CK high, coagulopathy.
Hypovolaemia / volume depletion
  • Ix: ABCDE, lying/standing BP, capillary refill, JVP, urine output; U&E, lactate/VBG, FBC, glucose; identify bleed, vomiting, diarrhoea, sepsis.
  • Tx: oral/enteral fluids if mild; IV crystalloid 250–500 mL boluses with reassessment; blood products if haemorrhage.
  • Escalate: admit/ICU if shock, lactate high, AKI, oliguria, GI bleed, sepsis, frailty, severe electrolyte disturbance.
  • Mimics: cardiogenic shock, sepsis, adrenal crisis, DKA/HHS, SIADH, diuretic excess, autonomic failure.
  • Red flags: SBP <90, confusion, anuria, tachycardia, melaena/haematemesis, severe dehydration, hypernatraemia, postural syncope.
Incidental finding
  • Ix: verify source, imaging/report details, symptoms, cancer risk, previous imaging; check if result has formal follow-up pathway.
  • Tx: explain uncertainty; manage according to lesion-specific guidance: adrenal, lung nodule, renal cyst, thyroid nodule, liver lesion, aneurysm.
  • Escalate: urgent pathway if suspicious imaging features, mass with red flags, rapid growth, obstructive symptoms, neurological compromise.
  • Mimics: artefact, benign cyst, anatomical variant, old injury, inflammatory change, overcalled scan finding because radiology enjoys suspense.
  • Red flags: weight loss, haematuria, haemoptysis, jaundice, new neuro signs, large/irregular/solid lesion, lymphadenopathy.
Infertility / subfertility
  • Ix: define after 12 months trying, earlier if age >35 or risk; semen analysis, ovulation progesterone, TFT/prolactin if indicated, chlamydia screen, tubal assessment.
  • Tx: optimise BMI, smoking/alcohol, folic acid, timing intercourse; treat ovulation/STI/endocrine causes; IVF/IUI/ICSI via fertility pathway.
  • Escalate: fertility referral after 1 year, or earlier if female age >35, amenorrhoea, severe endometriosis, known tubal/male-factor issue.
  • Mimics: irregular intercourse timing, erectile/ejaculatory dysfunction, PCOS, endometriosis, premature ovarian insufficiency, thyroid disease.
  • Red flags: amenorrhoea, pelvic mass, galactorrhoea, severe dysmenorrhoea, previous chemo/radiotherapy, azoospermia, recurrent miscarriage.
Malnutrition
  • Ix: MUST score, weight loss %, BMI, intake history; FBC, U&E, Mg/phosphate, LFT, CRP, glucose, B12/folate/vit D; screen swallowing.
  • Tx: food-first, dietitian, oral nutritional supplements; thiamine before refeeding if high risk; correct electrolytes and underlying cause.
  • Escalate: admit/specialist if BMI very low, rapid weight loss, dysphagia, dehydration, refeeding risk, neglect, cancer or eating disorder concern.
  • Mimics: cachexia, depression, dementia, hyperthyroidism, malabsorption, malignancy, substance use.
  • Red flags: BMI <16, negligible intake >10 days, phosphate/K/Mg low, oedema, pressure sores, aspiration, safeguarding concern.
Medically unexplained symptoms
  • Ix: validate symptoms; focused exam and targeted tests only; review prior investigations, medications, mental health, trauma, sleep and function.
  • Tx: continuity with one clinician, planned reviews, functional goals, symptom management, CBT/physio-based rehab if acceptable.
  • Escalate: specialist/urgent review if new objective signs, abnormal tests, red flags, severe disability, self-harm risk or safeguarding concern.
  • Mimics: missed organic disease, depression/anxiety, fibromyalgia, ME/CFS, endocrine disease, inflammatory disease, medication effects.
  • Red flags: weight loss, fever, bleeding, focal neurology, night sweats, abnormal inflammatory markers, suicidal ideation, escalating opioid/benzo use.
Metastatic cancer
  • Ix: symptom-directed imaging: CT chest/abdomen/pelvis, MRI spine/brain if neuro; FBC/U&E/LFT/Ca/ALP, tumour biopsy if primary unknown.
  • Tx: MDT; systemic therapy/radiotherapy/surgery/palliative care; treat emergencies: cord compression, SVCO, hypercalcaemia, sepsis, obstruction.
  • Escalate: same-day if cord compression, brain mets with raised ICP, hypercalcaemia, pathological fracture, neutropenic sepsis, uncontrolled pain.
  • Mimics: infection, inflammatory disease, osteoporosis, myeloma, benign liver/lung lesions, degenerative spine disease.
  • Red flags: weight loss, night pain, new neuro deficit, bone pain, hypercalcaemia, jaundice, ascites, haemoptysis, seizures.
Multi-organ dysfunction syndrome
  • Ix: ABCDE, NEWS2/SOFA trend, lactate, ABG/VBG, FBC/U&E/LFT/coag, cultures, ECG/CXR/source imaging; monitor urine output.
  • Tx: treat cause fast: sepsis, shock, trauma, pancreatitis, burns; oxygen/ventilation, fluids/vasopressors, antibiotics/source control, renal support.
  • Escalate: ICU immediately; involve relevant specialties early because organs do not fail politely one at a time.
  • Mimics: severe dehydration, DKA/HHS, poisoning, adrenal crisis, cardiogenic shock, liver failure.
  • Red flags: rising lactate, AKI, coagulopathy, hypoxia, hypotension, altered mental state, oliguria, thrombocytopenia.
ME / CFS
  • Ix: clinical after ≥3 months symptoms: disabling fatigue, post-exertional malaise, unrefreshing sleep, cognitive symptoms; exclude anaemia, thyroid, inflammatory disease.
  • Tx: personalised energy management/pacing, symptom treatment, sleep/pain support, social/education/work adjustments; do not prescribe fixed-increment graded exercise.
  • Escalate: specialist ME/CFS service if severe, diagnostic uncertainty, children, major functional loss, nutritional compromise or safeguarding concerns.
  • Mimics: depression, sleep apnoea, hypothyroidism, anaemia, autoimmune disease, long COVID, malignancy, medication effects.
  • Red flags: weight loss, fever, night sweats, focal neurology, inflammatory markers high, syncope, chest pain, progressive weakness.
Obesity
  • Ix: BMI plus waist:height ratio, BP, HbA1c, lipids, LFT, U&E/eGFR, OSA screen, mental health, medication and endocrine contributors.
  • Tx: personalised nutrition/activity/behaviour plan; orlistat if criteria met; GLP- 1/GIP agents e.g. semaglutide/tirzepatide via eligibility pathways.
  • Escalate: weight-management specialist/bariatric service if BMI high with comorbidity, failed structured care, complex eating disorder or severe OSA.
  • Mimics: hypothyroidism, Cushing syndrome, PCOS, fluid overload, medication weight gain, binge eating disorder.
  • Red flags: obesity hypoventilation, severe OSA, rapidly rising weight with oedema, diabetes, NAFLD fibrosis risk, hypertension, immobility.
Sarcopenia / frailty
  • Ix: frailty score, gait speed, grip strength, falls history, cognition, nutrition, polypharmacy; FBC/U&E/TFT/B12/vit D if reversible cause suspected.
  • Tx: resistance exercise, protein optimisation, vitamin D if deficient, medication review, falls prevention, MDT comprehensive geriatric assessment.
  • Escalate: frailty team/geriatrics if recurrent falls, delirium, weight loss, carer breakdown, unsafe discharge, advanced care planning need.
  • Mimics: depression, dementia, Parkinsonism, malignancy, inflammatory disease, hypothyroidism, medication sedation.
  • Red flags: recurrent falls, unintentional weight loss, new confusion, pressure sores, immobility, dysphagia, safeguarding concern.
Transplantation
  • Ix: monitor graft function: U&E/eGFR, LFT, drug levels, FBC, infection screen; biopsy/imaging if rejection suspected.
  • Tx: maintain immunosuppression; avoid interacting drugs; infection prophylaxis per transplant plan; rejection treated with steroids/antibody therapy specialistled.
  • Escalate: transplant centre urgently for fever, graft pain, falling urine output, rising creatinine/LFTs, severe diarrhoea/vomiting or missed immunosuppression.
  • Mimics: sepsis, drug toxicity, dehydration, obstruction, recurrent primary disease, opportunistic infection.
  • Red flags: fever, graft tenderness, AKI, jaundice, breathlessness, neutropenia, CMV symptoms, inability to absorb/take immunosuppression.
Vitamin B12 / folate deficiency
  • Ix: FBC/MCV, B12, folate, reticulocytes; intrinsic factor antibodies if pernicious anaemia suspected; check neuro signs before folate.
  • Tx: B12 deficiency with neuro signs: hydroxocobalamin 1 mg IM alternate days until no improvement, then maintenance; folic acid 5 mg OD usually 4 months.
  • Escalate: same-day/admit if severe anaemia with cardiac symptoms, pancytopenia, pregnancy, progressive neuro signs or suspected malignancy.
  • Mimics: MDS, hypothyroidism, alcohol, liver disease, haemolysis, coeliac disease, drug effect, copper deficiency.
  • Red flags: paraesthesia, ataxia, cognitive change, glossitis, pancytopenia, infertility, severe macrocytic anaemia; give B12 before folate if both low.
Vitamin D deficiency
  • Ix: 25-OH vitamin D if symptomatic/high-risk; calcium, phosphate, ALP, PTH, U&E if bone pain, weakness, CKD or suspected osteomalacia.
  • Tx: loading colecalciferol e.g. total 300,000 IU over 6–10 weeks, then maintenance 800–2000 IU/day; add calcium if dietary intake low.
  • Escalate: endocrine/renal if severe hypocalcaemia, seizures/tetany, CKD, malabsorption, recurrent deficiency, unexplained bone pain/fractures.
  • Mimics: osteoporosis, osteomalacia, hyperparathyroidism, myeloma, fibromyalgia, inflammatory myopathy.
  • Red flags: proximal myopathy, bone pain, fragility fracture, low Ca/phosphate, raised ALP, seizures, laryngospasm.

Public Health / Preventioninferred heading

4 conditions
Disease prevention / screening
  • Ix: assess age, sex, pregnancy, ethnicity, FHx, smoking, BMI, BP, QRISK, vaccine status; use UK-nation screening schedules.
  • Tx: primary prevention: vaccines, smoking cessation, alcohol reduction, exercise, diet, weight, BP/lipid/diabetes control, contraception/preconception advice.
  • Escalate: genetic/high-risk clinics for strong cancer/CVD FHx; urgent if screening test positive via relevant pathway, because screening is not “diagnosis cosplay”.
  • Mimics: opportunistic case-finding, diagnostic testing in symptomatic patients, occupational health checks, private screening panels.
  • Red flags: symptoms despite negative screening, missed invitations, high-risk family history, immunosuppression, pregnancy, safeguarding barriers.
Iatrogenic condition
  • Ix: timeline symptoms against drugs, procedures, devices, radiation, surgery; medication reconciliation; FBC/U&E/LFT/coag/ECG/drug levels as relevant.
  • Tx: stop/reverse culprit if safe, treat complication, document clearly, inform patient, incident report/Duty of Candour where applicable.
  • Escalate: senior/specialist review if organ failure, sepsis, bleeding, anaphylaxis, SJS/TEN, surgical complication, device failure or missed diagnosis harm.
  • Mimics: disease progression, new unrelated illness, infection, autoimmune disease, malignancy, non-adherence, interaction rather than single-drug effect.
  • Red flags: rapid deterioration after intervention, wrong-site/wrong-drug/dose error, retained foreign body, AKI, severe rash, shock, death/serious harm.
Non-accidental injury
  • Ix: safeguard first; separate history, developmental plausibility, top-to-toe exam/body map; FBC/coag, skeletal survey/head imaging/ophthalmology per paeds safeguarding pathway.
  • Tx: treat injuries, analgesia, safety plan; do not discharge to unsafe setting; document verbatim history and findings carefully.
  • Escalate: immediate safeguarding lead/social work/paeds/ED; police if immediate danger, sexual assault, serious injury or unsafe removal risk.
  • Mimics: accidental injury, bleeding disorder, osteogenesis imperfecta, EDS, birth injury, cultural practices, dermatological mimics.
  • Red flags: bruising in non-mobile child, patterned injury, burns with clear margins, fractures of different ages, delay seeking help, inconsistent story.
Notifiable diseases
  • Ix: suspect clinically; take relevant samples, exposure/travel/contact history; do not wait for lab confirmation before notification when suspicion is strong.
  • Tx: treat infection, isolate if needed, give PEP/vaccination to contacts where indicated, provide public-health advice.
  • Escalate: notify local Health Protection Team/UKHSA or UK nation equivalent; urgent phone notification for high-risk diseases/outbreaks.
  • Mimics: viral exanthem, flu/COVID, sepsis, meningitis, food poisoning, travel fever, STI syndromes, vaccine reactions.
  • Red flags: measles, meningococcal disease, diphtheria, polio, rabies, viral haemorrhagic fever, food/water outbreak, TB, imported fever, healthcare outbreak.
Source-check used for current UK framing: NHS population screening programmes, UKHSA notifiable disease reporting, NICE child maltreatment guidance, and NICE medicines optimisation/patient-safety incident guidance.
